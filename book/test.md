
## You Cannot Build What You Cannot Describe

Before the playbook. Before the phased roadmap. Before the first pilot. A leader needs a concrete definition of the destination. Not a consultant abstraction. Not a vendor demonstration. A shared, operational picture of what the organisation looks like after transformation.

Chapters 1 and 2 established two preconditions: the scale of the shift and the non negotiable role of leadership. Most leaders now accept both. Acceptance, however, is not the same as clarity. Ask a leadership team to describe the organisation they are building, rather than the tools they are purchasing, and the answer is often vague, technology focused, or borrowed from a sales narrative.

That gap between commitment and clarity is where investment fragments, pilots multiply without coherence, and the micro productivity trap persists. Every subsequent decision, from hiring to governance to technology investment, requires an anchor. This chapter provides it.

The organisation described in this book is built on employees who work in deep partnership with AI. The shift from task executor to orchestrator is not a marginal change in job description. It is a redefinition of what it means to be productive, skilled, and valuable. The structured model that organises the full transformation is introduced in Chapter 5. This chapter establishes the human and conceptual foundation on which that structure depends.

## The Definition Gap

Eight in ten companies now use generative AI. Eight in ten report no significant bottom line impact. That paradox, introduced in Chapter 1, has a specific cause: organisations lack a coherent model of what they are building towards.

The evidence is consistent. The majority of deployment effort is consumed not by the AI itself but by data engineering, stakeholder alignment, governance, and workflow integration. Only a minority of organisations have data that can genuinely support scaled AI use. Very few have deployed autonomous systems at scale, despite widespread interest and intense vendor activity.

Confidence in fully autonomous systems has declined. That decline does not reflect disappointment in technical capability. It reflects a growing recognition that deploying autonomous systems without redesigning how the organisation works around them produces fragile, difficult to govern outcomes.

Meanwhile, adoption accelerates. Executives report increasing budgets and expanding experimentation. Yet many initiatives remain add ons to processes designed for a different era. The underlying operating logic of the organisation remains unchanged. A significant share of these initiatives will fail, not because the technology underperforms, but because success was never clearly defined beyond the pilot.

As ING's Chief Analytics Officer Bahadir Yilmaz observed: "The conversation is no longer about if AI works. It's about understanding where it works and, more importantly, where it matters to the client." The constraint is not technical feasibility. It is disciplined choice.

The gap is organisational. Algorithms represent a small fraction of what is required to generate durable value. Infrastructure is another portion. The majority lies in people and processes. Without a shared understanding of how work is meant to function in the transformed state, investment disperses across disconnected efforts.

The micro productivity trap persists because there is no shared picture of what lies beyond it. More tools do not solve that problem. A clear destination does.

## The Organisation and Its People

The organisation described in this book is one in which humans and AI systems operate as an integrated whole. This chapter makes that definition concrete by focusing on its central element: the people inside it.

May Habib, CEO of Writer, framed the shift at the World Economic Forum in 2025: "The enterprise was designed for a world where execution was the primary constraint. Today, execution is cheap, abundant and instantaneous. Now the constraint is orchestration."

When digital systems can execute at near zero marginal cost, scarcity shifts. The scarce resource is no longer the ability to perform tasks. It is the ability to direct, coordinate, and judge what is produced.

This is where a new kind of employee emerges. The term agentic describes the capacity to achieve outcomes independently. Applied to the workplace, it refers to individuals who use AI to extend their reach while retaining accountability for results. They do not simply use tools. They shape how work is done.

> **Definition: The Agentic Employee**
>
> An agentic employee is a person who works alongside autonomous AI systems as collaborators, directing rather than merely executing.

They set objectives, define boundaries, review outputs, and handle exceptions. Digital systems handle routine and complex execution. The most effective employees create reusable workflows and share them, allowing capability to compound across teams.

In organisations that have progressed, three role patterns are visible:

| Profile | Role | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Supervisor** | Coordinates multiple AI systems across workflows | A project manager overseeing scheduling, reporting, and resource allocation through AI support |
| **Specialist** | Handles exceptions and edge cases beyond automated resolution | A compliance officer reviewing flagged transactions |
| **Augmented Frontline Worker** | Uses AI to remove repetitive tasks and focus on human value | A customer success representative freed from manual data gathering |

These are not theoretical constructs. They describe roles already operating in practice.

Evidence of productivity gains is substantial across software development, legal services, and operational functions. In each case, value emerged not from technology alone but from a redesigned relationship between person and system. Coordination outperforms isolation. Shared workflows outperform individual optimisation.

The required mindset shift is material:

| Previous Assumption | Emerging Assumption |
|---------------------|--------------------|
| "I complete the work" | "I direct how the work is completed" |
| "AI is a tool I occasionally use" | "AI systems are collaborators I work with daily" |
| "My value is my output" | "My value is the outcomes I enable" |
| "I learn to stay relevant" | "I create and share capabilities to multiply impact" |
| "I follow processes" | "I improve and redesign processes" |
| "I wait for instruction" | "I set objectives and review results" |

This transition does not occur through training alone. It requires redesigned roles, new metrics, and incentives that reward coordination and shared capability rather than individual throughput. It is an organisational design challenge.

Two operational principles underpin this model.

First, meaningful oversight must be built into high impact decisions.

> **Definition: Human Oversight**
>
> Human oversight is a workflow design principle in which people intervene at consequential decision points rather than at every step.

Second, capability should be reusable and shareable.

> **Definition: A Reusable Workflow**
>
> A reusable workflow is a repeatable sequence of AI supported actions created by an employee and made available to others.

The deeper technical implications are addressed later in the book. For now, the central point is simple. The organisation is not transformed by deploying software. It is transformed by enabling people to direct, judge, and improve how that software is used.

Chapter 5 introduces the full structural model that connects vision, customer offering, ways of working, and enabling infrastructure. This chapter provides the human foundation that makes that structure meaningful.

Any model for AI adoption ultimately depends on how people work, how responsibilities are shaped, and how capabilities are developed. It also depends on the infrastructure that supports experimentation and learning.

Without individuals who take initiative in working with AI, and without clarity on roles and behavioural expectations, even the most carefully designed structure remains theoretical. A blueprint becomes real only when people understand their part in it and act accordingly.
