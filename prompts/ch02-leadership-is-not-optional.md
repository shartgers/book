# Deep Research Prompt: Chapter 2 — Leadership Is Not Optional

## Purpose of This Prompt

Use this prompt to conduct **extensive external research** on why AI transformation fails when delegated and why CEO, C-suite, and board ownership is non-negotiable. The goal is to gather evidence, case material, and frameworks that can be added to the **research** folder to support writing this chapter.

---

## Chapter Context (What We Already Know)

**Chapter title:** Leadership Is Not Optional  
**Place in book:** Part I — The Burning Platform (with Chapter 1)  
**Target length:** ~8% of book (~2,800 words)  
**Core argument:** Transformation fails when delegated; the roles of CEO, C-suite, and board matter; shared accountability is a prerequisite for success.

### Evidence already in our research

- **95% failure / 5% success:** MIT NANDA 95% failure rate; BCG: only 5% of 1,250+ firms achieve AI value at scale; RAND: AI failure ~80%; S&P Global: 42% scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025.
- **Bain “micro-productivity trap”:** Grassroots experimentation does not self-organise into enterprise-wide impact without clear direction from the top.
- **CEO as predictor:** Organisations with strong executive sponsorship have **6x higher** AI success rates; BCG: C-level deeply engaged are **12x more likely** to be in top 5%; **72%** name CEO as primary AI decision-maker in 2025 (up from ~33%).
- **Trailblazer CEOs (BCG):** ~15% spend 6+ hours/week on AI upskilling, 3x investment, 2x workforce upskilling; half of CEOs believe job stability depends on getting AI right; >50% of non-CEOs say CEO/board should resign if company loses share due to inadequate AI strategy.
- **Mental model shift:** McKinsey: CEO must ask “How do we want decisions made, work to flow, and humans to engage where software can act?” — not “How do we add AI?” Eric Kutcher (McKinsey): “80% business transformation, 20% tech transformation.”
- **Workforce sabotage:** 1 in 3 workers actively sabotage corporate AI rollouts; 41% millennial/Gen Z non-compliance; 75% at or past change saturation — underscoring need for leadership-led narrative and trust.
- **Board gap:** 66% of boards report limited/no AI knowledge; only 15% receive AI metrics; <25% have board-approved AI policies; 88% of organisations use AI in at least one function — governance lag as risk.

### Book thesis

European CEOs are dangerously underprepared for the largest organisational paradigm shift since the industrial revolution. This chapter must make the case that leadership is not optional: delegation to IT or middle management is the default path to the 95% failure outcome.

---

## Main Concepts to Research Deeply (External Focus)

1. **CEO-led vs delegated AI transformation**
   - Comparative studies (success rates, time to value, scale) when CEO owns vs when ownership is delegated to CDO/CTO/COO or “AI committee.”
   - How “CEO as primary decision-maker” has changed over 2023–2025; regional (especially European) breakdowns if available.
   - Examples of transformations that failed or stalled after initial delegation; post-mortems or analyst reports.

2. **C-suite and shared accountability**
   - Models of shared accountability: CEO + COO, CEO + CDO, CEO + board. What does “shared” mean in practice (governance, metrics, incentives)?
   - Tension between central AI/tech ownership (CTO, CDO) and business-unit ownership; how leading companies assign decision rights and budgets.
   - European C-suite structures (e.g. no CDO vs dedicated AI/Data roles) and impact on AI outcomes.

3. **Board role in AI transformation**
   - Board duties: oversight vs strategy vs talent; what “AI governance” means at board level; committee structures (audit, tech, new AI subcommittee).
   - Data on board AI literacy, training, and metrics (beyond the 66% / 15% we have); how boards that “get it” differ in behaviour.
   - EU/corporate governance requirements (e.g. NFRD/CSRD, EU AI Act) that touch board accountability and reporting.

4. **Why delegation fails**
   - Organisational and political reasons (silo ownership, competing priorities, lack of authority to redesign processes).
   - “Zero-based process design” vs “bolt-on AI” — further evidence and case examples.
   - Change management and “change saturation” — link to leadership messaging and consistency.

5. **European-specific leadership context**
   - European CEO surveys or interviews on AI ownership and accountability (who owns AI strategy, who is held accountable).
   - Contrast with US/Asia where data exists; any Europe-specific barriers (e.g. works councils, two-tier boards) and how leaders navigate them.
   - Quotes or positions from European CEOs (BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Klarna, Schneider, others) on why they took personal ownership.

## Suggested Research Questions

- What does “CEO-led” mean operationally? (time allocation, decision rights, board reporting, internal communications.)
- How do companies that moved from “delegated” to “CEO-led” describe the transition and the impact?
- What board metrics and reporting cadences do AI-leading companies use?
- Is there evidence that board AI literacy programmes or dedicated AI committees improve outcomes?
- How do European corporate governance codes or regulations influence who “owns” AI at the top?
- What role do COO, CDO, and CTO play in successful European AI transformations, and how is that coordinated with the CEO?
