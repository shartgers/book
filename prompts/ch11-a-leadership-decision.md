# Deep Research Prompt: Chapter 11 — A Leadership Decision

## Purpose of This Prompt

Use this prompt to conduct **extensive external research** on the concluding themes: the choice every leadership team faces, the cost of waiting, what the Agentic Enterprise looks like in the near future, and who is building it today. The goal is to gather evidence and narrative material for the **research** folder to support writing this short, punchy conclusion.

---

## Chapter Context (What We Already Know)

**Chapter title:** A Leadership Decision  
**Place in book:** Conclusion  
**Target length:** ~6% of book (~2,100 words) — punchy, not long  
**Core argument:** The choice every leadership team faces; the cost of waiting; what the Agentic Enterprise looks like three years from now; who is building it today.

### Evidence already in our research

- **The choice:** CEO-led transformation vs delegation; “how do we want decisions made, work to flow, and humans to engage where software can act?” (McKinsey); 95% failure vs 5% success; first-mover advantage narrowing.
- **Cost of waiting:** Compounding gains of leaders (DBS, Schneider, Allianz); 6× higher success with executive sponsorship; Trailblazer CEOs (15%) pulling ahead; half of CEOs believe job stability depends on getting AI right; >50% non-CEOs say CEO/board should resign if company loses share due to inadequate AI strategy.
- **Three years from now:** McKinsey: by 2027, AI could complete 4 days of work without supervision; BCG: 250% increase in AI decision-making authority in 3 years; “far more AI agents than people” (BCG/OpenAI); org charts as agentic networks; EU AI Act full application 2026 (possibly 2027).
- **Who is building it:** BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Klarna, Schneider (European cases); DBS, Shopify, GitHub, JPMorgan (global); PE and mid-market (EQT, FTI plays). European CEOs who have spoken publicly (Torres Vila, Yilmaz, Karuth-Zelle, Neike, Siemiatkowski, Rambach).
- **Book thesis:** European CEOs are dangerously underprepared; this is the largest organisational paradigm shift since the industrial revolution; leadership is not optional; the Agentic Enterprise is the destination.

### Book thesis

The conclusion must land the “leadership decision” without repeating the whole book: the reader is faced with a clear choice, the cost of waiting is tangible, the future state is vivid, and the builders are identifiable. Tone: direct, memorable, call to action.

## Main Concepts to Research Deeply (External Focus)

1. **The choice (framing and evidence)**
   - **Dual outcome:** Lead the transformation (join the 5%) vs delegate or wait (join the 95%); how other books or thought leaders frame this binary or spectrum.
   - **Decision triggers:** What actually prompts CEOs to “decide” (crisis, board, competitor, regulator, talent); European triggers (EU AI Act, investor pressure, talent).
   - **Quotes:** Strong closing quotes from CEOs or board members on “the choice” or “no choice”; European voices if available.
   - **Avoiding fatalism:** Balance urgency with agency — “it’s not too late” but “the window is narrowing”; evidence on whether laggards can catch up and under what conditions.

2. **Cost of waiting (quantified and narrative)**
   - **Competitive:** Divergence between leaders and laggards (BCG: 2× ROI, 1.5–2× TSR); market share and valuation; sector-specific (banking, insurance, industrial).
   - **Talent:** Loss of AI talent to leaders; employee expectations (Gen Z, millennials); “job stability” (CEO) and “resign if…” (non-CEO) — more data or quotes.
   - **Regulatory:** EU AI Act deadline as forcing function; cost of late compliance (penalties, catch-up, reputation).
   - **Narrative:** “Cost of waiting” as a story (e.g. competitor launched while we piloted; we lost our best data scientists). Case snippets or hypotheticals that resonate with European CEOs.
   - **Reversibility:** What can be caught up later (e.g. some tech) vs what compounds (data, culture, talent, trust).

3. **Three years from now (2027–2028)**
   - **Technology:** Autonomous work (4 days without supervision — McKinsey); agent density (“more agents than people”); MCP and ecosystem maturity; model capability (PhD-level, etc.).
   - **Organisation:** Agentic networks; manager span; operating model; hybrid human–agent as norm; “Minimum Viable Organisations” in some departments.
   - **Market:** Who will be “Agentic Enterprises” by then (sectors, regions); who will have been disrupted; European landscape (winners, laggards, regulators).
   - **Regulation:** EU AI Act in full force; possible new rules or guidance; global convergence or divergence.
   - **Scenarios:** Optimistic, baseline, pessimistic (or “leader vs laggard”) for a typical European company; use to make “three years from now” concrete.

4. **Who is building it today**
   - **European builders:** Updated list and one-line descriptor for each (BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Klarna, Schneider, others); any new European names (2025–2026) with public commitment or results.
   - **Sectors and sizes:** Not just large corporates; mid-market, PE-backed, startups; which European sectors are moving fastest.
   - **Profiles:** “Trailblazer” CEO (BCG); CEO as primary decision-maker (72%); what builders have in common (ownership, time, investment, narrative).
   - **Quotes:** One or two powerful “we are building this” or “this is the future” quotes from European leaders for the closing pages.
   - **Reader identification:** How to help the reader see themselves as a “builder” (next step: playbook Ch 8, diagnostic Ch 9, board Ch 10).

5. **Tone and structure for conclusion**
   - **Call to action:** What exactly the reader should do (e.g. run the diagnostic, convene the board, choose one pilot with clear criteria); avoid vague “embrace AI.”
   - **Book recap in one paragraph:** Thesis + model (Agentic Enterprise, agentic employee, tiers, Value/Capability/Trust) + playbook + European edge — so the conclusion feels like closure, not repetition.
   - **Final line:** Memorable closing (quote, image, or challenge); examples from other business books or speeches that land well.
   - **European note:** End on European advantage (trust, regulation, pragmatism) and the builders in Europe — so the book closes where it started (European CEO guide).

## Suggested Research Questions

- What do CEO surveys (2025–2026) say about “the decision” to prioritise AI and the consequences of not doing so?
- Are there quantified “cost of waiting” studies (e.g. revenue or margin gap between AI leaders and laggards over 3–5 years)?
- What do forecasters (consultancies, think tanks) say the enterprise landscape will look like in 2027–2028 (agentic adoption, winners, losers)?
- Who are the newest or most cited “European AI transformation” leaders (companies or CEOs) in 2025–2026?
- What makes for an effective conclusion in comparable business books (e.g. “Rewired,” transformation books) — structure and tone?
- Is there a striking statistic or quote that captures “the choice” or “the cost of waiting” that we haven’t used yet?
