# Writing Instructions

Must-have instructions and guidelines for writing this book. All agents must follow this file. You may add further ideas or suggestions here; they will be treated as required for drafting and review.

## Named Models

The book introduces several models with **specific names**. Use these exact names and element lists in prose, beat sheets, research briefs, and diagrams. Do not rename or reorder the elements.

### 1. The AI Transformation Framework

**Layers (four), top to bottom** — match images/ai-transformation-framework.png: 
(1) **Vision** — vision, strategy, values, objectives; 
(2) **Products** — by platform or business unit; product vision & objectives, products, personas, customer journeys, frictions; 
(3) **Operating Model** — People: roles, target skills & gaps, org sizing, talent journey, training, team incentives; Process: org design, cross-functional teams, product way-of-working; 
(4) **Foundation** — Data: reusable data products, analytics, architecture, monetization; Technology: target architecture, platforms & solutions, integration; Enablement: culture, AI & engineering toolbox, automation, agents, skills.

Use when describing what the Agentic Organisation is made of or how to assess organisational readiness. Introduced in Chapter 5 (The AI Transformation Framework). Do **not** refer to Value/Capabilities/Trust or to governance, KPIs, financials, or implementation as "framework layers"—they are covered in the book but are not part of the four-layer framework.

**Enablement** is called out explicitly in this framework (within the Foundation layer) and is critical to AI transformation. When writing about the framework or transformation, emphasise that:
- Employees need to understand AI so that change can be driven **bottom-up**, not only top-down.
- Enablement supports people to generate new ideas, adopt an orchestrator / agentic mindset, and contribute as “agentic employees.”
- **Management** must also participate in enablement and model the behaviour; it is not only for frontline staff.

### 2. The Four Tiers of Transformation

**Tiers (four):** Individual → Team → Department (functional) → Organisation (cross-functional).

Use when describing how transformation scales: from one person to teams, then departments, then the whole organisation. Introduced in Chapter 4 (The Four Tiers of Transformation).

### 3. The AI Decision Framework

**Elements (three):** Value, Capabilities, Trust.

Use when describing how to evaluate AI investments and set priorities. Introduced in Chapter 8 (Value, Capabilities, Trust). This is **not** a layer of the AI Transformation Framework; it is a cross-cutting decision lens.

## Token Use

When writing about AI adoption, talent, or organisational policy, include the following points on **token use** where relevant.

**Unlimited token use as a talent perk**  
Companies that allow unlimited (or generous) token use for employees can use this as a differentiator to attract and retain the best talent. Frame it as a perk that signals investment in enablement and in people’s ability to work with AI.

**Consumer token usage projection**  
It is estimated that every consumer will use [X] tokens per day by [date Y]. Research and source the latest industry or analyst estimates (e.g. from API providers, market reports, or adoption studies) and update [X] and [Y] when drafting; cite the source in the book.

**Accenture and promotion eligibility**  
Accenture has included token use (and more broadly, regular adoption of AI tools) in their criteria for promotion eligibility. Employees at certain levels (e.g. L6 and above—senior managers, associate directors, directors) must demonstrate regular use of internal AI platforms (e.g. weekly log-ins to monitored tools such as AI Refinery and SynOps) as a visible input in talent and promotion discussions. Note that exemptions apply in some jurisdictions (e.g. 12 European countries and US federal contracting). Use this as an example of how organisations are tying career progression to AI adoption; research for the most current scope and wording.

## Trust (Value, Capabilities, Trust)

When writing about **Trust** as a core pillar or in the AI Decision Framework, treat it as covering:

- **Compliance** — regulatory, legal, and policy alignment.
- **User trust** — how customers and end-users perceive and rely on AI.
- **Employee trust** — how staff trust AI tools, data use, and the organisation’s AI direction.

This is especially important in **Europe**, where stakeholders tend to be more sceptical of sub-optimal AI. Acknowledge that perfect AI is rare today, but the field is still early; much more can be expected, and the bar for trust will rise as capabilities improve.

## Chapter 1: No Named Framework

Chapter 1 is a momentum chapter. Its job is to build urgency for change — not to give the reader a diagnostic or assessment tool. It has **no named framework**. Do not introduce or imply any Bolt-On / Adapt / Redesign model, Paradigm Shift Diagnostic, or similar assessment lens in Chapter 1. Beat 3 (Framework Introduction) is omitted for this chapter. The chapter uses Beats 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7 only.
