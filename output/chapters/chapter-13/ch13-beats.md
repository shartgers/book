# Chapter 13 — Measuring What Matters

## Strategic Tension

Eighty-eight per cent of organisations use AI in at least one function — yet only 23% actively measure return on investment. The ones that do often measure too early: MIT found that 95% of the companies it studied assessed AI ROI at six months post-pilot, precisely the window in which most value has not yet materialised. The result is a self-fulfilling prophecy — organisations kill projects that would have delivered, then cite the failure as evidence that AI does not work. Meanwhile, boards operate almost blind: 66% of directors report limited or no AI knowledge, only 15% receive AI-related metrics, and fewer than 25% have approved an AI policy. The tension this chapter confronts: measurement is the mechanism that separates transformation from theatre, yet most organisations either do not measure, measure the wrong things, or measure at the wrong time — and the board, which should be the final check, lacks the literacy and the data to intervene.

## Core Principle

The KPIs that prevent pilot purgatory and sustain board confidence are not technical metrics but business-outcome metrics — measured against baselines, gated with upfront success criteria, and governed at the board level with the same rigour as financial performance.

## Named Framework

**The Measure–Gate–Govern Model** — Three stages that connect AI measurement to decision discipline to board accountability. (1) **Measure**: track AI progress across four KPI categories — Financial (ROAI, cost savings, revenue impact, risk avoidance), Operational (cycle time, throughput, error rate, first-pass yield), Adoption (daily active users, workflows redesigned, processes AI-enabled, sentiment), and Strategic (NPS/CSAT, decision quality, innovation velocity) — all measured against pre-established baselines. (2) **Gate**: apply pre-defined success criteria and kill criteria to every pilot, with a maximum six-month decision window to scale, iterate, or kill — eliminating the drift into pilot purgatory. (3) **Govern**: ensure the board receives a structured AI dashboard covering value, risk, and operations — with ownership clarity, escalation speed, and alignment to strategic intent. Visualisable as a three-stage horizontal flow (left to right) with the four KPI categories nested within the Measure stage, and the board dashboard as the output of the Govern stage.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook
Section heading: The Six-Month Trap

Open with the chapter hook: "If you cannot measure it, you cannot defend it to the board. If you cannot defend it to the board, you will not sustain the investment long enough to see results." Then the data that makes this concrete: MIT found 95% of companies measured AI ROI at six months — the exact window when most projects have not yet delivered full value. DBS Bank, by contrast, measured over years with control groups and disclosed S$1 billion in validated AI economic value in 2025 (up 33% year-on-year). JPMorgan's CEO Jamie Dimon — overseeing $2 billion in AI investment used daily by 200,000 employees — calls tech ROI "the hardest thing to measure." The chapter argues that the difficulty is not a reason to avoid measurement but a reason to do it properly. Introduce the Measure–Gate–Govern Model as the structure for the chapter: what to measure, how to decide, and what the board must see.

### Beat 2 — The Problem
Section heading: Pilot Purgatory and the Measurement Crisis

Why most AI programmes stall: 87% of enterprise AI projects never move beyond the pilot stage. The root cause is not technology but organisational failure across five domains — intelligence (wrong use cases), implementation (underestimating production complexity by 250–400%), governance (30–50% of development time lost to compliance bottlenecks), ecosystem (no platform), and capital allocation (hidden costs exceeding projections by 40–60%). Bain's "micro-productivity trap": grassroots experimentation delivers localised efficiency gains that feel like progress but never compound without top-down direction. Only 23% of companies can tie AI initiatives to tangible business outcomes despite 74% ranking AI as a top-three priority. The financial services sector achieves the best pilot-to-production rates (15–20%) because it has clear unit economics, executive sponsorship, and pre-existing compliance infrastructure — not because it has better technology. The measurement crisis and the scaling crisis are the same crisis: without the right metrics, defined upfront, organisations cannot distinguish a pilot worth scaling from one worth killing.

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction
Section heading: The Measure–Gate–Govern Model

Introduce the **Measure–Gate–Govern Model** — three stages that give CEOs a structured approach to AI measurement, decision discipline, and board accountability.

**Measure — The Four KPI Categories.** Every AI initiative should be tracked across four categories, all measured against pre-established baselines:
- *Financial:* ROAI (Return on AI Investment), cost savings, revenue impact, risk avoidance. Formula: ROAI = (Incremental Revenue + Cost Savings + Risk Avoidance − Total Cost) ÷ Total Cost. Use NPV/DCF for multi-year benefits. BBVA: cost-to-income ratio down to 38.8%; fraud incidents down 75% over three years.
- *Operational:* Cycle time, throughput, error rate, first-pass yield, cost per task vs. baseline. Google Cloud's three-pillar framework for agentic AI: reliability (tool selection accuracy, hallucination rate), adoption (workflow integration), and business value. Critical nuance: detect "silent failures" — correct outputs through flawed processes.
- *Adoption:* Daily active users, tasks augmented vs. automated, workflows redesigned, processes AI-enabled as % of total portfolio, employee sentiment, superuser and champion metrics. JPMorgan: 200,000 daily users — but "time saved is often too vague to measure concretely."
- *Strategic:* NPS/CSAT trajectory, decision quality (accuracy, speed, consistency), innovation velocity (time from idea to production). Allianz measures across four balanced dimensions: customer satisfaction, efficiency, effectiveness, and employee satisfaction.

The portfolio view: track the mix of pilots vs. scaled vs. killed. Bain data: software development leads at 40% pilot-to-production; customer service at 32%. A healthy portfolio kills decisively — not reluctantly.

**Gate — Decision Discipline.** Define success criteria and kill criteria before every pilot launches. Kill if: no measurable business-value metrics defined upfront; running >6 months without production commitment; dependent on manual workarounds or individual champions; tests only technical feasibility, not workflow change; cost projections have diverged significantly. Scale if: measurable business value with clear production pathway; demonstrated adoption and process change; can operate without constant human intervention. The success matrix covers four dimensions: business impact (quantified), risk tolerance (regulatory), resource requirements (budget, timeline), and scalability indicators (architecture, org readiness). ING's 90% pilot-to-production rate — three times the industry average — demonstrates what disciplined gating produces.

**Govern — Board-Level Oversight.** What the board should receive: a structured AI dashboard tracking value (cycle time, revenue lift), risk (error rate, bias metrics, compliance incidents, override rates), and operations (incident response time, retraining cadence, model drift). Plus governance indicators: ownership clarity (who owns AI risk at executive level?), decision rights (authority chains), escalation speed (how fast problems reach the board), and alignment to intent (are initiatives staying true to approved purpose?). McKinsey's four board archetypes — Pragmatic Adopters, Functional Reinventors, Internal Transformers, Business Pioneers — each require different metrics and cadence. Companies with AI-savvy boards outperform peers by 10.9 percentage points in ROE.

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications
Section heading: The Board's Evolving Role

The governance gap in numbers: 66% of directors report limited-to-no AI knowledge; only 39% of Fortune 100 companies have formal board-level AI oversight; only 14% discuss AI at every meeting. Yet 2026 is the inflection point where AI governance becomes mandatory, not optional — driven by the EU AI Act (penalties up to 7% of global revenue), investor demands for "AI Governance Maturity" as a valuation factor, and hardening standards (ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF). Two novel fiduciary duties are emerging under the AI Act: "AI due care" (technologically literate oversight of algorithmic systems) and "AI loyalty oversight" (verifying delegated systems remain impartial). Directors need not become coders — but must know which questions to ask about design assumptions, data provenance, and bias. European two-tier boards (Germany, Netherlands, Austria) have a structural advantage: the supervisory board provides natural separation between AI execution and governance. Works councils add a stakeholder dimension through co-determination rights on AI deployment affecting work conditions. The board's job: demand metrics not demos, appoint at least one AI-literate director, invest in board education, and ensure the CEO translates AI complexity into business language the board can govern. NACD: AI literacy must precede AI deployment — boards cannot govern what they do not understand.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions
Section heading: Questions for the Board — Measurement and Governance

1. Do you have pre-established baselines for the processes your AI initiatives target — or are you measuring improvement against assumptions?
2. Can you articulate how many AI pilots are active, how many have been scaled to production, and how many have been deliberately killed in the past twelve months?
3. Does your board receive a structured AI dashboard covering value, risk, and operations — or does it see demos and anecdotes?
4. Are success criteria and kill criteria defined before every AI pilot launches — and who has the authority to enforce them?
5. Who at board level owns AI governance, and can they explain the organisation's exposure under the EU AI Act's high-risk classification?

### Beat 7 — Case Study
Company: DBS
Dilemma: How does a bank prove that $1 billion in AI value is real — not a projection, not a theoretical estimate, but validated, incremental, and attributable? DBS Bank achieved S$1 billion in AI economic value in 2025 (up from S$750 million in 2024) and validated the figure using control-group methodology: comparing outcomes between customers offered AI-driven solutions and those who were not. This is the gold standard of AI attribution — and it is exceptionally rare. Most organisations cannot do this because they never established baselines, never designed for measurement, and never committed to the rigour required to isolate AI's incremental impact from other variables. DBS demonstrates all three stages of the Measure–Gate–Govern Model: **Measure** (financial value tracked across revenue, cost savings, and risk avoidance with control groups), **Gate** (disciplined scaling from pilot to production across consumer and institutional banking), and **Govern** (chief data and transformation officer reporting validated metrics to the board and to external stakeholders). The case illustrates a central argument of this chapter: the measurement problem is a design problem, not a data problem — organisations that design for measurement from the start are the ones that can prove AI works.
