## Framework Evidence

Evidence supporting the three stages of the **Measure–Gate–Govern Model** and its four KPI categories.

### 1. The measurement gap justifies the Measure stage

**Claim:** Only 23% of enterprises actively measure AI ROI despite 78% using AI in at least one function. Organisations with structured ROI measurement achieve 5.2× higher confidence in AI investments. Early pilots reported 31% returns, but scaled ROI collapsed to 7% by 2025 — below most cost-of-capital hurdle rates — partly because hidden costs exceed initial projections by 40–60%.
**Sources:** Second Talent / enterprise survey, 2026; Dima AI, "How to Measure AI ROI: The 2026 Reality Check," 2026.
**Use for:** Beat 1 (Opening Hook) and Beat 3 (Measure stage) — the crisis that makes structured KPIs non-negotiable.

### 2. Pilot purgatory justifies the Gate stage

**Claim:** 87% of enterprise AI projects never move beyond the pilot stage. Five organisational failure domains — intelligence (wrong use cases), implementation (production costs 250–400% above POC), governance (30–50% of dev time lost to compliance bottlenecks), ecosystem (no platform), and capital allocation (hidden costs 40–60% over projections) — drive the failure, not technology. ING's 90% pilot-to-production rate (vs. ~13–15% industry average) demonstrates what disciplined gating produces.
**Sources:** GAI Forum, "The Pilot Purgatory Index," 2025; McKinsey, "Overcoming Two Issues Sinking Gen AI Programs," 2025; Ziptone / Computer Weekly on ING, 2025.
**Use for:** Beat 2 (The Problem) and Beat 3 (Gate stage) — pre-defined kill/scale criteria as the escape from purgatory.

### 3. The board governance gap justifies the Govern stage

**Claim:** 66% of directors report limited-to-no AI knowledge; only 15% of boards receive AI-related metrics; fewer than 25% have approved an AI policy; only 14% discuss AI at every meeting. Yet 88% of organisations use AI. Companies with AI-savvy boards outperform peers by 10.9 percentage points in ROE; those without lag 3.8% below industry average.
**Sources:** McKinsey, "The AI Reckoning: How Boards Can Evolve," 2025; NACD, "2025 Public Company Board Practices & Oversight Survey," 2025.
**Use for:** Beat 4 (Governance Implications) and Beat 3 (Govern stage) — the board dashboard and oversight model.

### 4. The four KPI categories are validated by practitioner evidence

**Claim:** The four KPI categories (Financial, Operational, Adoption, Strategic) align with real-world measurement practice at leading companies:
- **Financial:** DBS validated S$1 billion in AI economic value using control groups; BBVA reduced cost-to-income to 38.8% and fraud incidents 75% over three years. ROAI formula: (Incremental Revenue + Cost Savings + Risk Avoidance − Total Cost) ÷ Total Cost.
- **Operational:** Google Cloud's three-pillar framework for agentic AI (reliability, adoption, business value). BBVA tracks cost per task, cycle time, first-pass yield, throughput. Critical nuance: detect "silent failures" — correct outputs through flawed processes.
- **Adoption:** JPMorgan has 200,000 daily users of its LLM Suite; yet Dimon acknowledges "time saved is often too vague to measure concretely." Key metrics: daily active users, workflows redesigned, processes AI-enabled as % of portfolio, sentiment.
- **Strategic:** Allianz measures across four balanced dimensions: customer satisfaction, efficiency, effectiveness, and employee satisfaction — preventing over-indexing on cost alone.
**Sources:** DBS Bank disclosures, 2025; BBVA operational disclosures, 2025; Google Cloud, "KPIs That Actually Matter for Production AI Agents," 2025–2026; JPMorgan disclosures, 2025–2026; Allianz media centre, 2025.
**Use for:** Beat 3 (Measure stage — the four KPI categories).

### 5. McKinsey's four board archetypes map to differentiated metrics

**Claim:** McKinsey identifies four AI board archetypes — Pragmatic Adopters, Functional Reinventors, Internal Transformers, Business Pioneers — each requiring different governance postures. The recommended board dashboard tracks three dimensions: Value (cycle time, revenue lift), Risk (error rate, bias metrics, compliance incidents, override rates), and Operations (incident response time, retraining cadence, model drift). Governance indicators include ownership clarity, decision rights, escalation speed, and alignment to intent.
**Sources:** McKinsey, "The AI Reckoning," 2025; EC-Council, "Board-Level Metrics for Measuring AI Accountability," 2025.
**Use for:** Beat 3 (Govern stage) and Beat 4 (Board's Evolving Role).

## European Context

### EU AI Act — board accountability and fiduciary duties

The EU AI Act does not formally rewrite directors' duties but sharpens what reasonable digital governance looks like. Penalties: up to **7% of global revenue**. Two novel fiduciary duties are emerging:

1. **AI due care:** Directors must exercise informed, technologically literate, and ethically grounded oversight of algorithmic systems. This requires "cognitive adequacy" — the capacity to question, understand, and monitor technological tools shaping corporate choices.
2. **AI loyalty oversight:** Directors must verify that delegated systems remain impartial and aligned with company objectives. Procedural compliance alone is insufficient.

Implementation is phased: AI practices banned early 2025; general-purpose AI obligations from August 2025; high-risk system requirements across 2026–2027. Directors need not become coders but must know which questions to ask about design assumptions, data provenance, and bias.
**Sources:** Directors' Institute, 2025; Oxford Law Blogs, "Fiduciary Duties and the Business Judgment Rule 2.0 in the AI Act Age," January 2026; The Corporate Governance Institute, 2025.

### Two-tier boards as structural advantage

In two-tier systems (Germany, Netherlands, Austria), the management board (Vorstand) handles AI strategy and implementation while the supervisory board (Aufsichtsrat) provides oversight — a natural separation of execution and governance that can be leveraged for AI accountability. Works councils have co-determination rights on AI deployment affecting work conditions, adding a stakeholder dimension absent in Anglo-American models.
**Sources:** Directors' Institute, 2025; Oxford Law Blogs, 2026; existing book research.

### 2026 as governance inflection point

Three converging forces make 2026 the year AI governance becomes mandatory:
1. **Regulatory enforcement:** EU AI Act penalties; FTC/SEC actions; NIST AI RMF as the de facto negligence standard.
2. **Investor demands:** "AI Governance Maturity" emerging as a valuation and risk factor.
3. **Standards hardening:** ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, and EU AI Act converging into a baseline governance standard.

Corporate governance codes across Europe (UK, German, Dutch) are increasingly interpreted to require digital literacy at board level. NFRD/CSRD reporting may require disclosure of AI's impact on workforce, environment, and governance.
**Sources:** NovaEdge Digital Labs, "AI Governance in 2026," 2026; ReadItQuik, 2026; Directors' Institute, 2025.

## Case Study Data — DBS Bank

### Dilemma
How does a bank prove that S$1 billion in AI value is real — not a projection, not a theoretical estimate, but validated, incremental, and attributable? Most organisations cannot do this because they never established baselines, never designed for measurement, and never committed to the rigour required to isolate AI's incremental impact from other variables.

### Decision
DBS adopted **control-group methodology** — comparing outcomes between customers offered AI-driven solutions and those who were not. This is the gold standard of AI attribution and is exceptionally rare. The bank designed for measurement from the start: baselines established before deployment, attribution methods chosen upfront, and validated results reported to the board and to external stakeholders.

### Outcome and Metrics
- **S$1 billion** in AI economic value in 2025 (up 33% from S$750 million in 2024; up from S$180 million in 2022).
- Value validated through **A/B control-group comparison** — not theoretical estimates.
- Economic value tracked across three dimensions: **revenue enhancement, cost savings, and risk avoidance**.
- Share price tripled; ROE doubled from 8.4% to 18% under CEO Piyush Gupta's transformation.
- Named the **GANDALF framework** — benchmarking against Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, Facebook rather than other banks.
- Only 2 banks globally (DBS and JPMorgan Chase) have publicly disclosed total realised AI value.
- Chief data and transformation officer reports validated metrics to the board and to external stakeholders.

### Connection to the Measure–Gate–Govern Model
DBS demonstrates all three stages:
- **Measure:** Financial value tracked across revenue, cost savings, and risk avoidance with control groups and baselines.
- **Gate:** Disciplined scaling from pilot to production across consumer and institutional banking.
- **Govern:** CDTO reporting validated metrics to the board and disclosing externally — the highest standard of AI governance transparency.

**Sources:** DBS Bank annual disclosures, 2025; Business Times Singapore, 2025; Forrester analysis, 2025; book-research-claude.md Section 7.
