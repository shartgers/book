## Framework Evidence

**1. Vision must precede strategy — the 75%/30% paradox**
Nearly 75% of CEOs globally are now their organisation's main decision-maker on AI strategy (Conference Board / WEF, 2026). Yet fewer than 30% report measurable ROI from AI investments. The gap is not technology — it is the failure to translate vision into measurable strategic objectives. Vision is declared; execution is abandoned. Seventy percent of CEOs are involved in shaping AI strategy, but engagement drops sharply at the translation points: identifying use cases (32%), evaluating technologies (26%), developing tools (18%), reskilling workers (13%). (WEF / BizTech synthesis, 2025)
**Use for:** Opening tension; the gap between "CEO owns vision" and "transformation delivers value"

**2. Vision and strategy are not the same instrument**
The world Economic Forum's 2025 analysis draws a clear functional boundary: "CEOs set the AI vision for the company, while CIOs apply knowledge of every part of the organisation to build the processes that realise the vision." Vision is the direction and why; strategy is the how and where. When vision is absent or delegated entirely to the CIO/CTO, strategy becomes a technology plan rather than a transformation direction. "AI cannot be delegated to IT alone. This is not about systems and servers — it's about strategy, culture, workforce, and risk." (9altitudes, 2025; WEF 2025)
**Use for:** The vision-strategy distinction; why vision must precede strategy; what happens when vision is delegated

**3. Vision must be derived from business strategy, not produced in parallel**
Effective AI leadership involves articulating "a clear AI vision tied to business objectives" and committing resources — budgets, data, and technology — accordingly. When AI vision is produced as a separate workstream from business strategy (typically by a data or technology team), it produces a technology roadmap that is disconnected from the competitive choices, customer ambitions, and investment priorities the business has already made. BizTech Magazine / WEF synthesis (November 2025).
**Use for:** AI vision derived from business strategy; the parallel-workstream failure mode

**4. What CEO-owned vision looks like: DBS Bank**
DBS's transformation is powered by "an early and unwavering strategic vision from leadership, the development of proprietary in-house technology platforms, the cultivation of a deeply embedded data-driven culture, and a robust governance framework that actively enables, rather than hinders, rapid innovation." (Klover.ai, 2025). The GANDALF framework (Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, Facebook) was the vision instrument — not a plan but a reframing of the competitive set entirely. By benchmarking against tech giants rather than other banks, Piyush Gupta gave the organisation a direction that strategy could then specify. The vision: "make banking joyful" by rendering banking invisible and effortless for the customer.
**Use for:** What a CEO-level vision looks like in practice: reframes the competitive set, human-centred, specific enough to direct strategy

**5. Values in the Vision Layer are governance commitments, not aspirational statements**
Board-level AI governance frameworks produce 55% higher ROI on AI investments than organisations without them (governance synthesis, NACD/WilmerHale, 2026). WilmerHale (January 2026): "Fiduciary duty now explicitly extends to AI oversight — the era of 'passive awareness' is over." Boards must verify that management has identified all AI systems, classified them by risk level, and assigned accountability. Values that hold under pressure are demonstrated through scorecards covering explainability, fairness, resilience, auditability, intervention readiness, and evidence of consistent behaviour under stress. DBS's cross-functional Responsible AI Council — senior leaders from legal, risk, and technology — oversees and approves AI use cases, ensuring adherence to both legal requirements and the bank's ethical standards.
**Use for:** Values as governance commitments; board stress-testing the Vision Layer; the Responsible AI Council model

**6. BCG: CEO deeply engaged with AI is 12x more likely to be in the top 5%**
BCG's AI Radar shows C-level executives deeply engaged with AI are 12x more likely to be among the top 5% of companies winning with AI. Organisations with strong executive sponsorship achieve AI success rates 6x higher than those where leadership delegates to IT or middle management. (research/book-research-claude.md Section 2)
**Use for:** The CEO ownership imperative; what "deeply engaged" means relative to "signed off the vision deck"

**7. The McKinsey mental model shift**
CEOs currently frame AI as an IT project, a cost centre, an experiment, or a tool to bolt onto existing processes. The required shift: AI as strategic transformation, operating model redesign, and competitive necessity. "The CEO must ask not 'How do we add AI?' but 'How do we want decisions to be made, work to flow, and humans to engage in an environment where software can act?'" (McKinsey, research/book-research-claude.md Section 1). This is a Vision Layer question — it precedes every strategy and product decision.
**Use for:** The fundamental Vision Layer question; framing the chapter's core claim

## European Context

**EU AI Act as a values governance instrument**
The EU AI Act operates as a values charter embedded in law. Article 4 (in force February 2025) creates a legal mandate for AI literacy — all staff operating or overseeing AI systems must be trained. The full high-risk obligations framework takes effect August 2026, covering risk management systems, technical documentation, human oversight, fundamental rights impact assessments, and conformity assessments. Penalties: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover — exceeding GDPR maximums. (research/book-research-claude.md Section 6; research/ch5 - The AI Transformation Framework.md Section 3)

For European CEOs, the EU AI Act is not a compliance task to delegate to legal — it is a values governance instrument that belongs in the Vision Layer. An organisation that embeds the Act's risk classification (unacceptable, high-risk, limited, minimal) into its AI vision is operationalising values. One that treats it as a compliance project will be slower, less coherent, and more exposed.

**Allianz and the EU AI Pact as competitive signal**
Allianz SE joined the EU AI Pact on 27 November 2024, committing to three actions: adopting an AI governance strategy, mapping high-risk AI systems under the Act, and promoting AI literacy. Over 200 companies have signed the EU AI Pact, including Deutsche Telekom, IBM, Nokia, PwC, Salesforce, and Telecom Italia — with over half pledging additional commitments beyond the three core actions. Proactive compliance builds trust moats. (research/book-research-claude.md Section 5; research/ch5 - The AI Transformation Framework.md Section 3)
**Use for:** EU AI Act as vision-layer instrument; Allianz proactive compliance as competitive advantage framing

**ING's pragmatic Vision Layer**
Bahadir Yilmaz (ING Chief Analytics Officer): "The conversation is no longer about if AI works. It's about understanding where it works and, more importantly, where it matters to the client." ING achieved 90% pilot-to-production rate (vs. industry average 30%), 75% of customer queries automated, 25% productivity gains in operations. ING's vision is not a technology statement — it is a customer and domain value statement. (research/book-research-claude.md Section 7)
**Use for:** European example of CEO-level vision that is business-value anchored rather than technology-anchored

**Board AI literacy is still an acute gap**
66% of European and global boards report limited to no AI knowledge (McKinsey, 2025 — used in Ch 5 in governance context; cite differently here: focus on board's role in stress-testing vision). Only 14% of boards discuss AI at every meeting. MIT CISR 2025: organisations with digitally and AI-savvy boards outperform peers by 10.9 percentage points in return on equity. (research/ch5 - The AI Transformation Framework.md Section 3)
**Use for:** Board's role in stress-testing the Vision Layer; governance gap framing

## Case Study Data — DBS Bank

DBS is the recommended primary case study for this chapter. It has already been used in Ch 2 (CEO-led transformation, S$180M→S$750M economic value, ROE doubled, share price tripled, GANDALF framework) and Ch 14 (measurement angle, S$1B validated AI value, Measure–Gate–Govern Model). **The Vision Layer angle is distinct and unused: how Piyush Gupta built and sustained an AI vision over 10+ years — from the GANDALF reframing of competitive set, through values-based governance (Responsible AI Council), to measurable strategic objectives — and how that vision outlasted the man who set it.**

Memory note: DBS was used in Ch 2 (transformation arc, financials) and Ch 14 (measurement). The Vision Layer angle focuses on the vision instruments themselves — GANDALF as competitive reframing, the "make banking joyful" human-centred purpose, the Responsible AI Council as values governance, and the transition question: does a CEO-set vision survive leadership change?

**Dilemma faced:**
How does a traditional bank in Singapore transform into a technology company — and sustain that direction across a decade, regulatory environments, multiple markets, and eventually a CEO transition — when the industry default is to treat AI as a series of projects rather than a strategic direction?

**Decision made:**
Piyush Gupta set a vision that was not "use more AI" but "compete with tech giants, not banks." GANDALF (Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, Facebook) became the cultural benchmark — "leaving an opening for DBS to become the letter 'D'." The vision was human-centred ("make banking joyful") and translated into a clear governance structure: the Responsible AI Council composed of senior leaders from legal, risk, and technology, overseeing and approving every AI use case. Transformation was initiated around 2014 and built steadily.

**Outcome / results:**
- AI economic value: S$180M (2022) → S$750M (2024), target S$1B (2025)
- 1,500+ AI/ML models deployed across 370+ distinct use cases by early 2025
- Share price tripled; ROE doubled from 8.4% to 18%
- Named World's Best Bank multiple years
- Piyush Gupta stepped down March 28, 2025 after 16-year tenure — the vision he set outlasted him as an institution-level commitment, not a personal project
- DBS cutting approximately 4,000 temporary roles due to AI — a consequence of a vision that was serious, not decorative

**What makes this a Vision Layer case (not a general transformation case):**
The chapter needs to illustrate three Vision Layer elements: (1) vision derived from business strategy (compete with tech giants, not banks); (2) human-centred vision that directs AI strategy (make banking joyful → AI renders banking invisible); (3) values as governance instruments (Responsible AI Council). DBS illustrates all three. The Klarna case (Ch 3) showed the hybrid human-AI model; the ING case (Ch 4) showed tiers of transformation; DBS here shows what a sustained, governed, CEO-owned vision looks like over time.
