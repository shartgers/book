# Chapter 02 — Research Brief

**Chapter:** Leadership Is Not Optional
**Word budget:** 8% of book (~2,800 words at 35k midpoint)
**Audience:** European CEOs of mid-to-large organisations — sceptical, data-driven, time-poor, peer-level advisee
**Chapter role:** The accountability argument. Why transformation fails when delegated. What personal ownership actually requires.

---

## Chapter Purpose

To make an irrefutable, evidence-based case that the single strongest predictor of AI transformation success is CEO-level ownership — and to define, through the Leadership Ownership Model and the DBS Bank case study, what that ownership looks like in practice.

---

## Key Statistics and Evidence

### Leadership Impact Data

- **CEO-led transformations succeed at 6x the rate** of those delegated to IT or middle management. (Source: research synthesis, Section 2)
- **C-level executives deeply engaged with AI are 12x more likely** to be among the top 5% of companies winning with AI. (Source: BCG AI Radar, referenced Section 2)
- **72% of respondents in 2025 identify the CEO as the primary decision-maker on AI** — up sharply from one-third the year before. This shift in expectation is itself data: the market has concluded that delegation fails. (Source: Section 2)
- **15% of CEOs are "Trailblazers"**: they spend 6+ hours weekly upskilling on AI, commit 3x the investment of their peers, and invest 2x in workforce upskilling. Half of all CEOs now believe their job stability depends on getting AI right. (Source: BCG, referenced Section 2)
- **More than half of non-CEOs** believe the CEO or board should resign if the company loses market share due to inadequate AI strategy. (Source: Section 2) — Use carefully: powerful signal of stakeholder expectation shift.
- BCG's top 5% of AI leaders ("future-built companies") achieve **1.7x revenue growth, 3.6x three-year total shareholder return (TSR), and 1.6x EBIT margin** versus laggards. They plan to spend more than twice as much on AI in 2025 compared to those who are falling behind. (Source: BCG, "The Widening AI Value Gap," September 2025 — cross-referenced from Chapter 01 web research)

### Failure Mode Analysis

- **MIT NANDA Report (2025):** 95% of enterprise generative AI implementations fail to deliver sustained, measurable impact. This is the headline failure statistic — shared with Chapter 01 but used here with a different emphasis: the failure is not technological, it is governance.
- **BCG:** Only 5% of 1,250+ firms achieve AI value at scale. 60% achieve no material value at all. (Source: BCG survey, Section 2)
- **RAND Corporation:** AI project failure rate exceeds 80% — more than double the failure rate for non-AI IT projects. The doubling of failure relative to standard IT projects is the most damning signal that AI is being managed as an IT initiative when it is not one. (Source: RAND, Section 2)
- **S&P Global (2025):** 42% of companies scrapped most of their AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the year before. Year-on-year near-tripling of cancellation rate signals the "pilot purgatory" crisis is accelerating. (Source: S&P Global, Section 2)
- **Bain & Company — "micro-productivity trap":** A proliferation of proofs of concept and isolated use cases delivering modest, localised efficiency gains that fail to scale. The trap is not inaction — it is the wrong kind of action without leadership direction. (Source: Bain, Section 2)
- **Human resistance dimension — the silent sabotage problem:**
  - 1 in 3 workers actively sabotage corporate AI rollouts. (Source: Section 2)
  - 41% of millennial and Gen Z employees engage in non-compliance with AI mandates. (Source: Section 2)
  - 75% of organisations are at or past the change saturation point. (Source: Section 2)
  - Writer's note: These numbers establish that delegation to HR or change management is also insufficient — resistance at this scale requires visible CEO accountability, not programme management.
- **McKinsey "Gen AI Paradox":** Nearly 8 in 10 companies report using gen AI, yet just as many report no significant bottom-line impact. Approximately 90% of transformative use cases remain stuck in pilot mode. (Source: McKinsey June 2025 CEO Playbook, Section 1) — Cross-chapter link; use in Chapter 02 to frame why leadership posture, not tool adoption, is the variable.

### Board Governance Gap

The board data is a distinct layer of the accountability argument: even where CEOs are engaged, board-level governance is largely absent — creating a structural accountability vacuum above the CEO.

- **66% of boards report limited, minimal, or no AI knowledge or experience.** (Source: Board survey, Section 9)
- **Only 15% of boards receive AI-related metrics.** (Source: Section 9) — The board cannot govern what it does not measure.
- **Fewer than 25% of boards have board-approved AI policies.** (Source: Section 9)
- **Only 14% of boards discuss AI at every board meeting.** (Source: Section 9)
- **88% of organisations already use AI in at least one business function.** (Source: Section 9) — The gap between 88% adoption and 14% board discussion is the governance chasm.
- **McKinsey board prescription:** Boards should determine their "AI posture," codify governance frameworks, and demand specific metrics including: ROI by business unit, percentage of processes AI-enabled, workforce reskilling progress, regulatory alignment status, and override rates. (Source: McKinsey, Section 9)

### The Named Framework: Leadership Ownership Model

The framework is implicit in the research but must be made explicit in the chapter. The three levels are:

**Delegate** — The CEO assigns AI to IT or a middle-management function. The org runs pilots. Nothing scales. This describes 60–95% of current enterprise AI programmes depending on the metric used.

**Sponsor** — The CEO publicly champions AI, allocates budget, attends reviews. Better results, but transformation remains downstream. The CEO is an enabler, not the driver.

**Own** — The CEO personally upskills, sets direction, ties accountability to outcomes, models the behaviour change. This is the posture of BCG's "Trailblazers" (6+ hours weekly, 3x investment) and the CEO profile behind DBS Bank's transformation. The research shows this is also the posture of the 5% achieving scale.

Writer's note: The movement from Delegate → Sponsor → Own is the core diagnostic tool of this chapter. European CEOs will recognise their current position in the model. The chapter's job is to make staying at Delegate or Sponsor feel untenable, and to show what Own looks like through DBS.

## Case Study Data: DBS Bank

**Selected as primary case study because:** DBS is the gold-standard example of CEO-led transformation producing measurable, publicly disclosed financial results — and because it demonstrates that this model works outside the US, which is directly relevant to European CEOs who may assume these results only apply to Silicon Valley-native organisations.

### Context

- DBS Bank, Singapore. One of the largest banks in Southeast Asia by assets.
- Pre-transformation reputation: nicknamed "Damn Bloody Slow" — a legacy institution with bureaucratic culture and slow digital capability.
- Transformation led personally by CEO Piyush Gupta.

### CEO Leadership Posture

- Gupta set the strategic direction and created the GANDALF framework — an acronym benchmarking DBS not against peer banks but against Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, and Facebook.
- The signal: by choosing technology companies as the benchmark, Gupta reframed DBS's competitive identity. The bank was not competing with HSBC; it was competing with the organisations that were reinventing financial services.
- This is "Own" posture: the CEO defining the ambition, not delegating the ambition-setting to strategy teams.

### Key Metrics (verifiable and publicly disclosed)

| Metric | Data point |
|--------|------------|
| AI economic value (2022) | S$180 million |
| AI economic value (2024) | S$750 million |
| AI economic value (projected 2025) | S$1 billion |
| Share price trajectory | Tripled during transformation period |
| Return on Equity (pre-transformation) | 8.4% |
| Return on Equity (post-transformation) | 18% |
| Global disclosure status | One of only 2 banks (alongside JPMorgan Chase) to publicly disclose total realised AI value |

### Structural Signals

- DBS is one of only 2 banks globally to have publicly disclosed total realised AI value. The willingness to disclose is itself an accountability signal — consistent with CEO-led ownership rather than delegated experimentation.
- The "World's Best Bank" recognition (multiple rankings) followed the transformation, not preceded it. The external validation is the outcome, not the input.

### Dilemma for Case Study Agent

The tension to surface: Gupta was leading a regulated Asian bank with legacy systems, cultural resistance to change, and no particular technology heritage. He had every structural reason to delegate transformation to a Chief Digital Officer and manage by exception. He did not. The dilemma is not "whether to transform" but "how deeply must the CEO personally own it?" — the answer DBS provides is instructive.

### Transferable Lessons (for Writer)

1. The benchmark matters: Choosing Google/Amazon/Netflix as comparators rather than peer banks changed what "good" looked like internally. European CEOs can apply this: benchmark against the most digitally capable organisations in adjacent sectors, not against sector peers who are equally behind.
2. Financial disclosure is a commitment mechanism: Publishing S$180M → S$750M creates accountability that internal targets do not. Consider what equivalent public commitments European CEOs might make.
3. The GANDALF framework is a communication tool: It gave every employee a concrete picture of the destination. European CEOs need an equivalent language for their organisations.

## Research Gaps

The following areas have thin or uncertain evidence and should be handled with appropriate care in the draft:

1. **The 6x success rate figure** — presented in the research synthesis (Section 2) but without a named primary source. Writer should treat as "the research shows" rather than attributing to a specific study until source is confirmed. The Perplexity Gate pass (Wave 3) should verify.

2. **The 12x figure (BCG AI Radar)** — similarly, the BCG AI Radar is the cited source but the exact study name, date, and sample size are not specified in the research. Use with BCG attribution but flag for Perplexity Gate verification.

3. **The "more than half of non-CEOs would support board/CEO resignation" figure** — emotionally powerful but source is unspecified in the synthesis. Use with caution; may need softening to "research suggests a majority of..." unless the Perplexity Gate locates the source.

4. **Piyush Gupta direct quotes** — none are available in the research files. The DBS transformation is well documented in public sources. Case Study Agent should search for direct Gupta quotes on CEO leadership posture, the GANDALF framework rationale, or the decision to benchmark against tech companies rather than peer banks. A direct quote from the protagonist would strengthen the case study significantly.

5. **European CEO-specific leadership data** — the failure rate data, the 6x and 12x figures, and the Trailblazer CEO profile data are global, not Europe-specific. No European-specific equivalent has been located. The chapter can still use this data (it applies to European CEOs) but should not claim European specificity for global statistics.

6. **Board AI governance improvement actions** — the research documents the gap (66% limited knowledge, 14% regular discussion) but has limited data on what boards that have acted have done, or what results followed. McKinsey's framework prescriptions are available but empirical outcomes from improved board AI governance are thin.
