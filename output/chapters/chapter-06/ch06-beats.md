# Chapter 06 — The Operational Layer

## Strategic Tension

Seventy-eight per cent of C-suite executives say agentic AI requires a new operating model. Yet BCG's 10/20/70 rule shows that 70% of AI transformation effort must go into people and processes — and most organisations invest backwards, pouring resources into technology and algorithms while leaving the operating model untouched. The result: successful pilots that never scale. The tension is between the wide recognition that the operating model must change and the persistent failure to actually change it — because redesigning roles, skills, and processes is harder, slower, and more politically charged than deploying technology. This chapter makes the Operating Model layer of the AI Transformation Framework explicit, practical, and unavoidable.

## Core Principle

The Operating Model layer — roles, skills, process, and org design — is the 70% of AI transformation that most organisations neglect, and the primary reason successful pilots fail to scale into enterprise-wide impact.

## Named Framework

**The Agentic Operating Model** — A deep-dive into the third layer of the AI Transformation Framework (introduced in Ch 5), structured around its two dimensions: **People** (three emerging roles — Domain Leader, Agent Orchestrator, AI Product Manager — plus the skills shift from task-doer to orchestrator) and **Process** (radical workflow redesign around agents as primary actors, plus three org design patterns — Centralised, Hub-and-Spoke, Embedded — that define how the operating model scales). ### Beat 2 — The Problem

Section heading: Why Pilots Succeed but Transformation Stalls

Why the operating model is the bottleneck. Harvard Data Science Review: despite 78% of companies claiming AI adoption, 80% report no measurable impact on earnings — because AI is layered onto workflows designed for humans, not for human-agent collaboration. Bain's "micro-productivity trap": grassroots AI experimentation sparks innovation but does not self-organise into enterprise-wide impact. BCG: successful scaling requires end-to-end process redesign around agents as default executors, not automation of discrete steps within existing workflows. Synvestable: nearly two-thirds of AI initiatives fail to advance beyond pilots — specifically because organisations redesign technology without redesigning workflows. The operating model is what bridges a successful pilot to scaled impact: who does the work, how the process runs, and how the organisation is structured must all change — not just the technology. Frame around the 10/20/70 insight: when you invest in the 10% and 20% but neglect the 70%, you are structurally guaranteed to stall.

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction

Section heading: The Agentic Operating Model

Introduce the **Agentic Operating Model** — the Operating Model layer of the AI Transformation Framework (reference Ch 5 and the framework image), structured around its two dimensions from the framework: **People** and **Process**.

**People — Three New Roles:**

1. **Domain Leader (N-2/N-3)** — McKinsey: "probably the single most critical role any business needs for its AI transformations." Translates strategy into operational AI change. Currently, only 17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders' skills are technical; just 5% have held technical roles. This creates a massive capability gap at the exact level where strategy becomes execution.

2. **Agent Orchestrator** — The new middle-management function: manages synthetic AI talent (agent provisioning, monitoring, coordination) rather than just people. The role bridges the "Service as a Software" paradigm shift. BCG: agent orchestration is the "central nervous system" of agent-first organisations — the capability that separates organisations that deploy agents from those that scale them.

3. **AI Product Manager** — Owns the roadmap for AI agent capabilities, governs agent lifecycle, designs multi-step reasoning workflows, and establishes performance metrics. By 2026, 85% of companies expect to customise autonomous agents for specific business needs, but only 1 in 5 have mature governance models.

Connect to Ch 3: these roles map onto the three agentic employee profiles (supervisor, specialist, augmented frontline). The domain leader is the executive-tier version; the Agent Orchestrator is the team-tier version; the AI Product Manager governs the product interface.

**People — The Skills Shift:**

Over 70% of employer-sought skills will endure but be applied differently in partnership with AI. The shift is from producing first drafts to framing questions, validating outputs, and applying judgment. AI fluency demand has grown nearly sevenfold in two years, spreading beyond technical roles into management, finance, healthcare, and frontline services. Key complementary skills: critical thinking, adaptability, quality assurance, coaching, decision-making. Only 6% of companies have begun upskilling in a "meaningful way" despite 89% acknowledging the need. The most valuable agentic employees create reusable AI skills and share them — producing compounding organisational value (connect to Ch 3 and Ch 4). Europe-specific: up to 12 million occupational transitions by 2030 — double the pre-pandemic pace (McKinsey Global Institute).

**Process — Radical Workflow Redesign:**

Organisations must adopt "zero-based, outcome-driven processes" — redesigning from scratch with AI at the core rather than automating legacy workflows. BCG: 30–50% business process acceleration, 25–40% reduction in low-value work time, 40–60% cuts in engineering and design lead times — but only when processes are redesigned end-to-end. An industrial goods company redesigned its quote-to-order process using four coordinated AI agents, achieving 30–40% labour cost reductions and tens of millions in additional revenue. Connect to multi-agent orchestration patterns (Hierarchical/Manager-Worker, Supervisor, Pipeline, Swarm) — the process design determines which pattern fits. The Supervisor pattern dominates enterprise deployments because it mirrors existing human team structures.

**Process — Org Design:**

Three primary org design patterns are emerging: (1) **Centralised** — for low AI maturity, scarce talent, high regulatory exposure; standardisation and learning speed, but bottleneck risk. (2) **Hub-and-Spoke (Federated)** — for diverse business contexts, speed; ownership and business relevance, but fragmentation risk without guardrails. (3) **Hybrid** — combining centralised standards with embedded product teams; balance of control and agility, but governance complexity. The typical progression: centralise first (build standards, governance, talent pool), then decentralise delivery as maturity grows. Chief AI Officer reporting directly to CEO/COO with real decision authority; lean AI governance council; embedded product teams in business units owning outcomes. Connect to CoE progression (Ch 11) and readiness (Ch 12).

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications

Section heading: The Operating Model as Leadership Accountability

The operating model is a CEO/board responsibility, not a delegation target. Barbara Karuth-Zelle (COO, Allianz): "Digital and AI transformations are not mainly about the technology but rather the mindset, the people, and the organization." Only 21% of organisations have rigorous AI governance (Deloitte). The challenge of change management: BCG finds that organisations integrating change management from the planning stage — not as an afterthought — are significantly more likely to achieve lasting AI impact. The 10/20/70 rule's 70% must begin at the beginning, not when technology is "ready." For European organisations: works councils and collective bargaining — only 20% of EU trade unions currently have collective bargaining agreements addressing AI, though 42% are in social dialogue. Workers' exposure to algorithmic management could rise from 42.3% to 55.5%. The European CEO must lead the operating model shift while navigating social dialogue requirements that are unique to Europe. This is a strength (trust, legitimacy, sustainability) not a weakness — but only if the CEO leads it rather than deferring it. Connect to Ch 9 (Europe's Distinctive Edge) and Ch 12 (Readiness). Span of control is also changing: McKinsey envisions one manager with a small human team orchestrating hundreds of agents acting autonomously across workflows, 24/7 — traditional hierarchies give way to flexible configurations of human-agent teams.

### Beat 5 — Closing / Transition

Section heading: From Structure to Foundation

The Operating Model layer is the 70% — and now the CEO reader has a framework for redesigning it: new roles, new skills, and new processes built for human-agent collaboration. But the operating model does not stand alone. It depends on a foundation: data quality, technology architecture, and enablement. The Operating Model and Foundation layers are tightly coupled — process redesign requires data readiness (63% of organisations lack adequate data management for AI); skills shift requires tooling and enablement infrastructure; org design requires platforms that enable collaboration. Teams succeed when they spend at least 50% of project timeline on data preparation before model development — an operating model question, not just a technology one. Chapter 7 dives into the Foundation layer: data, technology, and enablement — the final layer of the AI Transformation Framework and the base upon which the operating model is built.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions

Section heading: Questions for the Board — The Operating Model

1. Does your current operating model — roles, skills, team structures, and processes — reflect the way AI-augmented work actually gets done, or does it mirror the pre-AI organisation with AI tools layered on top?
2. Who in your organisation fills the Domain Leader role: a senior executive (N-2/N-3) who combines business judgment with AI fluency to translate strategy into operational AI change? If no one does, what is your plan to build or hire for that capability?
3. How are you investing the "70%" — people and processes — compared to the 30% for technology and algorithms? Is the ratio deliberate, or has technology absorbed most of the budget?
4. Which of the three org design models (centralised, hub-and-spoke, hybrid) describes your current AI setup — and which should it evolve toward as maturity grows?
5. Are your processes redesigned end-to-end for human-agent collaboration, or are agents layered onto legacy workflows? What would a zero-based redesign of your most important process look like?
