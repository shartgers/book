# Chapter 08 — Value, Capabilities, Trust

## Strategic Tension

Organisations that have built their foundations — data, technology, enablement — still struggle with the same question: where should we invest next? The tension is between the overwhelming number of possible AI initiatives and the finite resources (budget, talent, governance capacity) to pursue them. Without a decision lens, companies either chase the highest-profile use case regardless of readiness, or stall in analysis paralysis. The result is the same: scattered pilots, wasted investment, and a board that cannot explain why AI spending is not translating into value. European CEOs need a repeatable filter — not for what AI can do, but for what the organisation should do, in what order, and with what safeguards. That filter is the AI Decision Framework: Value, Capabilities, Trust.

## Core Principle

Every AI initiative must be evaluated through three lenses — Value (where does it create measurable business value?), Capabilities (can we actually deliver it?), and Trust (can we govern it responsibly?) — and the interplay between these three determines both priority and sequencing.

## Named Framework

**The AI Decision Framework** — Three elements: (1) **Value** — where does this initiative create business value: efficiency, revenue, risk reduction, or strategic capability? Mapped to Deploy (off-the-shelf, quick wins), Reshape (end-to-end function redesign), or Invent (new business models). (2) **Capabilities** — does the organisation have the people, data, process, and technology to deliver? Assessed across roles, skills readiness, data maturity, and operating model. (3) **Trust** — can the organisation govern this responsibly: regulatory compliance, customer trust, employee trust, board oversight? Visualisable as a triangle or three-column scorecard where every initiative is rated on all three dimensions before investment decisions are made. The framework is a cross-cutting decision lens, not a layer of the AI Transformation Framework.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook
Section heading: The Thirty-One to Seven Problem

Open with the data point that stops CEOs in their tracks: early AI pilots showed 31% returns in 2023, but as projects scaled, ROI dropped to just 7% by 2025 — below most companies' cost of capital. Meanwhile, 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to deliver sustained P&L impact (MIT, August 2025). The root cause is not technology failure — it is a decision failure. Organisations pick the wrong initiatives, fund them without capability readiness, and govern them as afterthoughts. The chapter introduces a three-angle lens — Value, Capabilities, Trust — that European CEOs can apply to every AI investment decision. This is not a framework layer (those were covered in Ch 5–7); it is the filter that sits across the entire framework and determines where to play, how to win, and what to protect.

### Beat 2 — The Problem
Section heading: Why Most AI Investments Disappoint

Expand on why the pilot-to-scale gap exists. BCG: only 5% of 1,250+ firms achieve AI value at scale; 60% achieve no material value at all. The "micro-productivity trap" (Bain): proliferation of proofs of concept delivering modest gains that fail to scale. McKinsey: 88% use AI in at least one function, but only 39% report enterprise-level EBIT impact; the single largest driver of EBIT impact is workflow redesign, yet only 21% have done it. MIT study: over 50% of AI budgets go to sales and marketing, yet strongest ROI appears in back-office automation — a misallocation problem. The common thread: organisations lack a structured way to evaluate initiatives across value, capability readiness, and governance risk simultaneously. They optimise for one angle (usually value) and ignore the other two. The AI Decision Framework corrects this.

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction
Section heading: The AI Decision Framework

Introduce the **AI Decision Framework** with its three elements — Value, Capabilities, Trust. This is a cross-cutting decision lens, not part of the four-layer AI Transformation Framework (Ch 5). Every AI initiative, from a quick-win chatbot to an end-to-end process redesign, should be scored on all three dimensions before investment is approved.

**Value** — Where does this initiative create business value? BCG's Deploy-Reshape-Invent (DRI) taxonomy provides the value map: Deploy (off-the-shelf tools, 10–15% productivity); Reshape (end-to-end AI workflows in core functions); Invent (new business models and revenue). AI-mature companies generate 72% of their value in core functions (BCG). The four layers of ROI: efficiency, revenue/margin, risk/compliance, and strategic capability — each matters at different maturity stages. Value must be measured against baselines, not aspirations; and multi-year, not six-month windows.

**Capabilities** — Can the organisation deliver? BCG's 10/20/70 rule: 70% of the effort is people and process, not algorithms or technology. Key gaps: only 6% of companies upskill meaningfully (BCG) despite 89% acknowledging the need; only 17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders have technical skills (McKinsey). The critical "domain leader" (N-2/N-3) translates vision into operational change — "probably the single most critical role" (McKinsey). New roles: Agent Orchestrator, AI Product Manager, AI Ops. Capability assessment covers data readiness, talent, process maturity, and technology infrastructure.

**Trust** — Can the organisation govern this responsibly? Trust is not just compliance; it encompasses regulatory alignment (EU AI Act, GDPR), customer trust (transparency, explainability), and employee trust (data use, job impact, organisational direction). Gartner: in 57% of high-maturity organisations, business units trust and use AI vs. only 14% in low-maturity — trust is a leading indicator of adoption and value. Accenture: 77% of executives say trust is the foundation for unlocking AI's benefits; yet 74% of companies have paused AI projects due to risk concerns. Trust frameworks (IBM, Microsoft, Deloitte, NIST) converge on similar principles; the EU AI Act operationalises them for Europe.

Include a definition block for the AI Decision Framework. Emphasise it is visualisable as a triangle or three-column scorecard. Note that the three angles interact: high-value but low-capability initiatives need sequencing; high-value but high-risk initiatives need trust-first investment; quick wins build momentum that funds capability and trust building.

This is the most substantial beat and should receive the most words.

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications
Section heading: From Scorecard to Boardroom

The AI Decision Framework is not just an operational tool — it is a governance instrument. The board should demand that every AI initiative is presented with its Value, Capabilities, and Trust scores before approval. Current governance gap: 66% of boards report limited-to-no AI knowledge; only 15% receive AI-related metrics; nearly 50% say AI is not yet on the board agenda (Deloitte). The framework gives the board a structured reporting mechanism: a portfolio view of all AI initiatives scored on three dimensions, with clear escalation criteria (e.g. high-risk initiatives requiring trust review before proceeding). For European companies, trust governance is not optional: EU AI Act penalties reach €35 million or 7% of global turnover. But proactive compliance is competitive advantage — over 230 organisations have signed the EU AI Pact, with 63% EU-based; more than 50% have gone beyond mandatory requirements. The board's job: ensure that trust is not treated as a cost centre but as an enabler of scale and adoption.

### Beat 5 — Closing / Transition
Section heading: Trust as the European Edge

Close by connecting Value, Capabilities, and Trust to the European context that Chapter 9 will explore. For European organisations, the Trust angle is not a constraint — it is a differentiator. The EU AI Act, GDPR, and cultural expectations around data privacy create a "trust moat" that, when navigated well, accelerates adoption rather than slowing it. Accenture: 91% of European business leaders expect revenue growth in 2026 amid rising AI investment; the EESC positions trustworthy AI as Europe's competitive advantage. The organisations that master all three angles — creating real value, building the right capabilities, and earning trust from regulators, customers, and employees — are the ones that will lead the next decade. Chapter 9 picks up this thread and makes the full case for why Europe's distinctive regulatory and cultural context is an advantage, not a handicap.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions
Section heading: Questions for the Board — Evaluating AI Investments

1. When your team proposes an AI initiative, are they required to present its expected value, capability readiness, and trust/governance implications — or only one of the three?
2. Can you articulate whether your current AI portfolio is weighted toward Deploy (quick wins), Reshape (function redesign), or Invent (new models) — and is that balance intentional?
3. Do you know which capability gaps — people, data, process, or technology — are most likely to prevent your highest-value AI initiatives from scaling?
4. Has your organisation established trust governance (regulatory compliance, explainability, employee engagement) as a prerequisite for scaling AI, or is it treated as an afterthought?
5. If your board reviewed every active AI initiative through the lens of Value, Capabilities, and Trust today, how many would pass all three?

### Beat 7 — Case Study
Company: Allianz
Dilemma: How does Europe's largest insurer turn regulatory compliance into a competitive weapon? Allianz — #2 globally in the Evident AI Insurance Index, employing 10% of the entire AI workforce across 30 major insurers — demonstrates all three angles of the AI Decision Framework in action. **Value:** Close to 400 GenAI use cases; Project Nemo reduces claim processing time by 80% with a 7-agent workflow executing in under 5 minutes. **Capabilities:** A dedicated AI talent pool at scale; COO Barbara Karuth-Zelle's insistence that "digital and AI transformations are not mainly about the technology but rather the mindset, the people, and the organisation." **Trust:** Among the first signatories of the EU AI Pact (November 2024); partnership with Anthropic to deploy auditable, governance-focused AI agents across insurance operations — setting a benchmark for how regulated industries build trust moats through proactive compliance rather than minimum adherence. The Allianz case shows that the three angles are not sequential — they compound. Value funds capability investment; capability enables trust at scale; trust unlocks adoption that generates more value.

## Thesis Check

This chapter advances the central argument by giving European CEOs the decision lens they lack: a structured, repeatable filter for evaluating every AI initiative across value, capability, and trust. Without it, the foundations built in earlier chapters (leadership, vision, framework, operating model, technology) cannot be translated into prioritised, governed, scalable investment — and the 95% failure rate persists.
