# Research Brief — Chapter 08 — Value, Capabilities, Trust

## Framework Evidence

1. **Pilot-to-scale ROI collapse proves the need for a decision lens.** Early AI pilots showed 31% returns in 2023, but as projects scaled, ROI dropped to only 7% by 2025 — below most companies' cost of capital. Meanwhile 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to deliver sustained P&L impact. The root cause is not technology failure but a decision failure: organisations pick the wrong initiatives, fund them without capability readiness, and govern them as afterthoughts. — DIMA AI, 2026; MIT Sloan "GenAI Divide," August 2025 (150 interviews, 350-employee survey, 300 public deployments). Stat: "95% of enterprises report zero ROI from generative AI investments, despite $30–40 billion in global spending" — deepsense.ai, 2025.

2. **BCG's Deploy-Reshape-Invent (DRI) taxonomy maps the Value dimension.** Deploy: off-the-shelf tools for 10–15% productivity boost. Reshape: end-to-end function redesign with AI workflows. Invent: new business models and revenue streams. AI-mature companies generate 72% of their AI value in core functions (operations, marketing, sales), not support functions. The four layers of enterprise AI ROI — Efficiency, Revenue/Margin, Risk/Compliance, Strategic Capability — map cleanly onto DRI's maturity arc. — BCG, 2025; Fusemachines, "AI ROI in 2026," 2026.

3. **The 10/20/70 rule anchors the Capabilities dimension.** 10% of resources should go to algorithms, 20% to technology infrastructure, and 70% to people, processes, and change management. Most organisations get this backwards — they spend on the 10% and 20% but starve the 70%. Only 6% of companies have begun upskilling "meaningfully" versus 89% acknowledging the need. Only 17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders have technical skills; just 5% held technical roles during their careers. Forbes (January 2026) calls 10/20/70 "probably the most important principle CEOs and everyone else should internalise." — BCG, 2025; McKinsey, "Building the AI Muscle of Your Business Leaders," 2025; Forbes, January 2026.

4. **Trust is a measurable leading indicator of AI success and adoption, not just a compliance checkbox.** In 57% of high-maturity organisations, business units trust and are ready to use new AI solutions versus only 14% of low-maturity organisations. High-maturity organisations keep AI projects operational 3+ years (45% vs. 20%). 77% of executives say trust is the foundation for unlocking AI's benefits; yet 74% of companies have paused AI projects due to risk concerns. Only 2% have fully operationalised responsible AI. Trust covers three dimensions per `docs/instructions.md`: regulatory compliance, customer/user trust, and employee trust. — Gartner, Q4 2024 survey (432 respondents); Accenture/AWS/Stanford, 2025 (1,000+ executives).

5. **External decision frameworks converge on three angles.** Pertama Partners' AI Strategic Decision Framework (2025) explicitly structures CEO decisions around Value, Capability, and Trust. The IJSRA three-gate decision funnel (2025) compresses cycle times by 33–40% and doubles PoC-to-production conversion rates — its gates map to Value (does this create business value?), Capability (can we deliver?), and Trust (can we govern this?). Microsoft's AI Decision Framework (2025) uses business-outcome-first intake across five recurring decision types: invest/don't invest, build/buy/partner, prioritise, scale, retire. — Pertama Partners, 2025; IJSRA, 2025; Microsoft, 2025.

## European Context

- **EU AI Act timeline and penalties.** Entered into force 1 August 2024. Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI applied 2 February 2025. AI literacy training obligations began February 2025. GPAI obligations applied 2 August 2025. Full high-risk system requirements apply 2 August 2026. Penalties reach €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover. Practical challenges remain: delays in designating national competent authorities, lack of harmonised standards, risk of increased compliance costs. — EU Commission; Orrick, 2025; European Parliament Research Service, 2025.

- **EU AI Pact as trust moat.** Over 230 voluntary pledgers, 3,200+ community members. 63% of signatories are EU-based companies. More than 50% have gone beyond mandatory requirements — advancing AI literacy, supporting startups/SMEs, adapting tools to AI Act standards. Proactive compliance is competitive advantage: shorter operational cycles, reduced costs, ability to scale AI safely in compliance-heavy environments. — European Commission, "AI Pact Pledgers' Achievements," 2025.

- **GDPR/AI Act overlap creates compliance complexity.** Both regimes impose overlapping transparency, logging, and impact assessment obligations. 7 of 8 high-risk AI system categories involve personal data processing — over 87% of high-risk AI cases require GDPR compliance in addition to AI Act compliance. — Lexology; European Parliament; Advisera, 2025.

- **European business confidence and investment.** 91% of European business leaders expect revenue growth in 2026 amid rising AI investment; confidence increased five percentage points vs. prior year, with France (+22 points), Netherlands (+17 points), and UK (+5 points) leading. Organisations expect to allocate 5% of annual budgets to AI by 2026, up from 3% in 2025. More than half commit to sustained investment over a five-year horizon. — Accenture, 2026; Capgemini, "The Multi-Year AI Advantage," 2026.

- **European AI talent landscape.** AI talent in the EU more than doubled between 2016 and 2023. 28% of European workers already use AI tools; but 40–60% have poor AI literacy. 6 in 10 employees face some form of AI-related task transformation. 15% of employees fear job loss within five years (higher in Southern and Central Europe). The EU AI Act requires AI literacy training since February 2025. Strategic responses include the AI Continent Action Plan, Union of Skills strategy, AI Skills Academy, and proposed EU talent pool regulation. — European Commission, 2025; CEDEFOP, 2025; Interface EU, 2025.

- **European productivity evidence.** AI adoption increases labour productivity by 4% on average in European firms, driven by capital deepening rather than job losses — a counter to displacement fears. — EIB Working Paper 2026/02.

- **EESC positions trust as competitive advantage.** "Building trustworthy AI can be Europe's competitive advantage" — trust is not a regulatory burden but a strategic asset that encourages adoption, unlocks innovation, and drives value creation. — EESC, 2025.

- **The executive-employee trust gap.** 84% of European executives express confidence in AI's business impact, but only 61% of employees share that belief — a 23-point gap that threatens to undermine the full value of AI investments. — Accenture, 2026.

- **PE and mid-market.** AI is the "third pillar of value enhancement" alongside financial engineering and operational excellence (Roland Berger, 2025). Mid-market firms ($500M–$5B) account for nearly 40% of buyout activity; competitive advantage shifting from capital access to data standardisation. AI-powered deal intelligence identifies targets weeks ahead of competitors. Use-case-driven AI sprints deliver results in 4–6 weeks. EQT's Motherbrain platform is a differentiator. AI usage among mid-sized European businesses grew 60% year-over-year. — Roland Berger, 2025; Equiforte, 2025; EQT Group, 2025.

## Beat-by-Beat Evidence Map

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook: The Thirty-One to Seven Problem
- **31% → 7% ROI collapse:** DIMA AI, 2026.
- **95% of enterprise GenAI pilots fail sustained P&L impact:** MIT Sloan, "GenAI Divide," August 2025.
- **Only 5% of 1,250+ firms achieve AI value at scale; 60% achieve no material value:** BCG, 2025.
- **$30–40 billion in global AI spending producing near-zero ROI for most:** deepsense.ai, 2025.
- **Root cause is decision failure, not technology failure:** MIT study found failures due to "learning gap" in enterprise integration — generic tools not adapted to workflows, pilots remain isolated, lack of strategic alignment.

### Beat 2 — The Problem: Why Most AI Investments Disappoint
- **Micro-productivity trap (Bain):** Proliferation of proofs of concept delivering modest, localised gains that fail to scale. "Grassroots experimentation… does not self-organize into enterprise-wide impact."
- **McKinsey function-level data:** 88% use AI in at least one function, but only 39% report EBIT impact at enterprise level. Workflow redesign has the biggest effect on EBIT capture — yet only 21% have done it.
- **Budget misallocation:** MIT found over 50% of AI budgets go to sales and marketing, yet strongest ROI appears in back-office automation and business process optimisation.
- **Vendor vs. internal success:** Vendor partnerships succeed 67% of the time versus 33% for internally developed systems (MIT, 2025).
- **Missing baseline problem:** The single biggest measurement mistake is failing to establish performance baselines before implementing AI (DIMA AI, 2026).
- **Measurement window problem:** Traditional ROI models fail for AI because systems evolve with data/integration depth. Multi-year tracking needed; quarterly cost savings alone rarely reflect transformation (AICompetence.org, 2026).
- **69% of enterprise GenAI initiatives stall before operationalisation** (IJSRA, 2025).
- **Nuance on 95% stat:** MIT measured P&L impact of pilots at short timescales, not long-term scaled deployments. The chapter should use this carefully — a pilot-stage finding, not a verdict on AI's potential (BigDataWire critique, September 2025).

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction: The AI Decision Framework
**Value:**
- BCG DRI taxonomy: Deploy (10–15% productivity), Reshape (end-to-end function redesign), Invent (new business models/revenue).
- 72% of AI value in core functions (BCG).
- Four-layer ROI: Efficiency, Revenue/Margin, Risk/Compliance, Strategic Capability (Fusemachines, 2026).
- McKinsey function-level revenue impact: Strategy/corporate finance revenue increases jumped 43% → 70%; supply chain 67%; marketing/sales 66%; service operations 63%.
- Quantified value cases: procurement AI cost reductions up to 27% (Dreher, 2025); finance AP automation: 90% reduction in data entry hours, payback in 8 months (SUPALABS, 2025); predictive maintenance: 30% cost reduction, 40% less unplanned downtime (SUPALABS/KFactory EU, 2025).
- Value must be measured against baselines, not aspirations; and multi-year, not six-month windows.

**Capabilities:**
- 10/20/70 rule: 70% is people, process, and change management (BCG).
- Only 6% upskill meaningfully vs. 89% acknowledging the need (BCG, 2025).
- Only 17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders have technical skills; just 5% held technical roles (McKinsey, 2025).
- Domain leader (N-2/N-3): "probably the single most critical role" — translates vision into operational change. A domain = 5–15 interrelated use cases. Must develop "second muscle" beyond traditional executive skills (McKinsey).
- Agent Orchestrator: "The most important job of 2026" — not IT but genuine middle management managing synthetic talent. 82% of executives expect AI agents within 18 months, but only 23% feel confident about effective integration (Eightfold AI, 2026; HBR, February 2026).
- Only 48% of AI projects reach production; average prototype-to-production timeline: eight months (Everworker AI, 2026).
- Chief AI Officer ≈ 10% higher ROI (deepsense.ai, 2025).

**Trust:**
- Three dimensions: regulatory compliance, user/customer trust, employee trust (per `docs/instructions.md`).
- Gartner trust-maturity correlation: 57% of high-maturity orgs' business units trust AI vs. 14% of low-maturity. 45% of high-maturity keep projects 3+ years vs. 20%. 91% of high-maturity have dedicated AI leaders (Gartner, Q4 2024, 432 respondents).
- Accenture: 77% say trust is foundation for AI benefits; 74% paused projects due to risk; only 2% fully operationalised responsible AI. GenAI-driven incidents account for roughly two-thirds of all AI incidents (Accenture/AWS/Stanford, 2025).
- Trust frameworks converge: IBM (five pillars), Microsoft (NIST-aligned), Deloitte (seven dimensions), NIST AI RMF (Govern→Map→Measure→Manage). Implementation remains challenging — resource intensity, scalability, ambiguous targets (Springer, 2025).
- EU AI Act operationalises these principles for Europe: risk-based classification, penalties up to €35M / 7% global turnover.

**Three-angle interactions:**
- High-value but low-capability → sequence capability-building before scaling (McKinsey domain leader model).
- High-value but high-risk → trust-first approach; governance before scaling. Allianz-Anthropic model.
- Quick wins (Deploy) → build momentum → fund capability and trust investment → scale Reshape and Invent.

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications: From Scorecard to Boardroom
- **Board governance gap:** 66% of boards report limited-to-no AI knowledge. Only 15% receive AI-related metrics. Nearly 50% say AI is not yet on the board agenda. Only 14% discuss AI at every meeting. Yet 88% use AI in at least one function (Deloitte, 2025; main research section 9).
- **2026 Board roadmap (WilmerHale, January 2026):** (1) Comprehensive AI use/risk assessment, (2) Establish oversight structures, (3) Implement risk management aligned with recognised frameworks, (4) Empower teams to leverage AI.
- **EU AI Act penalties:** €35M or 7% of global turnover — board accountability.
- **EU AI Pact as proactive governance:** 230+ signatories; 63% EU-based; 50%+ going beyond mandatory requirements.
- **Integration with existing risk management:** 72% of boards already have risk oversight committees; 80%+ have risk management experts. AI governance must integrate with enterprise risk management, not operate in isolation.

### Beat 5 — Closing: Trust as the European Edge
- **91% of European business leaders expect revenue growth in 2026** amid rising AI investment (Accenture, 2026).
- **EESC:** "Building trustworthy AI can be Europe's competitive advantage."
- **Executive-employee trust gap:** 84% executive confidence vs. 61% employee belief — 23-point gap (Accenture, 2026).
- **EU AI Act + GDPR + cultural expectations** create a "trust moat" — when navigated well, accelerates adoption rather than slowing it.
- **European insurers lead in responsible AI talent:** 47% of specialists in data ethics, governance, and risk, despite 36% of total AI talent (main research section 6).
- **Capgemini:** Critical enablers include executive sponsorship (67%), external partnerships (59%), governance/ethical frameworks (53%) — trust infrastructure enables scale.
