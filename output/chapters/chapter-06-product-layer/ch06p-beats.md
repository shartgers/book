# Chapter 06 — The Product Layer

## Strategic Tension

Most European organisations have an AI strategy. Far fewer have a product strategy that is AI-native. The Products layer of the AI Transformation Framework — Layer 2 — is where strategy moves from corporate intention to customer experience: which customers, in which moments, through which platforms, removing which frictions, delivering which value. Yet in the majority of organisations this layer is delegated to product teams or IT, defined once at enterprise level, and never broken down by business unit or department. The result is a generic portfolio of AI features that impresses in a board deck but fails to compound into differentiated customer value. The tension: CEOs who would never delegate market strategy routinely delegate the product layer that determines where AI meets the customer.

## Core Principle

The Product Layer must be defined per business unit — not as a single enterprise abstraction — because where to play and how to win with AI is a leadership question, not a product management question.

## Named Framework

**The AI Product Canvas** — a five-element model for defining the Product Layer per business unit or department: (1) Value Proposition — what business value AI delivers to this unit's customers; (2) Customer Journeys — which journeys are being reimagined (including zero-click and adaptive journeys); (3) Personas and Moments — who the customer is and in which moments AI intervenes; (4) Platforms and Channels — through which platforms or channels the AI-native experience is delivered; (5) Frictions to Remove — which specific frictions, handoffs, or delays AI eliminates. One canvas per business unit; the collection forms the organisation's Product Layer.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook
Section heading: The Layer Most Often Delegated
The chapter hook: most AI strategies define what the organisation will build, but fewer define who it will serve, in what customer moment, and what business value it will deliver — unit by unit. Open with the observation that Product is the framework layer most commonly owned by product teams or IT rather than the CEO and board. Contrast: the same CEO who personally owns market strategy and competitive positioning delegates the AI product strategy to a product owner three levels down. Set up the argument that Product Layer ownership is a strategic leadership question.

### Beat 2 — The Problem
Section heading: Features Without a Strategy
Why the current approach fails. Most organisations build AI features (chatbots, recommendation engines, copilots) without a coherent product strategy per business unit. Evidence: the majority of AI use cases remain stuck in pilot; organic web traffic is declining 15–25% as zero-click AI results reshape discovery; 80% of Netflix viewing comes from AI recommendations, not search; 73% of B2B buyers want personalised, B2C-like experiences. The problem is not a shortage of AI features — it is the absence of a product-level view that connects features to customer value, journey by journey, unit by unit. Without that view, AI features remain experiments rather than compounding product advantages.

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction
Section heading: The AI Product Canvas
Introduce the AI Product Canvas: a five-element model that defines the Product Layer for each business unit or department. Walk through the five elements: (1) Value Proposition, (2) Customer Journeys, (3) Personas and Moments, (4) Platforms and Channels, (5) Frictions to Remove. Explain that one canvas is completed per business unit; the collection of canvases forms the organisation's Product Layer. Emphasise that this is a leadership exercise — the CEO or business unit head fills it in, not the product team alone. Connect to the AI Transformation Framework: the canvas operationalises Layer 2 (Products) as introduced in Chapter 5. Include the concept of AI-native products (products that could not exist without AI) and adaptive customer journeys. The canvas must be visualisable as a simple diagram (five boxes or columns, one row per business unit).

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications
Section heading: Why the CEO Must Own the Product Layer
The Product Layer is a strategic question of where to play and how to win — the classic strategy question applied to AI. It cannot be delegated to product management or IT without losing strategic coherence. The CEO and board must own the Product Layer for three reasons: (1) it determines competitive positioning per market segment; (2) it shapes investment allocation across business units; (3) it defines the customer experience that differentiates the organisation. Connect to EU AI Act: product compliance obligations (risk classification of AI-enabled products and services) sit in the Products layer and require board-level awareness, not just product-team compliance. Reference the governance gap from Chapter 5 (two-thirds of boards with limited AI knowledge). The Product Layer is where governance meets the customer.

### Beat 5 — Closing / Transition
Section heading: From What You Offer to How You Operate
The Product Layer defines what the organisation delivers to customers through AI. But delivery requires the right people, roles, processes, and org design — the Operating Model layer (Layer 3) that Chapter 7 covers. Close by connecting the two: a brilliant AI Product Canvas is worthless if the organisation cannot staff, structure, and operate the teams needed to build and run those products. The next chapter moves from what you offer to how you organise to deliver it.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions
Section heading: Questions for the Board: The Product Layer
1. Has your organisation defined the Product Layer — product vision, customer journeys, personas, frictions — for each business unit, or only at enterprise level?
2. Who owns the AI product strategy in your organisation: the CEO and business unit heads, or product management and IT?
3. Which customer journeys are being fundamentally reimagined by AI, and which are merely being optimised with bolt-on features?
4. Can each business unit articulate its AI Product Canvas — the value proposition, journeys, personas, platforms, and frictions AI will address?
5. How does your board assess whether the Product Layer is creating differentiated customer value or simply accumulating features?

### Beat 7 — Case Study
Company: Shopify
Dilemma: How Shopify redefined its entire product suite around AI — embedding 70+ AI features (Sidekick, Shopify Magic, AI-generated product descriptions, image editing, customer insights) across the platform — whilst making AI usage a baseline expectation for every team. The dilemma: invest in AI as add-on features for merchants, or redesign the product experience so that AI is the platform, not a feature on it. Shopify chose the latter, and CEO Tobi Lütke made "reflexive AI usage" a company-wide mandate, requiring teams to demonstrate why AI cannot do a job before requesting headcount. The case illustrates the Product Layer applied platform-wide: value proposition (merchant productivity), customer journeys (from store setup to marketing to fulfilment), frictions removed (content creation, customer service, inventory management), and the leadership decision to make product-layer AI a CEO-level priority rather than a product-team initiative.

## Thesis Check

This chapter advances the book's central argument by showing that European CEOs who delegate the Product Layer — the layer where AI meets the customer — are ceding the most consequential strategic decision to product teams and IT. The AI Product Canvas gives leaders a practical tool to reclaim ownership of where to play and how to win with AI, unit by unit, closing the gap between AI strategy and customer value.
