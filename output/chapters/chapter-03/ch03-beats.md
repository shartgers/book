# Chapter 03 — What the Agentic Organisation Looks Like

## Strategic Tension

Most leaders now accept that AI transformation is necessary. Chapters 1 and 2 established the scale of the shift and the non-negotiable role of leadership. But acceptance is not the same as clarity. Ask a leadership team to describe what their organisation looks like after transformation, not the tools it uses but how it actually operates, and the answer is typically vague, technology-centric, or borrowed from a vendor pitch. You cannot redesign what you cannot describe. The tension in this chapter is between the urgency to act (established) and the absence of a concrete, shared picture of the destination. Without that picture, every subsequent decision, from hiring to governance to technology investment, lacks an anchor.

## Core Principle

The Agentic Organisation is defined not by the AI it deploys but by how it organises people, processes, products, technology, and data around human-agent collaboration, and the shift begins with redefining the employee from task-doer to orchestrator.

## Named Framework

The AI Transformation Framework is a four-layer model (Vision, Products, Operating Model, Foundation) that defines what the Agentic Organisation is made of and how to assess readiness. It is introduced in full in Chapter 5. This chapter establishes the vision and the human side: the agentic employee, three key roles, and the mindset shift—foundational concepts only.

Section heading: You Cannot Build What You Cannot Describe

Connect to Chapters 1 and 2: urgency and leadership accountability are established; this chapter supplies the missing ingredient — a concrete, shared picture of the destination that makes every subsequent decision coherent.

### Beat 2 — The Problem

Section heading: The Definition Gap

The definition gap. Most organisations that claim to be "doing AI" are bolting tools onto unchanged structures. The evidence: McKinsey's Gen AI Paradox (8 in 10 companies use gen AI, 8 in 10 report no significant bottom-line impact); Deloitte's finding that 80% of AI deployment effort is consumed by "unglamorous tasks" (data engineering, stakeholder alignment, governance, workflow integration); only 12% of organisations have AI-ready data (WEF). The problem is not technology adoption. It is that organisations lack a coherent model of what they are building towards. Without a shared picture of how the organisation operates in the transformed state—how people, process, product, technology, and data fit together—investment scatters across disconnected pilots. The micro-productivity trap (defined in Ch1) persists because there is no shared picture of what lies beyond it.

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications

Section heading: The Governance Gap

Transformation creates governance demands across the whole organisation, not just technology. Board-level framing: a structured framework (introduced in Ch 5) is the basis for asking "where are we, and where do we need to be?" across each dimension. Governance of the agentic employee: tiered autonomy (risk-adjusted boundaries for what agents and employees can do independently), comprehensive logging, circuit breakers. The governance gap (only 21% of organisations have rigorous governance in place, per Deloitte). For European organisations, EU AI Act obligations map onto these dimensions: AI literacy (people), risk classification (process/technology), data protection (data), product compliance (products). The board needs a structured way to assess transformation progress rather than relying on anecdotal pilot reports; Chapters 5 and 13 develop this.

### Beat 5 — Closing / Transition

Section heading: From Picture to Route

The AI Transformation Framework provides the picture of the destination. But a picture is not a route. The next question is: how does transformation actually scale? The answer is not "everywhere at once." It progresses through four distinct tiers, each with different governance, different leadership demands, and different measures of success. Chapter 4 introduces The Four Tiers of Transformation (Individual, Team, Department, Organisation) and shows why skipping a tier, or assuming one tier's success automatically produces the next, is the most common structural mistake in AI programmes.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions

Section heading: Questions for the Board

1. If you asked your leadership team to describe the Agentic Organisation you are building (not the tools you are buying), would they give a consistent answer?
2. Which of the four framework layers (Vision, Products, Operating Model, Foundation)—and the decision framework Value, Capabilities, Trust (Ch 8)—is your organisation most advanced on, and which is most neglected? (Ch 5 introduces the full framework.)
3. How many of your employees could be described as agentic employees today, and what is your plan to develop the three profiles (supervisor, specialist, augmented frontline worker) across the workforce?
4. Are you designing workflows around human-agent collaboration from scratch, or layering AI onto processes designed for humans working alone?
5. Does your board have a structured way to assess AI transformation progress across these dimensions, or does it rely on technology metrics and pilot updates?

Company: Klarna
Dilemma: Klarna pursued aggressive AI-first automation under direct CEO leadership, achieving dramatic efficiency gains (AI assistant handling 2.3 million conversations monthly, equivalent of 700 agents, resolution time from 11 minutes to under 2), then discovered the limits of automation for complex, emotional customer interactions and partially reversed course by re-hiring human agents. The case illustrates both the power and the boundaries of the Agentic Organisation model: the destination is not full automation but a deliberately designed hybrid where humans and agents each do what they do best. Klarna's experience maps onto the AI Transformation Framework's four layers (vision and product choices, operating model, foundation) and the broader governance and value/trust dimensions covered in later chapters, and demonstrates why a holistic model matters rather than a technology checklist.
