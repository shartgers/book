# Chapter 03 — What the Agentic Organisation Looks Like

## Strategic Tension

Most leaders now accept that AI transformation is necessary. Chapters 1 and 2 established the scale of the shift and the non-negotiable role of leadership. But acceptance is not the same as clarity. Ask a leadership team to describe what their organisation looks like after transformation, not the tools it uses but how it actually operates, and the answer is typically vague, technology-centric, or borrowed from a vendor pitch. You cannot redesign what you cannot describe. The tension in this chapter is between the urgency to act (established) and the absence of a concrete, shared picture of the destination. Without that picture, every subsequent decision, from hiring to governance to technology investment, lacks an anchor.

## Core Principle

The Agentic Organisation is defined not by the AI it deploys but by how it organises people, processes, products, technology, and data around human-agent collaboration, and the shift begins with redefining the employee from task-doer to orchestrator.

## Named Framework

The AI Transformation Framework: five pillars (Product, People, Process, Technology, Data) that define what the Agentic Organisation is made of and how to assess organisational readiness.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook

Open with the hook from chapter-hooks.md: you cannot build what you cannot describe. Before the playbook, before the phased roadmap, before the first pilot, a CEO needs a concrete working definition of the destination. What does the Agentic Organisation actually look like? How does it make decisions? How does it differ from what you have today? Establish that the answer centres on a person, not a technology. The Agentic Organisation is built on agentic employees. Connect to the Redesign level from Chapter 1's Paradigm Shift Diagnostic: this chapter provides the picture of what Redesign actually produces.

### Beat 2 — The Problem

The definition gap. Most organisations that claim to be "doing AI" are bolting tools onto unchanged structures. The evidence: McKinsey's Gen AI Paradox (8 in 10 companies use gen AI, 8 in 10 report no significant bottom-line impact); Deloitte's finding that 80% of AI deployment effort is consumed by "unglamorous tasks" (data engineering, stakeholder alignment, governance, workflow integration); only 12% of organisations have AI-ready data (WEF). The problem is not technology adoption. It is that organisations lack a coherent model of what they are building towards. Without a target architecture for how people, processes, products, technology, and data interact in the transformed state, investment scatters across disconnected pilots. The micro-productivity trap (defined in Ch1) persists because there is no shared picture of what lies beyond it.

### Beat 3 — Framework Introduction

**The AI Transformation Framework** — introduce the exact name and five pillars in order: Product, People, Process, Technology, Data. Each pillar gets a concise description of what it means in an Agentic Organisation (not legacy definitions):

- **Product**: AI-native products and services; dynamic, adaptive customer journeys; zero-click discovery reshaping how value reaches customers.
- **People**: The agentic employee (introduce and define here). Three profiles: supervisor (orchestrates multiple agents across workflows), specialist (handles exceptions agents escalate), augmented frontline worker (freed from repetitive tasks to focus on human-centric work). The mindset shift table (old mindset vs agentic mindset, from research/the-agentic-employee.md).
- **Process**: End-to-end workflow redesign around human-agent collaboration, not task-level automation. Bain's zero-based process design. Measurable transformation across functions (marketing, sales, customer service, finance, HR, software development).
- **Technology**: The emerging enterprise stack (MCP as "USB-C for AI," A2A protocol, seven-layer architecture). Skills-based architecture: introduce "a skill" (reusable AI workflow) as the building block of agentic capability. The best agentic employees create skills and share them, producing a compounding network effect.
- **Data**: Data as strategic asset and competitive moat. McKinsey's "walled data gardens." The readiness gap (fewer than 1 in 5 organisations have high data maturity; 57% estimate their data is not AI-ready).

Include definition blocks for: The AI Transformation Framework, the agentic employee, a skill (reusable AI workflow). Introduce human-in-the-loop as a concept within the People or Process pillar discussion.

The framework must be visualisable as a five-pillar diagram.

### Beat 4 — Case Study

Company: Klarna
Dilemma: Klarna pursued aggressive AI-first automation under direct CEO leadership, achieving dramatic efficiency gains (AI assistant handling 2.3 million conversations monthly, equivalent of 700 agents, resolution time from 11 minutes to under 2), then discovered the limits of automation for complex, emotional customer interactions and partially reversed course by re-hiring human agents. The case illustrates both the power and the boundaries of the Agentic Organisation model: the destination is not full automation but a deliberately designed hybrid where humans and agents each do what they do best. Klarna's experience maps directly onto the five pillars (product redesign, people redeployment, process transformation, technology choices, data-driven learning) and demonstrates why the framework matters as a holistic model rather than a technology checklist.

### Beat 5 — Governance Implications

The AI Transformation Framework creates governance demands across all five pillars, not just technology. Board-level framing: the framework is the basis for asking "where are we, and where do we need to be?" on each pillar. Governance of the agentic employee: tiered autonomy (risk-adjusted boundaries for what agents and employees can do independently), comprehensive logging, circuit breakers. The governance gap (only 21% of organisations have rigorous governance in place, per Deloitte). For European organisations, EU AI Act obligations map directly onto the framework: AI literacy requirements (People), risk classification (Process/Technology), data protection (Data), product compliance (Product). The framework gives the board a structured way to assess transformation progress rather than relying on anecdotal pilot reports.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions

1. If you asked your leadership team to describe the Agentic Organisation you are building (not the tools you are buying), would they give a consistent answer?
2. Which of the five pillars (Product, People, Process, Technology, Data) is your organisation most advanced on, and which is most neglected?
3. How many of your employees could be described as agentic employees today, and what is your plan to develop the three profiles (supervisor, specialist, augmented frontline worker) across the workforce?
4. Are you designing workflows around human-agent collaboration from scratch, or layering AI onto processes designed for humans working alone?
5. Does your board have a structured way to assess AI transformation progress across all five pillars, or does it rely on technology metrics and pilot updates?

### Beat 7 — Closing / Transition

The AI Transformation Framework provides the picture of the destination. But a picture is not a route. The next question is: how does transformation actually scale? The answer is not "everywhere at once." It progresses through four distinct tiers, each with different governance, different leadership demands, and different measures of success. Chapter 4 introduces The Four Tiers of Transformation (Individual, Team, Department, Organisation) and shows why skipping a tier, or assuming one tier's success automatically produces the next, is the most common structural mistake in AI programmes.

## Thesis Check
