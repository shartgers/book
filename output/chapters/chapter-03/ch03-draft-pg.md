## Opening Hook

You cannot build what you cannot describe.

Before the playbook, before the phased roadmap, before the first pilot, every leadership team needs something that is surprisingly rare: a concrete, shared picture of the destination. Not a vision statement. Not a vendor's slide deck. A working definition of what the organisation actually looks like when transformation is complete: how it makes decisions, how work flows, where humans engage and where agents act.

Ask most leadership teams to describe the organisation they are building and the answer tends towards the technology. New platforms. Integrated data systems. Automated workflows. What is typically missing is a coherent account of the human side of the equation: who the people are in this new organisation, what makes their work different, and how the combination of human judgment and agentic execution produces outcomes that neither could produce alone.

The Agentic Organisation, as defined in Chapter 1, is a company redesigned so that humans and AI agents work together as a unified system. That definition establishes the principle. This chapter makes it concrete.

At the centre of that concreteness is not a technology decision. It is a people decision. The Agentic Organisation is built on agentic employees: people who orchestrate AI agents rather than merely using tools, who create reusable workflows and share them across the organisation, who exercise judgment at the moments that matter most. The shift from task-doer to orchestrator is not a minor adjustment in job description. It is a fundamental redefinition of what it means to be productive, skilled, and valuable at work.

This chapter introduces the AI Transformation Framework, a five-pillar model that defines what the Agentic Organisation is made of and how to assess where the organisation stands today. It is the anchor against which every subsequent decision in this book will be tested: from which pilots to prioritise, to how the board governs progress.

The Paradigm Shift Diagnostic from Chapter 1 described three levels of response to AI: Bolt-On, Adapt, and Redesign. This chapter describes what the Redesign level actually produces. It is the destination made operational.

## The Problem

Almost every significant organisation now describes AI transformation as a strategic priority. Strategy documents reference it. Budget allocations reflect it. Board presentations include it.

Yet when leadership teams are asked to describe what their transformed organisation actually looks like — not which tools it uses, but how it operates, how decisions are made, and what makes it structurally different from its predecessors — the answers are typically vague, technology-centric, or borrowed from a framework without being genuinely owned.

The data reflects this. Eight in ten companies use generative AI. Equally many report no significant bottom-line impact. This paradox exists not because the technology fails to perform, but because most organisations have not defined what they are building clearly enough to design towards it.

Eighty per cent of the work involved in deploying AI agents is consumed by tasks that rarely appear on strategic agendas: data engineering, stakeholder alignment, governance design, and workflow integration. This is not a complaint about implementation difficulty. It is a diagnostic signal. When organisations have not defined the destination, the work required to get there has no architecture. Each decision is made locally, disconnected from adjacent decisions being made in other functions. The effort is real; the compounding is absent.

The readiness gap on data, one of the five pillars examined in this chapter, illustrates the point. Fewer than one in eight organisations have data that is genuinely AI-ready. More than half estimate their data is not fit for the AI they are attempting to deploy. These are not technology shortfalls. They are the result of organisations that know which AI tools they are buying but have not defined what kind of organisation those tools are meant to serve.

Without a clear, multi-dimensional model of the destination, the micro-productivity trap from Chapter 1 persists indefinitely. Individual experiments succeed. Teams report real gains. Aggregate impact remains disappointingly small, because there is no shared picture connecting the experiments to a coherent whole.

The question that breaks this pattern is not "which AI use case should we run next?" It is "what does our organisation look like when transformation is complete, and is everything we are doing designed towards that picture?"

The AI Transformation Framework provides that picture.

## The AI Transformation Framework

The AI Transformation Framework defines the Agentic Organisation across five pillars: Product, People, Process, Technology, and Data. Together, they describe not just what the organisation uses but how it operates, how it creates value, and where competitive advantage is built and sustained.

> **Definition: The AI Transformation Framework**
>
> The AI Transformation Framework is a five-pillar model for describing and assessing the Agentic Organisation. The pillars are Product, People, Process, Technology, and Data. Together they define the dimensions along which an organisation must redesign to operate with human-agent collaboration at its core. Each pillar has a distinct meaning in the context of agentic transformation. Combined, they provide both a picture of the destination and a structured diagnostic for assessing where the organisation currently stands.

**Product**

The Agentic Organisation does not add AI features to existing products. It redesigns what it offers around AI-native experiences: products and services that could not function, or could not exist, without AI at their core.

The clearest signal of this shift is in how value reaches customers. Consumer discovery is being restructured at speed. Research suggests approximately 80% of consumers now rely on "zero-click" AI results in over 40% of searches, reducing organic traffic to traditional destinations by 15 to 25%. This has been described as the most consequential shift in discovery since the introduction of the search bar. Organisations whose products are not visible in AI-mediated discovery environments are not competing on the same terms as those that are.

Personalisation represents the most immediate product transformation. A significant share of major streaming platforms' viewing hours is driven by AI-powered recommendations rather than active user selection. E-commerce purchases attributable to personalised suggestions constitute a substantial share of total revenue at the platforms leading in this area. In B2B markets, over 70% of buyers now expect the kind of personalised experience they encounter in their consumer lives.

For European organisations, the shift in how customers find, evaluate, and engage with products is not a future development to monitor. It is already determining which organisations capture attention and which are bypassed. The product redesign question is not whether to make this transition, but how to manage the complexity of redesigning customer journeys that are already in active use.

**People**

The People pillar is where the AI Transformation Framework has its centre of gravity. The Agentic Organisation does not deploy AI alongside existing ways of working and expect the combination to produce transformation. It redefines what productive, skilled, and valuable work looks like at every level.

The concept at the core of this pillar is the agentic employee.

> **Definition: The agentic employee**
>
> An agentic employee is a person who works alongside autonomous AI agents as teammates, not merely as tools. Agentic employees orchestrate rather than execute: they set goals, define boundaries, review outputs, and handle exceptions, whilst agents handle routine and complex execution. The best agentic employees create reusable AI workflows and share them across the organisation, producing a compounding network effect where one person's innovation multiplies the capability of everyone around them.

Three distinct working profiles are emerging across organisations that have made meaningful progress on this pillar.

Supervisors orchestrate multiple AI agents across workflows. A project manager coordinating agents that handle scheduling, resource allocation, status tracking, and reporting, whilst focusing their own attention on decisions requiring stakeholder judgment and course correction, is working in this profile. The supervisor's value lies not in the execution they perform but in the coordination they provide and the judgment they apply at decision points agents cannot navigate reliably.

Specialists handle the exceptions and edge cases that agents identify as requiring human assessment. A compliance officer reviewing flagged transactions that an agent has escalated is working in this profile. The specialist's value is precision and judgment in exactly the situations where the cost of an error is highest. As agents become more capable, the volume of escalations may decline, but the significance of each individual escalation typically increases.

Augmented frontline workers use AI agents to eliminate the repetitive tasks that previously occupied the majority of their working day, freeing them to focus on work that benefits from human presence. A customer success manager whose agent handles data gathering, case preparation, and follow-up documentation, leaving the person free to focus on the relationship itself, exemplifies this profile.

These profiles are not job titles or roles to be created. They are modes of working that are emerging across functions and seniority levels as agentic tools become embedded in day-to-day operations. The leadership question is not which employees fit which profile, but whether the organisation is developing all three deliberately, and at a pace that matches the competitive environment.

The underlying shift is one of mindset as much as skill.

| Old mindset | Agentic mindset |
|---|---|
| I do the work | I orchestrate the work |
| AI is a tool I use sometimes | AI agents are teammates I collaborate with constantly |
| My value is what I produce | My value is the outcomes I enable |
| I learn skills to stay relevant | I create and share skills to multiply impact |
| I follow processes | I design and improve processes |
| I need instructions | I set goals and boundaries for agents, then review results |

Demand for AI fluency across all roles, not just technical ones, has grown sevenfold in two years. That rate of change reflects how rapidly the ability to work effectively with AI has moved from a specialised capability to a general professional requirement. The organisations furthest ahead in this transition are not only training their people in AI tools. They are restructuring how work is designed, how performance is measured, and how careers progress to reflect the emerging reality of agentic collaboration.

The most effective agentic employees do not just use AI. They create skills — the reusable AI workflows that package specific capabilities into shareable form — and distribute them across the organisation. A single well-built skill can multiply the productivity of an entire function. This is one of the primary mechanisms by which the Agentic Organisation compounds its advantage over time.

**Process**

The Process pillar is where the largest gap between stated ambition and actual practice tends to live. Most organisations have automated some tasks with AI. Very few have redesigned workflows end to end.

The distinction is not minor. Value in the Agentic Organisation is created primarily at the workflow level, not the task level. A workflow redesigned from the beginning for human-agent collaboration, with agents acting as primary participants in the high-volume steps and humans engaged at decision points requiring judgment, operates fundamentally differently from a workflow where AI tools have been layered onto steps designed for sequential human execution.

Bain's concept of zero-based process design, referenced in Chapter 1 as the operating principle of the Redesign level, captures this requirement precisely: design processes from the beginning with AI as a native participant, not an optimisation layer applied to a process that already exists. The resulting workflow architecture is different in kind, not just in efficiency.

Across functions, the measurable difference between task-level AI adoption and workflow-level redesign is substantial. Marketing operations that have redesigned campaign workflows end to end report cycle time reductions of a scale that isolated task automation does not produce. Sales organisations that have redesigned the full prospecting-to-close workflow around human-agent collaboration report material improvements in deal velocity and acquisition cost. Finance functions running agentic procure-to-pay workflows are seeing cycle time reductions that suggest structural rather than marginal change, with professional services research indicating that the majority of finance processing will be heavily automated within a few years.

The unifying pattern across all of these is that the gains come from redesigning workflow architecture, not from adding an AI feature to an existing step.

A concept essential to managing this redesign responsibly is human-in-the-loop.

> **Definition: Human-in-the-loop**
>
> Human-in-the-loop refers to the deliberate design of human checkpoints within agentic workflows. These are the points at which an agent pauses and presents its output or proposed action to a human before proceeding. Human-in-the-loop is a governance design choice about where human judgment is most valuable and where the consequences of an error are highest. Effective agentic workflows do not have humans in the loop everywhere — that would eliminate the efficiency advantage of agentic design. They have humans in the right loops.

The governance discipline of the Process pillar requires organisations to identify, for each workflow, where human judgment is genuinely necessary and where it has been inserted by convention, and to distinguish these two things with the same rigour applied to other architectural decisions.

**Technology**

The Technology pillar is where most organisations begin their AI journey. It is also where the risk of misallocation is highest. Comfort in the technology dimension can tempt organisations to over-invest here relative to the four pillars that ultimately determine whether technology creates value. The finding established in Chapter 1, that algorithms account for only 10% of what is required to generate real AI value whilst people and processes account for 70%, applies with full force here.

The technology landscape for agentic enterprise systems is converging around a set of emerging standards that will determine interoperability and competitive positioning for years ahead. The most significant is the Model Context Protocol, a universal standard for connecting AI agents to enterprise tools, data sources, and external systems. Introduced in late 2024 and adopted within months by all major foundation model providers, it was subsequently contributed to an open-source foundation under shared industry governance.

Its significance is architectural: it defines how agents communicate with the systems they need to access in a standardised, interoperable way, replacing dozens of proprietary integrations with a single common protocol. The shorthand that captures its role is "the USB-C for AI": a universal connector that makes interoperability a baseline rather than a project.

The complementary standard is Agent-to-Agent protocol, which handles communication between agents within multi-agent systems. Together, these two protocols form the communication infrastructure on which enterprise agentic architectures run.

The building block of capability within this architecture is a skill.

> **Definition: A skill (reusable AI workflow)**
>
> In the context of agentic systems, a skill is a reusable AI workflow that packages a specific capability into a form that can be invoked, shared, and built upon across the organisation. Skills are the unit of innovation in the Agentic Organisation: when an employee builds a skill that solves a problem in their domain and shares it, that skill becomes organisational capability rather than individual productivity. The compounding effect of skill creation and sharing is one of the primary mechanisms by which the Agentic Organisation outperforms organisations where AI adoption remains individual and uncoordinated.

The leadership task in the Technology pillar is not to achieve technical expertise in any of these standards. It is to ensure that architectural decisions about interoperability, governance infrastructure, and how skills are built, managed, and shared are made deliberately rather than by default. These decisions have consequences that outlast any individual tool purchase.

**Data**

The Data pillar is the most frequently underestimated of the five and the most consequential for long-term competitive positioning.

The readiness gap is significant. Fewer than one in five organisations have high maturity in any aspect of data readiness. More than half estimate their data is not ready for AI deployment at the level they are planning. Only around one in eight has data that meets the requirements for advanced agentic applications. These are not technology shortfalls. They are governance and investment failures accumulated over years of treating data as an operational necessity rather than a strategic asset.

The competitive implication is direct. When AI foundation models become broadly available and their underlying capabilities increasingly commoditised, the organisations with proprietary, well-governed, domain-specific data have a structural advantage that is difficult to close. McKinsey's framing of curated proprietary datasets as "walled data gardens" captures this: the organisation that invests now in building data quality, governance, and domain specificity is building a moat that late entrants cannot close simply by deploying a more powerful model.

For European organisations, the Data pillar carries regulatory obligations that are unique globally. The interplay between AI deployment requirements and data protection law creates specific design constraints that require attention from the start of any transformation programme. These constraints, explored fully in Chapter 6, are not simply compliance burdens. Handled with governance discipline, they produce data practices that build rather than erode the customer trust that European organisations are well-positioned to leverage as a competitive advantage.

## Governance Implications

The AI Transformation Framework is not only a model for describing the destination. It is the structure through which governance of the transformation becomes tractable.

Without a framework like this, boards typically receive technology project updates: model performance metrics, pilot results, cost variances. These are activity data, not governance data. They tell the board whether individual projects are proceeding as planned, not whether the organisation is transforming as intended.

The framework changes the question from "how are the AI projects performing?" to "where does the organisation stand on each of the five pillars, and is the rate of progress on each sufficient?"

The People pillar provides the starkest illustration of the governance gap. Only 21% of organisations have rigorous governance mechanisms in place for agentic AI deployment, even as the majority already have agents running in production. Research projections suggest that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will fail or be cancelled within the next two years, with governance failures as a primary cause.

This is not primarily a technology governance failure. It is a People and Process governance failure: organisations deploying agentic capability without defined answers to who supervises what, what boundaries agents operate within, and how the organisation learns from agent errors.

The governance design for the agentic workforce rests on a principle called tiered autonomy: agents operate within risk-adjusted boundaries, their autonomy expands as their reliability is established, and human oversight is positioned at the decision points where it matters most. This requires comprehensive logging of agent actions, circuit breakers that can halt agent activity when boundaries are breached, and systematic pre-deployment assessment of data access, system permissions, and risk classification.

For European organisations, each of the five pillars has direct EU AI Act implications. The Product pillar requires compliance with the Act's risk classification system: AI-native products in high-risk categories face transparency, accuracy, and human oversight requirements that must be designed in from the start, not retrofitted. The People pillar connects to the Act's AI literacy requirements, already in force since early 2025, creating an obligation to develop workforce AI capability that is regulatory requirement, not merely aspiration.

The Process pillar's governance must account for fundamental rights impact assessments and transparency obligations in automated decision-making. The Technology pillar must ensure traceability of AI system decisions and governance of prohibited practices. The Data pillar operates at the intersection of the EU AI Act's data requirements and the existing GDPR framework, creating design constraints that determine how data can be collected, retained, and used in agentic systems.

The board's practical task is to use the framework as a structured review mechanism. At each board meeting where AI is on the agenda, the question should be addressed across all five pillars: not "are our AI projects on track?" but "where are we on each pillar, what is the trajectory, and is the governance appropriate to the organisation's current level of agentic capability?" This requires board members sufficiently fluent in AI to ask specific questions and assess specific answers, not to receive briefings and express confidence in management.

The organisations that govern transformation well are those where the board and leadership team share a common model of what they are building. The AI Transformation Framework provides that common model. Without it, every stakeholder assesses progress against a different implicit picture of the destination.

## Closing

The AI Transformation Framework provides the picture of what the Agentic Organisation actually looks like across five essential dimensions. But a picture is not a route.

The next question is how transformation scales through an organisation that is already operating: with existing processes, existing structures, and existing measures of success. The answer is not to launch everywhere at once, and it is not to wait until every pillar is ready before beginning.

Transformation progresses through four distinct tiers: Individual, Team, Department, and Organisation. Each tier has different governance demands, different leadership requirements, and different measures of success. The most common structural mistake in AI programmes is skipping a tier, or assuming that results at one level automatically produce results at the next.

Chapter 4 introduces The Four Tiers of Transformation and examines what leaders must do differently at each stage.

## Case Study: Klarna — When Automation Meets Its Limits

### Strategic Dilemma

In 2023, Klarna's leadership confronted a dilemma that every organisation pursuing serious AI transformation will eventually face: how far to push automation before the model breaks down.

The Swedish fintech, operating in over 45 markets and processing millions of customer interactions each month, deployed an AI customer service assistant under direct CEO leadership. The initial results were extraordinary. Within weeks, the system was handling a volume of conversations equivalent to 700 full-time customer service agents. Resolution time fell from eleven minutes to under two. The projected annual profit improvement reached $40 million.

Then Klarna partially reversed course. Human agents, whose numbers had been substantially reduced as AI deployment scaled, were re-hired. The AI assistant had encountered something the efficiency metrics had not measured: customers who needed something other than speed.

### Decision Context

Klarna's CEO had made AI a visible, public strategic priority before this deployment. The decision to deploy the AI assistant at the scale and pace Klarna did was not cautious or experimental. It was driven by conviction that AI could handle the majority of customer service interactions without material loss of quality, and by performance data, in the first weeks of deployment, that appeared to support that conviction.

The efficiency gains were real. Resolution times of under two minutes represented a step-change from the industry norm, not incremental improvement. By the metrics the team had identified as the measures of success, the deployment was working.

What those metrics had not captured was the nature of the interactions being processed. Customer service for a consumer credit company involves a disproportionate volume of emotionally charged interactions: payment difficulties, billing disputes, confusion about charges, stress about financial commitments. These are not primarily requests for information to be answered quickly. They are requests for understanding, and often for flexibility, that benefit from human judgment in ways that efficiency metrics do not reflect.

Research on customer preferences makes the underlying dynamic consistent. When facing complex, stressful, or emotionally significant service interactions, the large majority of customers prioritise empathy over speed. Speed, in those moments, is not the relevant quality dimension. Klarna had optimised decisively for the wrong metric in the category of interactions where that metric did not predict satisfaction.

### Trade-offs

The decision to scale aggressively and then partially reverse course involved real costs in both directions.

- The efficiency gains from full AI deployment were genuine and substantial. Returning some volume to human agents sacrificed a portion of the projected $40 million in annual savings.
- Re-hiring human agents after a period of headcount reduction created internal complexity and sent a mixed signal to employees uncertain about the role of AI in their work.
- Acknowledging that the initial model required recalibration meant publicly correcting a programme that had been presented as a success. This is not a comfortable leadership position for any organisation in the public eye.
- The governance infrastructure to distinguish which interaction types should be handled by agents and which by humans had not been designed before deployment. It had to be built reactively, under the pressure of having already scaled.

### Outcome

The model Klarna operates today is more sophisticated than either the fully automated or fully human alternative. AI handles the high-volume, lower-complexity interactions where speed and accuracy are the most relevant quality dimensions. Human agents handle interactions that require empathy, judgment about exceptional circumstances, and relationship continuity.

The AI assistant continues to handle millions of conversations monthly. Customer satisfaction metrics, not only resolution rate and speed, now inform the governance of which interactions AI handles. The financial efficiency has been partially preserved whilst the quality failures of the initial model have been addressed.

Siemiatkowski's summary of the learning is precise: "AI gives us speed. Talent gives us empathy." That is not a retreat from AI deployment. It is a more accurate model of where AI creates value and where human capability remains the relevant input.

### Transferable Lesson

The AI Transformation Framework provides a useful lens on Klarna's experience. The initial deployment was strong on Technology and well-connected to Product: the AI assistant fundamentally changed what the customer service product offered. What was not designed alongside it was the People and Process architecture. The governance of who handles which interaction type, under what circumstances, and measured by what outcomes, was absent at the point of deployment.

The lesson for European leadership teams is specific: the Agentic Organisation is not defined by the maximum autonomy it can deploy. It is defined by the deliberate allocation of work between humans and agents based on a clear understanding of where each creates the most value for the outcomes that matter. That allocation requires explicit design across all five pillars, not optimisation of Technology and Product in isolation.

Klarna is not a cautionary tale against ambition. It is a demonstration of what happens when the definition of success is narrower than the full experience the organisation creates for its customers, and when the governance architecture is not designed alongside the technology deployment.
