# Chapter 1: The Largest Shift Since the Industrial Revolution

## Opening Hook

Ninety-five per cent of enterprise AI implementations fail to deliver sustained, measurable impact. That figure comes from the MIT NANDA Report (2025), and it is corroborated by BCG, RAND, and S&P Global data pointing in the same direction. Yet nearly eight in ten companies now report using generative AI in at least one business function.

Read those two facts together. Widespread adoption. Near-universal failure to capture value. This is not a technology problem. It is a recognition problem.

Most leadership teams have misidentified what kind of change this is. They are treating a paradigm shift as a software rollout: scoping pilots, funding proofs of concept, delegating to IT. The technology works. The organisational response does not.

This book argues that European CEOs are dangerously underprepared for what is coming. Not because they lack ambition or intelligence, but because the mental model they are applying (incremental, project-based, technology-first) is structurally incapable of capturing what this moment demands.

The good news: recognising the nature of the shift is the first and most consequential decision a CEO can make. This chapter lays out the evidence for why that recognition matters now.

## The Problem

Three forces are converging to make this moment structurally different from every previous technology wave.

### The speed of capability growth

AI task-completion ability has doubled every seven months since 2019. Since 2024, that rate has accelerated to every four months. By 2027, AI systems could potentially complete four days of autonomous work without supervision (McKinsey, 2025).

This is not a linear upgrade cycle. It is exponential. The gap between what AI could do twelve months ago and what it can do today is larger than most executive teams realise; and the gap twelve months from now will be larger still.

McKinsey envisions a fundamental recomposition of the organisation chart: "One manager with a small human team could orchestrate hundreds of agents acting autonomously in a workflow cutting across silos, running 24/7." That is not a marginal productivity improvement. It is a different kind of organisation.

> **Definition: AI Agent**
>
> An AI agent is a software system that can perceive its environment, make decisions, and take actions autonomously to achieve a specified goal. Unlike traditional software that follows fixed rules, or generative AI that responds to individual prompts, an agent can plan multi-step processes, use tools, and adapt its approach based on results without requiring human instruction at each step.

> **Definition: The Agentic Enterprise**
>
> An agentic enterprise is an organisation redesigned around human-AI collaboration. Humans and AI agents work side by side: agents handle complex, multi-step tasks autonomously whilst humans set direction, exercise judgment, and govern outcomes. It is not an organisation that uses AI tools. It is an organisation built to work differently because AI agents are part of the workforce.

BCG's analysis reinforces the trajectory: the combination of increased compute, improved model efficiency, and the progressive removal of capability constraints could deliver 10,000 times more AI power within three years, putting models at PhD-level expertise across every domain. As one BCG leader stated: "By 2030, you will have far more AI agents in your business than you have people."

Jamie Dimon's assessment of the shift is two words: "This is real." Tobi Lütke, CEO of Shopify, is more pointed: "If you're not climbing, you're sliding." Both reflect a growing consensus among leaders who have engaged deeply with the technology: the pace of change leaves no room for a wait-and-see posture.

### The micro-productivity trap

Bain & Company coined the term "micro-productivity trap" to describe what happens when AI adoption is left to grassroots experimentation. Teams adopt tools. Individual productivity rises. But the gains never compound into enterprise-wide impact.

> **Definition: The Micro-Productivity Trap**
>
> A term coined by Bain & Company. The micro-productivity trap occurs when individual employees and teams adopt AI tools and see genuine productivity gains, but those gains remain isolated. Without enterprise-wide coordination and leadership direction, grassroots AI adoption produces scattered, localised improvements that never compound into organisational impact.

The pattern is pervasive. McKinsey's CEO Playbook (June 2025) reports that approximately 90 per cent of transformative use cases remain stuck in pilot mode. S&P Global found that 42 per cent of companies scrapped most of their AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17 per cent the prior year. Gartner projects that over 40 per cent of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by the end of 2027 due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls.

> **Definition: Agentic AI**
>
> Agentic AI refers to AI systems that act autonomously to achieve goals, as distinct from generative AI that produces content in response to prompts. Where generative AI answers questions, agentic AI executes workflows: it can reason through a problem, break it into steps, use tools, make decisions, and learn from the results. The shift from generative to agentic is the shift from AI as assistant to AI as autonomous colleague.

Bain's assessment is direct: "Grassroots experimentation with AI tools sparks innovation and cultural momentum (all good) but it does not self-organize into enterprise-wide impact. Without clear direction from the top, these efforts remain fragmented, siloed, and ultimately shallow."

### The governance vacuum

The third force is the least visible and the most dangerous. Sixty-six per cent of board members report limited, minimal, or no AI knowledge (research synthesis, Section 9). Only 15 per cent of boards receive AI-related metrics. Fewer than 25 per cent have board-approved AI policies.

Yet 88 per cent of organisations use AI in at least one business function. McKinsey's "Superagency" report identifies the core paradox: the biggest barrier to scaling AI is not employees (who are already using AI three times more than their leaders realise) but leaders themselves, who are not steering fast enough. The gap between adoption velocity and governance maturity is the single greatest enterprise risk in the AI era.

Eric Kutcher, McKinsey's North America Chair, frames the scale plainly: "This is probably the biggest, most complex business transformation, but it's 80% business transformation and 20% tech transformation. That's different from how most people have thought about it."

## The Paradigm Shift Curve

Most organisations believe they are still in a safe phase: early enough to experiment gradually, watch what competitors do, run a few more pilots. The data says otherwise.

The **Paradigm Shift Curve** is a three-phase model that describes how technology-driven paradigm shifts move from early experimentation to structural separation between leaders and laggards.

<!-- DIAGRAM PLACEHOLDER: ch01-paradigm-shift-curve: GenerateImage unavailable -->

**Phase 1: Convergence.** Evidence accumulates. Early movers experiment. Most organisations are aware of the shift and begin exploring. The costs of delay are low and largely invisible. This is where the majority of European enterprises spent 2023 and 2024.

**Phase 2: Divergence.** Leaders begin compounding advantage whilst laggards stall in pilot purgatory. The gap accelerates because AI-driven organisations learn faster, decide faster, and redeploy resources at a pace that non-AI organisations cannot match. BCG data shows that AI-leading companies already generate twice the ROI on AI projects and 1.5 to 2 times the total shareholder return compared to the majority still in early stages.

> **Definition: Pilot Purgatory**
>
> Pilot purgatory is the state in which an organisation runs numerous AI proofs of concept and small-scale experiments, but none progress to full-scale production deployment. The pilots generate encouraging results, justify further experimentation, and consume resources, yet the organisation never makes the structural changes required to scale them.

**Phase 3: Separation.** The structural gap becomes insurmountable. Leaders have redesigned operating models, decision architectures, and workforce structures around human-AI collaboration. Laggards are still running pilots. The competitive distance is no longer a matter of investment; it is a matter of organisational capability that cannot be purchased or replicated quickly.

The critical insight: the data strongly suggests we are now at the inflection point between Phase 1 and Phase 2. The window for gradual experimentation is closing.

For European CEOs, the picture carries an additional urgency. EU enterprise AI adoption reached 19.95 per cent in 2025, up from 13.5 per cent in 2024, a dramatic acceleration. Yet the investment gap is stark: the US invested $109 billion in AI in 2024, roughly 24 times the UK figure. Europe produces only three of the world's top AI models versus 40 in the US.

More than 60 per cent of European firms remain at the earliest stages of AI maturity. Norway ranks first in Europe for AI adoption at 46.4 per cent, yet only 6 per cent of Nordic organisations qualify as "AI Achievers". Adoption rate does not equal transformation.

Consider the evidence already accumulating among leaders. DBS Bank's AI economic value grew from S$180 million in 2022 to S$750 million in 2024, with a projected S$1 billion in 2025. Share price tripled. Return on equity doubled from 8.4 to 18 per cent.

Schneider Electric reports over 100 million euros in generated value from AI, targeting 2.0 to 2.5 billion euros in cumulative industrial productivity gains between 2026 and 2030. Allianz's Project Nemo achieved an 80 per cent reduction in claim processing time using a seven-agent workflow executing in under five minutes.

These are not projections. They are realised results from organisations that moved early and moved with executive commitment.

Mark Bonchek captures the cognitive gap precisely: "Until we make an exponential shift in our mental model from spreadsheets to algorithms, AI tech will only give us an incrementally better spreadsheet." Organisations are putting AI on top of existing processes (horseless carriage thinking) when the shift demands redesigning how decisions are made, how work flows, and how humans and machines collaborate.

McKinsey's framing makes the required shift explicit. The CEO must ask not "How do we add AI?" but "How do we want decisions to be made, work to flow, and humans to engage in an environment where software can act?"

## Case Study: DBS Bank: Benchmarking Against the Wrong Competitors

### 1. Strategic Dilemma

In 2014, Piyush Gupta, CEO of DBS Bank in Singapore, posed a question to his leadership team that was either audacious or reckless depending on which side of the table you sat on: what if the bank stopped benchmarking itself against other banks? DBS had earned a reputation that its own staff grimly acknowledged: "Damn Bloody Slow." It was a legacy institution in a city-state that prided itself on speed. The choice Gupta forced was stark. Path one: continue the standard transformation playbook: modernise the core, digitise customer touchpoints, measure progress against peer banks. Safe, legible, and defensible to a board and regulators. Path two: declare that the real competition was no longer OCBC or Standard Chartered, but Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, and Facebook, and reorganise the entire institution to close a gap that most of the organisation could not yet see.

### 2. Decision Context

The external environment in 2014 made the dilemma credible but not obvious. Fintech disruption was beginning to reshape consumer expectations in Asia, but incumbents still held the regulatory moat, the balance sheets, and the customer trust. There was no burning platform in the conventional sense: DBS was not in crisis. It was, however, operating in a region where digital adoption was accelerating faster than in most Western markets, and where new entrant banks were beginning to acquire customers with frictionless mobile experiences that legacy institutions simply could not match.

Inside the organisation, the constraints were what you would expect of a bank that had grown through acquisitions across multiple Asian markets. Technology infrastructure was fragmented. Culture rewarded caution. Governance structures were designed for stability, not speed. Gupta had been in the CEO role since 2009 and had already overseen a significant technology investment programme, but the results were incremental, and the gap to the digital-native players was not closing.

The decisive context shift came from Gupta's reframing of the competitive category itself. He introduced the GANDALF framework (an acronym built from Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple, LinkedIn, and Facebook), not as a benchmarking exercise but as a cognitive forcing function. The question was no longer "are we better than the bank across the street?" It was "could a technology company replicate what we do, and if so, how fast?" That question made the urgency real in a way that peer benchmarking never could.

### 3. Trade-offs

Choosing to benchmark against technology giants rather than peer banks required DBS to sacrifice several things that a more conservative path would have preserved:

- **Competitive comfort.** Against peer banks, DBS was performing well. The GANDALF framing meant accepting that the organisation was, by its own definition, deeply behind, which required communicating that uncomfortable truth internally and externally.
- **Speed of governance.** Moving at the pace of a technology company meant compressing approval cycles, accepting higher levels of experimentation risk, and tolerating a failure rate that traditional banking governance was not designed to accommodate.
- **Organisational familiarity.** The transformation required embedding technologists into business units, restructuring how product teams operated, and introducing engineering disciplines (agile, DevOps, continuous deployment) that were foreign to a banking culture built on change control.
- **Short-term efficiency.** Significant capital was allocated to technology infrastructure and talent before the return was visible. For several years, the investment thesis required board-level patience that not every institution would extend.
- **Clarity about the endgame.** Benchmarking against GANDALF companies meant the destination was genuinely unknown: there was no established playbook for a bank that operated like a technology company whilst remaining a regulated financial institution.

### 4. Outcome

The data is now public and unambiguous. AI economic value at DBS grew from S$180 million in 2022 to S$750 million in 2024, with S$1 billion projected for 2025. DBS is one of only two banks globally (alongside JPMorgan Chase) to have publicly disclosed total realised AI value. The share price tripled over the transformation period. Return on equity doubled from 8.4 per cent to 18 per cent. DBS has been named the world's best bank by multiple independent rankings.

The outcome is not uniformly clean. The transformation took a decade of sustained CEO commitment, through multiple budget cycles, through periods where the thesis was contested internally, and through a governance and regulatory environment that required constant negotiation. Gupta's position that this was fundamentally an organisational transformation (not a technology project) meant that the hardest work was cultural and structural, not technical. The technology investment mattered; the mindset shift mattered more.

What the data cannot fully capture is the compounding nature of the advantage. DBS did not simply close a gap on its competitors; it created a new category of distance that most regional peers are still not positioned to close.

### 5. Transferable Lesson

The DBS transformation maps precisely onto the inflection point described in the Paradigm Shift Curve. Gupta's decisive move was not a technology decision; it was a cognitive one. He recognised, earlier than most, that the relevant competitive threat was not inside his existing category. Once that recognition was in place, the strategic and operational decisions followed with a coherence that piecemeal transformation cannot produce.

The lesson for a European CEO is a decision rule, not an inspiration: *before you set your AI ambition, define which curve you want to be on*. If you benchmark your AI progress against sector peers, you will optimise to remain in the pack. The Paradigm Shift Curve's Divergence phase does not reward keeping pace with the pack; it rewards leaving it. The question Gupta asked in 2014 (who are we actually competing with, and are we measuring the right thing?) is the question this book is asking you to answer before your window closes.

## Governance Implications

The board is the weakest link in the AI transformation chain, and most boards do not know it.

Sixty-six per cent of board members report limited-to-no AI knowledge. Only 14 per cent of boards discuss AI at every meeting. Fewer than 25 per cent have a board-approved AI policy. Meanwhile, 88 per cent of their organisations are already using AI in at least one function.

This is not an oversight problem. It is a strategic risk. Without board-level understanding of what the agentic enterprise requires, no transformation can be sustained past the first budget cycle. The board sets investment horizons, approves operating model changes, and oversees risk. If directors cannot evaluate AI strategy, they cannot govern it.

The reframe required is this: AI governance is not compliance. It is competitive positioning. Boards that treat AI as a technology risk to be managed will find themselves governing organisations that fall behind. Boards that treat AI as an organisational transformation to be led will find themselves governing the companies that pull ahead.

The data on executive engagement is unambiguous. Organisations with strong executive sponsorship achieve AI success rates six times higher than those where leadership delegates to IT or middle management. BCG's AI Radar finds that C-level executives deeply engaged with AI are 12 times more likely to be among the top 5 per cent of companies winning with AI. Half of all CEOs now believe their job stability depends on getting AI right.

Christoph Schweizer, BCG's CEO, underscores the gap between intent and execution: "While 75% of executives rank AI as a top three strategic priority, only a quarter report meaningful value." The distance between ranking something as a priority and extracting value from it is precisely the governance gap this book addresses.

The European context adds a further dimension. The EU AI Act's prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI applications took effect on 2 February 2025. Full requirements for high-risk systems arrive by August 2026. Penalties reach 35 million euros or 7 per cent of global annual turnover. European CEOs face a regulatory forcing function that compels the structured, board-led approach this book argues for: regulation is not the obstacle, but the clarifying constraint.

## Executive Reflection Questions

1. If AI task capability doubles every four months, what does your organisation look like in 18 months if you continue at the current pace of change?

2. Are you treating AI as an IT project, a departmental tool, or a fundamental redesign of how your organisation operates, and which of those does the evidence support?

3. When did your board last receive a substantive briefing on AI strategy (not a vendor demo, but a discussion) of operating model implications?

4. Can you name the three largest AI-driven competitive threats to your business in the next two years?

5. Would your leadership team describe your current AI efforts as "experimenting" or "transforming", and do you know which one the data says you are actually doing?
