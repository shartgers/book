# Chapter 10 — What the Leaders Did

## Strategic Tension

European CEOs have no shortage of AI case studies to read. What they lack is usable lessons. Most case studies describe what happened at a single company; few extract what transfers. A CEO running a mid-sized insurer in Munich gains little from knowing that Klarna handled 2.3 million conversations with AI — unless the lesson explains what Klarna learned when that strategy broke, and what the insurer should do differently as a result. The tension: the evidence base is rich (BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Klarna, Schneider Electric have each published detailed outcomes), but the gap between "impressive case study" and "transferable action" is where most readers — and most transformation programmes — stall. This chapter closes that gap by abstracting nine lessons that cut across sectors, geographies, and company sizes — each one a decision a CEO can act on.

## Core Principle

Nine transferable lessons — drawn from what European leaders actually did, not what consultants recommended — provide a practical decision checklist for any CEO building an Agentic Organisation, regardless of sector or starting point.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Opening Hook
Section heading: The Decisions That Mattered

Open with the chapter hook: "The most instructive moments in any transformation are not the launches. They are the decisions made under pressure, with incomplete information, when the easy path would have been to wait." Set the scene: six European companies — a Spanish bank, a Dutch bank, a German industrial giant, a German insurer, a Swedish fintech, a French industrial conglomerate — each made distinct strategic bets on AI between 2022 and 2025. Some scaled to 120,000 employees. Some built agentic workflows in under 100 days. One went too far and publicly reversed course. Their industries, cultures, and starting points differ. But the lessons that emerge — nine of them — are remarkably consistent. This chapter is lesson-led, not case-study-led: each lesson is a transferable takeaway; the case studies are referenced where they best illustrate the point. The reader should walk away not with admiration for any single company, but with a decision checklist they can apply to their own organisation.

### Beat 2 — The Problem
Section heading: Why Case Studies Fail to Transfer

Why do executives read case studies and still fail to act? Three reasons. First, case studies describe company-specific contexts that do not transfer directly: BBVA's relationship with OpenAI, Siemens' Nanjing factory, Klarna's fintech culture are each unique. Second, case studies tend to celebrate outcomes without exposing the dilemmas — the moments where leaders chose one path over another, often under pressure. Third, the industry fixates on "best practices" when what CEOs need is "best decisions" — the choices that can be abstracted from context and applied elsewhere. The research on these six companies reveals that the transferable lessons are not in what they built, but in how they decided: to govern before scaling, to embed expertise where value is created, to bring humans back when automation went too far, to reinvest savings into people. These are decisions any CEO can make. This chapter presents nine of them — each with the evidence that supports it and the cases that illustrate it.

### Beat 7 — Nine Lessons
Section heading: Nine Lessons from Europe's AI Leaders

This is the core of the chapter. Present nine lessons, each as a short section (~300 words) with: (a) the lesson statement, (b) the case evidence, (c) a leadership quote or data point. The lessons flow from strategic (CEO ownership, governance) through operational (central engine, measurement, pilot-to-production) to cultural (hybrid model, compliance, speed, people reinvestment). Each lesson should feel like a direct recommendation to the reader.

**Lesson 1: CEO Ownership Must Be Visible and Sustained**
The CEO and board must personally champion AI transformation — not just approve the budget but visibly use the tools and hold the agenda. Cases: BBVA (Chairman Torres Vila and CEO Genç among 250 senior leaders trained on ChatGPT Enterprise; board-level decision from the start), ING (CEO sits on AI Ethics Committee), Klarna (Siemiatkowski drove from the top — both the push and the public correction), Schneider Electric (Chief AI Officer reporting to executive leadership). Torres Vila: "We were pioneers in the digital and mobile transformation, and we are now entering the AI era with even greater ambition." Connect to Ch 2 (Leadership Is Not Optional): the 6x success rate for CEO-led transformation applies specifically here.

**Lesson 2: Govern First, Experiment Second**
The companies that scale fastest build governance, risk controls, and compliance into the foundation before they launch. Governance is an accelerator, not a brake. Cases: ING ("conservatively aggressive" — centralised governance, standardised platforms, real-time monitoring → 90% pilot-to-production; van Stiphout: "Without this governance... we might have seen bits of AI all over the place"), Allianz (EU AI Pact signatory; Anthropic partnership for auditable AI; 12,000 employees upskilled within a governance framework), BBVA (security, legal, compliance aligned from day one), Schneider Electric (Trust Charter governs all AI). Contrast with the 30% industry pilot-to-production average. ING's governance didn't slow them — it is why they are 3x faster.

**Lesson 3: Build a Central AI Engine, Then Embed Expertise Where Value Is Created**
Centralised capability sets standards and infrastructure; embedded specialists ensure AI solves real business problems. The hub-and-spoke model appears in every successful case. Cases: BBVA (AI Factory ~400 professionals centrally + ~300 "AI wizards" embedded in business units; now 1,000+ data scientists), ING (centralised platform across five strategic domains, deployed through local hubs), Siemens (Industrial Copilot developed centrally with NVIDIA/Microsoft/Accenture, deployed to individual factories), Allianz (central governance + Project Nemo developed locally in Australia, scaling to other markets).

**Lesson 4: Measure What the Board Cares About**
Leaders do not measure AI activity. They measure business outcomes: time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced, quality improved. Every initiative has a business KPI before it launches. Cases: BBVA (3 hrs/week saved, 83% usage, 87% query time reduction), ING (25% productivity gains, 90% pilot-to-production), Siemens (200% capacity increase, 78% lead-time cut, 46% fewer field failures), Allianz (80% claims processing reduction), Klarna ($1.1M revenue per employee), Schneider (€100M+ value, 15% yield improvement). Allianz's Diviach: "a disciplined approach to prioritising innovative AI projects, guided by the core principle of value creation."

**Lesson 5: Close the Pilot-to-Production Gap**
The industry average: 30% of pilots reach production. The leaders: 90%. The difference is not better technology — it is organisational readiness: clear go/no-go criteria, centralised platforms, pre-aligned governance, and executive accountability. Cases: ING (90% — the benchmark; centralised platform + five defined domains), BBVA (phased 3,000 → 11,000 → 120,000 with clear success criteria at each stage), Allianz (Project Nemo built in under 100 days, immediately designated for expansion), Siemens (Nanjing is a WEF Lighthouse, not a pilot; fifth Siemens site to achieve Lighthouse status). ING's 90% vs. the industry's 30% is the single most powerful proof point: the bottleneck is governance and readiness, not technology.

**Lesson 6: The Hybrid Human–AI Model Is the Endgame**
Full automation is not the goal. The endgame is humans and AI working together: AI handles volume, speed, and routine; humans handle complexity, empathy, and judgment. Cases: Klarna (the defining arc — 2.3M conversations/month, ~700 agents replaced; then public reversal: "cost unfortunately seems to have been a too predominant evaluation factor"; now human support as "VIP treatment"; 86% of customers prefer empathy over speed for complex issues), Allianz (Project Nemo: 7 AI agents but humans in the final decision loop — designed hybrid from the start), ING ("human in the loop" as standard), Siemens (Copilot assists engineers, does not replace them). Siemiatkowski: "AI gives us speed. Talent gives us empathy." The companies that got hybrid right from the start avoided costly reversals.

**Lesson 7: Turn Regulatory Compliance Into Competitive Moat**
European leaders treat EU AI Act compliance as capability-building — governance infrastructure, trust, auditability, and data quality that become competitive advantages. Cases: Allianz (EU AI Pact; Anthropic partnership logging every decision; European insurers hold 47% of global responsible AI specialists), ING (governance-first enables 90% production rate), BBVA (co-developed NeurIPS bias stress test for non-English GenAI models — compliance-driven research producing genuine innovation), Schneider Electric (Trust Charter + frugal AI aligned with sustainability values). Accenture's finding: "AI Achievers" — the 11% of European firms with 50% greater revenue growth — designed AI responsibly from the start, not as an afterthought.

**Lesson 8: Move Fast — Build in Weeks, Deploy in Months**
Governance does not mean slow. Standardised platforms, centralised tooling, clear decision criteria, and pre-aligned compliance mean teams move fast within clear boundaries. Cases: Allianz (Project Nemo: concept to live claims processing in under 100 days), BBVA (OpenAI partnership to full-scale 120,000 deployment within the same year), ING (customer due diligence from weeks to seconds), Siemens (Nanjing: lead times cut 78%; time-to-market from 45 days to 10). Project Nemo's 100-day arc directly counters the perception that European companies are slow or that governance slows deployment.

**Lesson 9: Reinvest AI Savings Into Your People, Not Just Your P&L**
Leaders reinvest AI-driven efficiency gains into the workforce — higher compensation, upskilling, new roles, career development. This creates a virtuous cycle: employees embrace AI because it visibly benefits them, accelerating adoption and generating more value. Cases: Klarna (60% pay increase, $126K → $203K; revenue per employee to $1.1M; tech share of workforce 36% → 52%), BBVA (surpassed 1,000 data scientists; 20,000+ custom GPTs created by employees), ING (5,000 trained on data fluency; 30–50 AI interactions per employee per week), Allianz (12,000 employees completed AI upskilling in 2024). Klarna's reversal validates the lesson: the companies that invested in people alongside AI avoided the correction Klarna had to make publicly.

### Beat 4 — Governance Implications
Section heading: What the Nine Lessons Mean for the Board

Step back from the individual lessons and surface the governance pattern that runs through all nine. Three board-level implications: (1) CEO accountability is non-delegable — the board must hold the CEO to visible AI ownership, not just budget approval (Lessons 1, 2); (2) governance is a value driver, not a cost centre — the board should track pilot-to-production rate and compliance-as-capability metrics alongside financial KPIs (Lessons 2, 5, 7); (3) the workforce contract must evolve — if AI savings flow only to the P&L and not back to employees, adoption stalls and trust erodes (Lessons 6, 9). For European boards specifically: the EU AI Act creates a governance forcing function, but the leaders treated it as an opportunity before regulators required it. The board question is not "are we compliant?" but "are we converting compliance into competitive advantage?"

### Beat 5 — Closing / Transition
Section heading: From Lessons to Playbook

The nine lessons describe what the leaders did. The next question is: in what order should you do it? The answer is not "all at once." Chapter 11 presents the playbook — a phased approach from Foundation through Activation to Scale — designed for the specific constraints and capabilities of a European organisation. The lessons from this chapter map directly onto that sequence: governance and CEO ownership (Lessons 1–2) are Foundation; central engine and measurement (Lessons 3–5) are Activation; hybrid model, compliance moat, speed, and people reinvestment (Lessons 6–9) are Scale. The leaders did not do all nine at once. They sequenced them. The playbook shows how.

### Beat 6 — Executive Reflection Questions
Section heading: Questions for the Board — Lessons from the Leaders

1. For each of the nine lessons, can you identify whether your organisation is ahead, on track, or behind? Which lesson represents your largest gap?
2. Is your CEO personally and visibly championing AI transformation — training alongside employees, reviewing use cases, holding the agenda — or has ownership been delegated to IT, a project team, or a consultant?
3. Do you have a central AI capability (factory, hub, or CoE) that sets standards and governance, and embedded specialists in business units who connect AI to business value? Or is AI dispersed without coordination?
4. What is your pilot-to-production rate? If you do not know, what does that tell you about your measurement discipline?
5. If you stopped all AI-related hiring and investment tomorrow, would your existing employees have the skills, compensation, and career incentives to sustain the transformation — or would adoption collapse?

## Thesis Check

This chapter advances the central argument by showing European CEOs — through the actions of their peers — that AI transformation is not a technology problem but a leadership, governance, and people problem. The nine lessons are transferable precisely because they are about decisions, not tools. The leaders who succeeded did not have better AI; they had better governance, clearer accountability, faster execution within guardrails, and a commitment to investing in their people. If European CEOs are dangerously underprepared, these nine lessons are the shortest path to preparedness — connecting the European advantage (Ch 9) to the execution playbook (Ch 11).
