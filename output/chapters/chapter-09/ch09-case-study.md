## Case Study: ING — When Governance Becomes the Accelerator

### Strategic Dilemma

ING, one of Europe's largest banks, faced a choice that confronts every financial institution deploying AI at scale. The bank could allow its business units to experiment freely, accepting fragmentation in exchange for speed. Or it could centralise AI governance under a single operating structure, accepting short-term friction in exchange for control and scalability. Neither path was cost-free. Decentralised experimentation risked producing, as COO Marnix van Stiphout put it, "bits of AI all over the place": duplicated effort, compliance blind spots, and no way to scale what worked. Centralised governance risked bureaucracy, slower time-to-market, and frustrating the teams closest to business problems.

### Decision Context

Banking is amongst the most heavily regulated sectors in Europe. ING operates under the EU AI Act, DORA (mandatory since January 2025), ECB supervisory oversight, and national financial conduct regulations across multiple jurisdictions. Credit scoring and customer risk assessment are classified as high-risk under the AI Act's Annex III. ECB Supervisory Board Chair Claudia Buch has been explicit: banks must prove they do not "blindly follow AI systems' recommendations".

ING had already built significant analytical capability. A 500-strong analytics team was working across the bank. AI was not new to the organisation. The question was not whether to use it, but how to govern its deployment as use cases multiplied across lending, fraud detection, customer service, and compliance.

The industry context sharpened the stakes. Most financial institutions struggle to move AI from experimentation to production. Roughly 30% of AI pilot projects reach production deployment across the industry. The gap between proof-of-concept and operational value is where most AI investment dies.

### Trade-offs

ING chose centralisation: what the bank's leadership calls a "conservatively aggressive" approach. All AI development was consolidated into five domains under COO oversight. An AI Ethics Committee was established with the CEO as a member. Every use case is reviewed before deployment. The trade-offs were real:

- **Slower initial autonomy for business units.** Teams could not launch AI initiatives independently. Every project required central review and domain allocation, adding process where speed was valued.
- **Concentration risk in leadership.** Placing all AI under the COO created a single point of dependency. If governance became a bottleneck, the entire pipeline would stall.
- **Cultural friction.** A 500-person analytics community accustomed to working across the bank had to adapt to a more structured operating model. Not every analyst welcomed the change.
- **Upfront investment in governance infrastructure.** Building the ethics committee, review processes, and domain structure required time and resources before producing visible returns.

### Outcome

The results challenge the assumption that governance slows AI down. ING's pilot-to-production rate reached 90%, three times the industry average of roughly 30%. That conversion rate is the clearest evidence that structured governance became the mechanism for acceleration, not a drag on deployment. Projects that passed the governance filter were better scoped, better documented, and better aligned with regulatory requirements before they reached production.

The operational impact was measurable. AI-powered chatbots now automate 75% of customer queries. Customer due diligence processes that previously took weeks were reduced to seconds. When AI was introduced to operations, the bank recorded a 25% productivity gain.

A partnership with Google produced results in financial crime detection that neither organisation could have achieved alone. The collaboration detects two to four times more financial crime whilst reducing false positives by 60%. As Chief Analytics Officer Bahadir Yilmaz framed it: "We are the largest mortgage provider in Europe. We have domain knowledge that can be transferred into digital workflows, supported by AI."

The shift in conversation inside ING is telling. Yilmaz again: "The conversation is no longer about if AI works. It's about understanding where it works and, more importantly, where it matters to the client."

### Transferable Lesson

ING's experience illustrates all three elements of the European Advantage Triad working in concert. **Clarity**: one governance framework across all AI activity, removing the ambiguity that fragments effort and creates compliance risk. **Trust**: CEO participation in the ethics committee, structured review of every use case, and transparent governance that satisfies regulators, employees, and customers simultaneously. **Capability**: a 90% production rate, built on the documentation discipline, data quality, and process rigour that regulatory compliance demands.

The actionable insight for European CEOs is this: centralise AI governance early, at the highest level of accountability you can sustain, and treat the governance process itself as the mechanism that converts pilots into production. ING did not succeed despite regulation. It succeeded because the discipline regulation requires, when embedded in operating structure rather than treated as an afterthought, produces faster and more reliable deployment than unstructured experimentation.
