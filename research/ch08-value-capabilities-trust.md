## 1. VALUE: Creation, Measurement, and Prioritisation

### 1.1 Alternative Value Taxonomies (Beyond BCG Deploy-Reshape-Invent)

**EY's Six-Category Taxonomy for Generative AI Use Cases** (October 2024) offers a more granular classification than DRI:
1. **Advisory** — domain-specific guidance (e.g. financial advisory)
2. **Assistive** — active task execution to improve workflows (e.g. travel booking automation)
3. **Cooperative** — collaborative back-and-forth between AI and users (e.g. cloud migration acceleration)
4. **Autonomous** — AI extending cognitive capabilities into new domains
5–6. Progressing toward **physically autonomous AI** with advanced robotics

*Source: EY, "A Six-Category Taxonomy for Strategic Use Cases," October 2024.*

**Three-Horizon Use-Case Portfolio** (Umbrex AI Value Creation Framework):
- **Horizon 1 (Now):** High-ROI, data-ready operational use cases — forecasting, personalisation, RAG-based knowledge retrieval
- **Horizon 2 (Next):** Function redesign with AI agents — customer service copilots, developer copilots, underwriting assistants
- **Horizon 3 (New):** Business model innovation and AI-enabled services

*Source: Umbrex, "AI Value Creation Framework," 2025.*

**Four Layers of Enterprise AI ROI** (Fusemachines, 2026):
1. **Efficiency ROI** — cycle time, labour hours, cost per transaction
2. **Revenue and Margin ROI** — retention, customer lifetime value, conversion rates
3. **Risk and Compliance ROI** — error rates, audit exceptions, regulatory adherence
4. **Strategic Capability ROI** — decision latency, innovation velocity, governance maturity

*Source: Fusemachines, "AI ROI in 2026: Measuring Value Beyond Proof of Concept," 2026.*

**Five-Dimension Framework** (DIMA AI, 2026):
1. Time savings (easiest to measure)
2. Revenue/margin impact
3. Risk reduction
4. Process transformation
5. Capability expansion

*Source: DIMA AI, "How to Measure AI ROI: The 2026 Reality Check," 2026.*

### 1.2 ROAI / ROI on AI: Definitions, Measurement, and Pitfalls

**The Pilot-to-Scale ROI Collapse:**
- Early pilots showed **31% returns in 2023**, but as projects scaled, ROI **dropped to only 7% by 2025** — below most companies' cost of capital (DIMA AI, 2026).
- **95% of enterprises report zero ROI** from generative AI investments, despite $30–40 billion in global spending (deepsense.ai, 2025).
- Only **5% of organisations demonstrate measurable business value at scale** (BCG, 2025; deepsense.ai, 2025).

**MIT "GenAI Divide" Study (August 2025):**
- 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to deliver **sustained impact on P&L**.
- Based on 150 interviews, survey of 350 employees, and analysis of 300 public AI deployments.
- Failures are **not due to AI model quality** but a "learning gap" in enterprise integration: generic tools don't adapt to enterprise workflows, pilots remain isolated, lack of strategic alignment.
- Over 50% of AI budgets go to sales and marketing, yet strongest ROI appears in **back-office automation and business process optimisation**.
- Vendor partnerships succeed **67% of the time** versus only 33% for internally developed systems.

*Source: MIT Sloan, "GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025," August 2025. Reported in Fortune, Computing UK, BigDataWire.*

**The Missing Baseline Problem:**
The single biggest measurement mistake is **failing to establish performance baselines before implementing AI**. Without baselines, organisations cannot distinguish genuine AI impact from noise (DIMA AI, 2026).

**Measurement Window Problem:**
Traditional ROI models assume stable inputs and predictable outputs, but AI systems evolve with data and integration depth. Cost-reduction metrics capture only surface impact while missing decision acceleration, process compression, and error stabilisation. Multi-year tracking is needed to reveal structural change; quarterly cost savings alone rarely reflect transformation (AICompetence.org, 2026).

**Key Insight for Chapter 8:** Pilot ROI ≠ Scaled ROI. The chapter should stress that value measurement must evolve as AI matures — from efficiency metrics (Deploy) to revenue/margin metrics (Reshape) to strategic capability metrics (Invent). The four-layer ROI framework maps well onto the DRI model.

### 1.3 Where Value Actually Appears

**McKinsey "State of AI" (2025) — Function-Level Revenue Impact:**
- **Strategy and corporate finance:** Revenue increases jumped from 43% to 70% of respondents (early 2024 to late 2024)
- **Supply chain and inventory management:** 67% reporting revenue increases (19% reporting >10% increases)
- **Marketing and sales:** 66% reporting revenue increases
- **Service operations:** 63% reporting revenue increases (18% reporting >10% increases)

While 88% of organisations report regular AI use in at least one business function, approximately two-thirds have **not yet begun scaling** AI across the enterprise. Only 39% report EBIT impact at the enterprise level. The redesign of workflows has the biggest effect on capturing EBIT impact — 21% of respondents have fundamentally redesigned at least some workflows.

*Source: McKinsey, "The State of AI: How Organizations Are Rewiring to Capture Value," 2025.*

**Industry Disconnect (McKinsey, 2025):**
- Financial services: Significant economic potential but **lower gen AI spend** relative to representation
- Consumer goods and retail: Second-highest economic potential but appears **least willing to invest**, possibly due to thin margins
- Healthcare and technology: Lead in gen AI spending
- Manufacturing: High potential but moderate spending

*Nuance vs. existing research:* BCG says 72% of value comes from core functions (operations, marketing, sales). McKinsey's data confirms marketing/sales and operations but adds **strategy/corporate finance** as a high-value area — worth including in Chapter 8.

### 1.4 Prioritisation: How Leading Companies Rank Use Cases

**The Three-Gate Decision Funnel** (IJSRA, 2025):
A six-point opportunity scorecard with three sequential gates helps enterprises systematically filter GenAI initiatives. Research shows this approach **compresses cycle times by 33–40%** and **doubles conversion rates from proof-of-concept to production**.

*Source: IJSRA, "Strategic Decision-Making Framework for Evaluating and Selecting GenAI Use Cases," 2025.*

**Microsoft AI Decision Framework (2025):**
Three-phase intake methodology that prioritises business outcomes before technology selection:
1. Does an existing tool solve this problem?
2. What user experience model is required (Embedded Feature, Assistive Companion, or Immersive Destination)?
3. What autonomy level does the AI need?

Five recurring decision types: invest/don't invest, build/buy/partner, prioritise, scale, and retire. Documentation of rationale creates accountability and enables organisational learning.

*Source: Microsoft, "Microsoft AI Decision Framework," 2025.*

**CSIRO Investment Evaluation Checklist (2025):**
Comprehensive six-dimension evaluation: Strategic Alignment → Resource & Investment Planning → Data Foundations → Analytical Evaluation (ROI, NPV, payback, weighted scorecards, real options for uncertainty) → Risk Management & Ethics → Portfolio Perspective.

*Source: CSIRO, "Guide to Identifying and Investing in AI Projects," 2025.*

## 2. CAPABILITIES: People, Process, Technology

### 2.1 The 10/20/70 Rule in Practice

BCG's **10/20/70 principle** states that 10% of resources should go to algorithms, 20% to technology infrastructure, and **70% to people, processes, and change management**. Most organisations get this backwards.

**What the 70% means in practice (BCG, 2025–2026):**
- Training and coaching programs for all levels
- Organisational restructuring and workforce redesign
- Change management and cultural alignment programs
- Leadership engagement from the start (not bolted on later)
- Co-development with frontline teams (not top-down mandates)
- Incentive and performance review redesign
- Workflow redesign (the single largest driver of EBIT impact, per McKinsey)

Forbes (January 2026) frames the 10/20/70 as "probably the most important principle CEOs and everyone else should internalise" — because it explains why $30–40 billion in AI spending delivers so little: organisations spend on technology (the 10% and 20%) but starve the 70%.

*Source: BCG, multiple publications, 2025; Forbes, "Why AI's 10-20-70 Principle Should Matter," January 2026.*

### 2.2 Critical Roles for the AI-Enabled Organisation

**Agent Orchestrator — "The Most Important Job of 2026" (Eightfold AI):**
- Not an IT position but a **genuine middle-management function** managing synthetic talent rather than people
- Responsibilities: AI operational literacy, agent provisioning (defining boundaries, APIs, data access), human-in-the-loop oversight, workflow integration
- While 82% of executives expect AI agents in their workforce within 18 months, only **23% feel confident** about effective integration
- 85% of companies plan to customise autonomous agents, but only **20% possess mature governance** for managing them

*Source: Eightfold AI, "The Most Important Job of 2026 Is the AI Agent Orchestration Specialist," 2026; Arion Research, 2026; HBR, February 2026.*

**Domain Leader (N-2/N-3) — "The Single Most Critical Role" (McKinsey):**
- Two to three levels below the CEO; has **operational responsibility** for translating transformation visions into real change
- Most companies derive the majority of their AI transformation benefits from **a few deeply transformed domains**
- A "domain" = an end-to-end business process or value stream, typically requiring **5–15 interrelated use cases** to capture transformational value
- Skills required: must develop a "second muscle" beyond traditional executive skills — tech-savvy enough to understand AI-enabled transformation roadmaps, modern software delivery, data estate health, technology platforms, and engineering talent
- Only **17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders** have technical skills; just **5%** held technical roles during their careers — a critical gap

*Source: McKinsey, "Building the AI Muscle of Your Business Leaders," 2025.*

**Supporting Roles (Everworker AI, 2026):**
- AI Product Owners (define outcomes)
- AI Worker/Agent Designers (translate processes into agent capabilities)
- AI Ops (monitor, version, manage agents)
- Domain Subject Matter Experts (validate outputs)
- Risk/Compliance Specialists (review guardrails)

*Source: Everworker AI, "Team Enablement for AI Agent Platforms: 2026 Guide," 2026.*

### 2.3 The Capability Gap: Skills, Readiness, and Failure Modes

**The Upskilling Crisis:**
- Only **6% of companies** have begun upskilling "meaningfully" vs. **89%** acknowledging the need (BCG, 2025)
- Only **48% of AI projects reach production**, with average prototype-to-production timeline at **eight months** (Everworker AI, 2026)
- 69% of enterprise GenAI initiatives stall before operationalisation (IJSRA, 2025)

**European AI Talent Landscape:**
- AI talent in the EU **more than doubled** between 2016 and 2023 (European Commission, 2025)
- **28% of European workers** already use AI tools in their workplaces, with higher adoption in Western Europe
- **40–60% of workers** have poor understanding of AI technology and its implications — a significant AI literacy gap
- **6 in 10 employees** susceptible to some form of AI-related task transformation
- **15% of employees** fear job loss due to AI within five years, with higher concerns in Southern and Central Europe
- EU AI Act requires **AI literacy training** since February 2025

*Source: European Commission, "Shaping and Strengthening European AI Talent," 2025; CEDEFOP, "Skills Empower Workers in the AI Revolution," 2025; Interface EU, "Solving Europe's AI Talent Equation," 2025.*

**European Strategic Responses:**
- **AI Continent Action Plan** (2025): emphasises retaining, attracting, and reskilling AI talent
- **Union of Skills strategy**: governance frameworks for skills development
- **AI Skills Academy**: specialised training on generative AI, pilot degree programs, apprenticeships
- **Proposed EU talent pool regulation**: matching job seekers from non-EU countries with EU employers for green and digital transition roles

*Source: European Commission, 2025; Interface EU, 2025.*

### 2.4 Cross-Functional and Agile: How AI Initiatives Are Resourced

**12-Factor Framework for Agentic AI ROI** (deepsense.ai, 2025):
Organised across three pillars:
1. **Strategy & Governance** — strategic alignment, governance, measurement, security
2. **Operating Model** — process redesign, orchestration, data readiness, partnerships
3. **Adoption & Scaling** — employee adoption, learning systems, use case selection

Key insight: companies with a **Chief AI Officer achieve ~10% higher ROI**. Formalised adoption, training, and incentives are essential.

*Source: deepsense.ai, "From Pilots to P&L: The 12 Factors That Determine Agentic AI ROI," 2025.*

## 3. TRUST: Governance, Risk, Ethics, Regulation

### 3.1 Trust Frameworks Compared

**IBM's Five Pillars of AI Governance:**
1. Fair decisions
2. Explainable outputs
3. Lifecycle monitoring
4. In-production oversight with automated guardrails
5. Accountability and auditability

IBM emphasises practical enterprise-scale implementation, addressing accuracy, bias mitigation, and explainability throughout the AI lifecycle.

*Source: IBM, "Guide for Implementing an AI Governance Framework," 2025.*

**Microsoft's Responsible AI (aligned with NIST AI RMF):**
Integrates NIST's four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) into development and deployment. Adds a business-centric maturity model focused on strategy, vision, culture, and change management. Microsoft's 2025 Responsible AI Transparency Report highlights investments in responsible AI tooling, compliance frameworks, and pre-deployment reviews.

*Source: Microsoft, "Responsible AI Transparency Report," 2025; AI Governance Group, 2025.*

**Deloitte's Trustworthy AI™ — Seven Dimensions:**
1. Transparent & Explainable
2. Fair & Impartial
3. Robust & Reliable
4. Respectful of Privacy
5. Safe & Secure
6. Responsible & Accountable
7. (Governance and regulatory compliance applied across the entire AI lifecycle)

Trust-focused actions correlate with better risk management outcomes and higher benefit realisation.

*Source: Deloitte, "Trustworthy AI," 2025; Deloitte, "Generative AI Trust Actions Correlate with Better Risk Management," 2025.*

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework:**
Four core functions: **Govern → Map → Measure → Manage**. Broad, multi-dimensional assessment including governance/ethics, technical proficiency, data management, and impact/value evaluation. Serves as the foundational layer that IBM, Microsoft, and others build upon.

*Source: NIST AI RMF, 2023; updated guidance 2025.*

**Terminology Distinctions:**
- "Trustworthy AI" (Deloitte, EU) = comprehensive term encompassing technical reliability, ethical behaviour, and regulatory compliance
- "Responsible AI" (Microsoft, Accenture) = emphasis on organisational accountability and duty of care
- "Ethical AI" = narrower focus on fairness, bias, and moral principles
- In practice, these terms overlap significantly; the EU and most consultancies now converge on "trustworthy" as the umbrella term

**Comparative Analysis Note:** A 2025 study of 17 global AI policies identified 11 key features common across frameworks, but found organisations face challenges with **resource intensity, scalability, and ambiguous implementation targets** (Springer, 2025). The gap between framework theory and practical implementation remains wide.

### 3.2 EU AI Act in Practice (2025–2026)

**Implementation Timeline:**
- **1 August 2024:** AI Act enters into force
- **2 February 2025:** Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI apply; AI literacy training obligations begin
- **2 August 2025:** Obligations for general-purpose AI (GPAI) models apply
- **2 August 2026:** Full application of high-risk AI system requirements and enforcement begins
- **2 August 2027:** Complete application of all remaining provisions

**Six Core Compliance Steps** (Orrick, 2025):
1. AI System Mapping — identify all AI systems in use, development, importing, or distribution
2. Role Clarification — determine role (developer, provider, deployer) for each system
3. Applicability Assessment — determine whether the Act applies
4. Risk Classification — classify using four-tier risk framework
5. Contract Review — assess AI-related service contracts
6. Governance Framework — implement or adapt compliance programs

**Practical Challenges (European Commission, 2025):**
- Delays in designating national competent authorities and conformity assessment bodies
- Lack of harmonised standards for high-risk requirements
- Insufficient guidance and compliance tools
- Risk of significantly **increased compliance costs** and slowed innovation

**Support Infrastructure:**
- AI Act Service Desk with single information platform
- Guidelines for GPAI model providers (published 2025)
- General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (voluntary tool)
- Commission templates and digital tools
- Harmonised standards expected before August 2026

*Source: EU Commission, Orrick, European Parliament Research Service, 2025.*

### 3.3 GDPR and EU AI Act Overlap

The intersection of these two regimes creates significant compliance complexity, particularly for:

**Impact Assessments:** AI Act requires fundamental rights impact assessments; GDPR mandates data protection impact assessments (DPIAs). These differ in scope, supervision, and procedure, creating duplication and uncertainty.

**Transparency and Logging:** Both regulations impose overlapping transparency and logging obligations.

**Data Rights:** Ambiguity over how to manage GDPR rights (access, rectification, erasure) when personal data is embedded in complex AI models.

**Financial Sector Pressure:** 7 of 8 high-risk AI system categories involve personal data processing — meaning **over 87% of high-risk AI cases require GDPR compliance** in addition to AI Act compliance.

**Scope Divergence:** The AI Act covers AI systems regardless of personal data processing; GDPR applies only to personal data. This creates broader AI Act applicability that compounds compliance complexity.

*Source: Lexology, European Parliament, Advisera, 2025.*

### 3.4 Trust as Competitive Advantage

**Gartner Trust-Maturity Correlation (Q4 2024 Survey, 432 respondents):**
- In **57% of high-maturity organisations**, business units trust and are ready to use new AI solutions vs. only **14% of low-maturity** organisations
- **45% of high-maturity** organisations keep AI projects operational for 3+ years vs. **20% of low-maturity**
- **63% of high-maturity** organisations implement financial analysis and metrics to quantify AI benefits
- **91% of high-maturity** organisations have appointed dedicated AI leaders
- Sr Director Analyst Birgi Tamersoy: **"Trust is one of the differentiators between success and failure for an AI or GenAI initiative"**

*Source: Gartner, "Survey Finds 45% of Organizations With High AI Maturity Keep AI Projects Operational for at Least Three Years," June 2025.*

**Accenture: Responsible AI from Risk to Value** (joint research with AWS and Stanford, 1,000+ executives):
- **74% of companies** temporarily paused AI projects due to risk concerns
- **56% of Fortune 500** companies cite AI as a "risk factor" in annual reports
- **77% of executives** believe unlocking AI's true benefits requires a foundation of trust
- Only **2% of companies** have fully operationalised responsible AI across their organisations
- Risk areas: privacy/data governance (51%), security (47%), reliability (45%)
- Generative AI-driven incidents account for roughly **two-thirds** of all AI incidents
- **77% of companies** expect to face AI-adjacent legal obligations within five years
- Value creation beyond compliance: enhanced customer satisfaction, improved product quality, better talent acquisition

*Source: Accenture, "Thrive with Responsible AI: Embedding Trust Can Unlock Value," 2025; Accenture, "From Compliance to Confidence," 2025.*

**EU AI Pact as Trust Moat:**
- Over **230 voluntary pledgers**, 3,200+ community members
- **63% of signatories** are EU-based companies
- More than **50% have gone beyond mandatory requirements** — advancing AI literacy, supporting startups/SMEs, adapting tools to AI Act standards
- Allianz's partnership with Anthropic: deploying auditable, governance-focused AI agents across insurance operations — sets benchmark for regulated industries
- Practical benefits: shorter operational cycles, reduced costs, ability to scale AI safely in compliance-heavy environments

*Source: European Commission, "AI Pact Pledgers' Achievements," 2025; i10x AI, 2025.*

### 3.5 Board and Audit Governance

**Current State:**
- **66% of boards** report limited, minimal, or no AI knowledge (existing research)
- Nearly **50% of board directors** say AI is not yet on the board agenda (Deloitte, 2025)
- Only **15% of boards** receive AI-related metrics
- Only **14% of boards** discuss AI at every meeting
- Yet **88% of organisations** use AI in at least one business function

**Governance Structures:**
- 72% of boards already have one or more committees responsible for risk oversight
- Over 80% have risk management experts on the board
- AI governance must integrate with **enterprise risk management (ERM)** rather than operating in isolation

**2026 Board Roadmap** (WilmerHale, 2026):
1. Comprehensive assessment of AI use and risks across the organisation
2. Establishment of effective oversight structures
3. Implementation of risk management protocols aligned with recognised frameworks
4. Empowerment of teams to leverage AI opportunities

**Risk Scoring (AI Governance Exposure Index):**
Composite metric weighing system impact, model autonomy, regulatory sensitivity, and monitoring maturity. Combined with continuous monitoring and runtime telemetry for high-risk systems with documented validation cycles.

*Source: WilmerHale, "Board Oversight and AI: Key Governance Priorities for 2026," January 2026; Deloitte, 2025; AICompetence.org, 2026.*

## 4. USING THE THREE ANGLES TOGETHER

### 4.1 Decision Frameworks Combining Value, Capability, and Trust

**Microsoft AI Decision Framework (2025):**
Applies across five recurring decision types: invest/don't invest, build/buy/partner, prioritise, scale, and retire. Uses an intake filter to prevent "shiny object syndrome" before evaluating business value, capability readiness, and governance requirements.

**CSIRO Six-Dimension Investment Checklist (2025):**
Strategic Alignment → Resource & Investment Planning → Data Foundations → Analytical Evaluation (ROI, NPV, payback, weighted scorecards, real options for uncertain projects) → Risk Management & Ethics → Portfolio Perspective. Evaluates projects within the broader AI portfolio context, balancing risk, reward, and strategic objectives against non-AI alternatives.

**Strategic Three-Gate Decision Funnel (IJSRA, 2025):**
Combined with a six-point opportunity scorecard. Compresses cycle times by 33–40% and doubles PoC-to-production conversion rates. The gates map well onto Value (does this create business value?), Capability (can we deliver this?), and Trust (can we govern this?).

**Pertama Partners AI Strategic Decision Framework (2025):**
Explicitly structures CEO decisions around:
- **Value:** Does this align with strategy? What's the expected business case?
- **Capability:** Do we have the skills, data infrastructure, and readiness?
- **Trust:** Can we govern this transparently with appropriate oversight?

*Source: Pertama Partners, "AI Strategic Decisions: Executive Decision-Making Framework," 2025.*

### 4.2 Tensions and Trade-offs

**High Value but Low Capability:**
Frequent scenario. The answer is not to avoid the opportunity but to **sequence capability-building** before scaling. McKinsey's domain leader model addresses this: identify the 5–15 use cases in a domain, then build capability iteratively rather than attempting all at once.

**High Value but High Risk:**
Requires a **trust-first approach**: governance, audit, and compliance frameworks must be in place before scaling. The Allianz-Anthropic partnership model shows how regulated industries can pursue high-value AI while maintaining trust. The "compliance-first" approach paradoxically accelerates deployment by removing regulatory uncertainty.

**Quick Wins vs. Capability-Building vs. Trust-Building:**
- **Quick wins** (Deploy): Build momentum and demonstrate value; require minimal capability and trust investment
- **Capability-building** (Reshape): Takes 6–18 months; requires domain leaders, process redesign, new roles
- **Trust-building** (Governance): Ongoing investment; pays off in adoption rates, regulatory readiness, and stakeholder confidence

The optimal sequencing: start with quick wins to build momentum and fund further investment → invest in capability and trust in parallel → scale Reshape and Invent plays once foundations are solid.

### 4.3 Link to Readiness (Chapter 12)

Value, Capability, and Trust serve as the three diagnostic dimensions in the readiness assessment:
- **Value readiness:** Are AI initiatives tied to measurable business outcomes? Are prioritisation criteria clear?
- **Capability readiness:** Do we have the skills, data, technology, and processes to deliver?
- **Trust readiness:** Do we have governance, compliance, and oversight structures in place?

Each dimension can be scored on a maturity scale (e.g. 1–5), producing a visual radar chart that highlights strengths and gaps. Weights can be adjusted by industry and regulatory context (e.g. heavier trust weighting for financial services or healthcare).

## 6. GAPS AND NUANCES VS. EXISTING RESEARCH

### What This Research Adds (New)
- **Four-layer ROI framework** (Fusemachines) that maps onto DRI
- **Pilot-to-scale ROI collapse**: 31% → 7% data point
- **MIT study details**: root causes (learning gap, not model quality), vendor vs. internal success rates (67% vs. 33%)
- **Agent Orchestrator** as defined role with specific responsibilities (Eightfold, HBR, Arion Research)
- **Domain Leader** detail: only 17% of Fortune 500 leaders have technical skills; a domain = 5–15 use cases
- **EU AI Act practical challenges**: lack of harmonised standards, delays in designating authorities
- **GDPR/AI Act overlap**: impact assessment duplication, 87% of high-risk cases involve personal data
- **Accenture**: 74% of companies paused AI projects due to risk; 77% say trust is foundation for value
- **European investment data**: 91% expect revenue growth (Accenture 2026); 5% budget allocation target (Capgemini)
- **PE mid-market**: Roland Berger's sprint model (4–6 weeks); Equiforte data on deal intelligence

### Conflicts / Nuances
- **MIT's 95% failure rate** has been criticised as oversimplifying (BigDataWire, September 2025) — the study measured P&L impact of pilots, not long-term scaled deployments. Chapter 8 should use this carefully: it's a pilot-stage finding, not a verdict on AI's potential.
- **ROI measurement window**: MIT measured at pilot stage; BCG and McKinsey argue multi-year investment is needed. The chapter should acknowledge that short-term ROI measurement (the "6-month trap") systematically understates AI value.
- **Where value appears**: BCG says 72% in core functions; McKinsey adds strategy/corporate finance as a high-value area — these are complementary, not contradictory.
- **Trust frameworks**: While IBM, Microsoft, Deloitte, and NIST all converge on similar principles, implementation remains challenging — a 2025 study found organisations struggle with resource intensity and ambiguous targets. The frameworks are aspirational; the chapter should be honest about the gap between theory and practice.
