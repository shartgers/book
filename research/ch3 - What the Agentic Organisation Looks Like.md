# The agentic enterprise: new evidence for European CEOs

**The "agentic enterprise" has crossed from concept to contested reality in under 18 months.** Between mid-2024 and early 2026, at least 15 major firms beyond McKinsey, BCG, and Deloitte published formal definitions or frameworks — yet only **2% of organisations** have deployed AI agents at scale, and trust in fully autonomous agents has actually declined from 43% to 27% in a single year. For European CEOs, this tension defines the strategic moment: the operating model shift is real, the technology is maturing fast, but the human, regulatory, and organisational dimensions remain dangerously underdeveloped. Europe holds distinctive advantages — co-determination traditions, a regulatory head start via the EU AI Act, and world-class industrial champions already deploying agentic systems — but only if leaders treat this as an operating model transformation, not a technology rollout.

---

## 1. Who defines the agentic enterprise — and how definitions diverge

Salesforce's Marc Benioff is widely credited with coining "agentic enterprise" in 2025, defining it as "a business where human employees and AI agents work together in a seamless, collaborative ecosystem." But this marketing-origin definition now competes with at least a dozen others, each revealing different strategic priorities.

**Gartner** takes the technology-forward view, naming agentic AI its #1 strategic technology trend for 2025 and defining it as "goal-driven software entities that use AI techniques to complete tasks and achieve goals" without predetermined outputs. Their quantitative forecasts are stark: **40% of enterprise applications** will integrate task-specific AI agents by end of 2026 (up from under 5% in 2025), and agentic AI could drive **$450 billion** in enterprise software revenue by 2035. Yet Gartner simultaneously warns that **over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by 2027** due to escalating costs and unclear value — and estimates that only about 130 of the thousands of agentic AI vendors are genuine, with the rest engaged in "agent washing."

**Forrester** offers the most architecturally rigorous and sceptical perspective. Their "Agentic Business Fabric" concept — "an intelligent ecosystem where AI agents, your data, and your employees work together to achieve business outcomes" — comes with a pointed warning. Principal Analyst Leslie Joseph argues that the "agent as employee" metaphor is dangerous because it "bestows a false sense of autonomy, judgment, and accountability to the agent" and "reinforces the organizational status quo." Forrester's AEGIS framework (Agentic AI Enterprise Guardrails for Information Security) introduces the principle of **"Least Agency"** — extending least-privilege security concepts to decisions and actions, not just data access.

**Capgemini's** Paris-headquartered Research Institute produced the most trust-focused framework. Their "Resonance AI" framework, built on three pillars (Envision, Embed, Enable), centres on what they call "human-AI chemistry." Their data is sobering: while AI agents could generate up to **$450 billion** in economic value by 2028, trust in fully autonomous agents has dropped sharply. Only **2% of organisations** have achieved at-scale deployment. Capgemini uses "AI-driven enterprise" rather than "agentic enterprise," a deliberate framing choice emphasising outcome over technology.

**The Hackett Group** provides what may be the most concise formal definition: "An agentic enterprise is an organization where artificial intelligence and the human workforce operate as a coordinated system, each enhancing the other's capabilities." **IBM** emphasises governance-first architecture and hybrid deployment — particularly relevant for European regulated industries — while **PwC's** survey of 308 executives found **79% already adopting** AI agents and **88% planning budget increases**, yet acknowledged most are "treating AI agents as bolt-ons to existing workflows."

The critical conceptual distinction now crystallising across all sources is between **agentic AI** (a technology capability — autonomous, goal-driven software) and the **agentic enterprise** (an operating model — the redesign of work, roles, governance, and structure around human-agent collaboration). Gartner, IEEE, and NIST focus on the former; Salesforce, Forrester, and the Hackett Group on the latter. McKinsey sits between, describing "the agentic organization" as the next organisational paradigm after industrial, digital, and agile eras. For CEOs, the operating model lens is what matters — technology capability without organisational redesign delivers incremental gains at best.

**European-specific frameworks are emerging.** The European Commission's "Apply AI Strategy" (October 2025) defines AI agents as "AI systems that can independently make decisions and take actions… orchestrating interactions including with humans." A January 2026 Commission-backed report, "Agentic AI: Leveraging European AI Talent and Regulatory Assets," identified three systemic European barriers: blurred responsibility from multi-step autonomous actions, fragmented data and technological dependence on foreign cloud providers, and uneven capacity across Member States. Encouragingly, the report found that **Europe allocates a higher share of venture capital to agentic AI than the US**, with France, Germany, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Munich, and Barcelona as leading hubs.

**Standards bodies are moving fast.** Singapore's IMDA published the world's first comprehensive governance framework specifically for agentic AI in January 2026 at Davos, built on four pillars: bounding risks upfront, making humans meaningfully accountable, implementing technical controls, and enabling end-user responsibility. NIST launched its AI Agent Standards Initiative in February 2026, prioritising industry-led standards, safeguards against misuse, and identity/credentialing for AI agents. OWASP expanded into agentic AI security, and the MAESTRO framework addresses multi-agent threat modelling. No ISO standard specifically covers agentic AI yet, though ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management systems) provides the nearest governance scaffolding.

## 2. Five elements of the agentic enterprise in practice

### Products: from search bars to zero-click commerce

The shift toward AI-native products is most visible in commerce, where **60% of searches now end without a single click** (up from 26% in 2022), with analysts projecting 70% by early 2026. European companies are leading this transformation. **Klarna** (Sweden) launched its Agentic Product Protocol — an open standard making over 100 million products discoverable by AI agents across 12 markets. This sits alongside Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol, Google's Agent Payments Protocol, and Stripe's protocol with OpenAI, creating the infrastructure layer for machine-mediated purchasing. Klarna's AI-driven dynamic "lookbooks" with Google Cloud boosted in-app time by **15%** and orders by **50%**.

**Zalando** (Germany) rolled out an AI-powered discovery feed across **22 European markets**, moving beyond traditional e-commerce toward emotion- and inspiration-driven shopping. Their acquisition of UK AR startup DeepAR is building Europe's largest digital twin catalogue for virtual try-on. **Inditex/Zara** (Spain) launched virtual fitting rooms across six European countries using generative AI, while AI-generated marketing imagery cut intercontinental photo crew travel by over 70%. Inditex's real-time data intelligence — corporate reports updated hourly across 6,000+ stores — enables new-product-to-production cycles of **10–15 days** versus the industry average of 150.

For B2B, agentic AI resolves what one analysis calls "the long tail of personalisation." A single B2B campaign targeting 3 industries × 4 company sizes × 5 buyer personas generates 60 unique content variations — multiplied across channels into hundreds of assets. Agentic AI manages this complexity autonomously, and companies leveraging personalisation drive **40% more revenue** than those that don't.

### People: the 89/6 gap between knowing and doing

The upskilling gap remains staggering. The widely cited statistics trace to a 2024 BCG study: while **89% of respondents** said their workforce needs improved AI skills, **only 6%** had begun upskilling "in a meaningful way." IBM estimates approximately 40% of workforces need reskilling within three years. Job postings for "AI operations" roles jumped **230%** in six months, and postings mentioning agentic AI grew **985%** between 2023 and 2024.

The emerging role taxonomy, detailed most comprehensively in a September 2025 CIO article, identifies six critical new positions: **AI Agent Orchestrator** ("the new CTO of a digital workforce"), AgentOps Specialist, Human-Agent Interaction Designer, AI Ethics & Governance Specialist, AI Workforce Integration Strategist, and Agentic Business Architect. Arion Research describes the Agent Orchestrator as someone who "doesn't manage people, but manages the synthetic talent that supports them," arguing that deploying an AI agent follows a similar arc to onboarding employees: credentialing, orientation, role definition, access provisioning, and a probationary period.

The World Economic Forum's January 2026 blueprint is explicit: "AI will create more jobs than it displaces, but **only if companies invest deliberately in people and redesign work**, rather than simply layering technology onto old structures." SAP exemplifies this at scale — dedicating **15% of employee working time** to AI learning and shifting from rigid job descriptions to skills-based frameworks. Chief People Officer Gina Vorgiu frames it starkly: "Skills for me are the new supply chain or the new currency in the age of AI."

### Process: from standalone agents to orchestrated swarms

The progression from standalone agents to orchestrated groups to autonomous swarms is now well-documented. Standalone agents handle single, human-prompted tasks. Agent groups use a central orchestrator delegating to specialised sub-agents. Agent swarms operate in fully decentralised or hybrid patterns, with agents coordinating autonomously. Three architectures have emerged: centralised/hierarchical (regulated industries), peer-to-peer (research), and hybrid (most common in enterprise).

KPMG estimates agentic AI will drive **$3 trillion in corporate productivity improvement**, with companies earning **$3.50 for every $1 invested** (top 5% earn $8). They project a **30% increase in workforce efficiency** and **25% decrease in operational costs** by 2027. The Minimum Viable Organisation concept, documented by German consultancy msg advisors and others, gains new resonance as AI agents handle tasks previously requiring headcount. Eric Dulaurans proposes a four-layer "Minimum Viable Philosophy" — MVP → Minimum Viable Service → MVO → Minimum Viable Ecosystem — where the MVO is "the organization with just enough people and process to support the Minimum Viable Service."

### Technology: MCP, A2A, and the seven-layer stack

Two protocols now form the connective tissue of the agentic enterprise. **Anthropic's Model Context Protocol (MCP)**, launched November 2024 and donated to the Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation in December 2025, replaces fragmented data integrations with a universal interface. Adoption has been explosive: MCP server downloads grew from approximately 100,000 to over **8 million** within six months, with 97 million monthly SDK downloads. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Microsoft all adopted MCP in 2025. NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang declared it "completely revolutionised the AI landscape."

**Google's Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol**, launched April 2025, complements MCP by enabling agent-to-agent communication — letting agents work as peers, handing off tasks via structured JSON messages and "Agent Cards." Where MCP connects agents to tools and data, A2A connects agents to each other.

A seven-layer agentic stack has crystallised: Foundation Models → Protocols & Interoperability → Orchestration Frameworks → Tools & Actuators → Memory → Evaluation & Governance → Applications. Value accrues at the evaluation/governance and application layers, while model and protocol layers commoditise rapidly. The **"agentic mesh"** concept, formalised in an O'Reilly book by Eric Broda, provides the unified runtime, control plane, and trust framework — drawing on established patterns from API service mesh and data mesh architectures. Microsoft's "Agent Factory" (a formal six-part deployment pipeline), AWS's Partner Agent Factory, and Oracle's AI Database Private Agent Factory all operationalise agent production at scale.

For organisational deployment, the hub-and-spoke model is emerging as preferred: a centre of excellence establishes enterprise-wide standards and governance while business units own execution. Microsoft's Cloud Adoption Framework recommends starting centralised, then transitioning to an advisory model — "Replace the CoE gatekeeper model that blocks work with an advisory group that sets guardrails."

### Data: the readiness gap Europe cannot ignore

Data readiness is the single largest blocker. **Only one-third of organisations** have data sufficiently prepared for agentic AI (PwC). **61%** say their data readiness still needs improvement (Sinequa/ChapsVision). The World Economic Forum reports **72%** will prioritise data foundations over the next 12 months. Precisely and Drexel University coined the term **"Agentic AI Data Integrity Gap"** — 87% report having necessary infrastructure, yet 42% admit infrastructure is their biggest obstacle.

For Europe specifically, the GDPR implications are profound. The International Association of Privacy Professionals detailed how a simple AI scheduling agent can trigger purpose creep, special-category data rules, cross-border transfer restrictions, and Article 22 prohibitions on fully automated processing — all in a single workflow. The European Data Protection Supervisor warned that agentic AI systems "could make decisions affecting human lives with minimal direct oversight, risking undermining human dignity and autonomy by reducing individuals to data points." Fifteen months after the AI Act came into force, the AI Office has still not issued guidance specifically for AI agents — creating both a gap and an opportunity for proactive governance.

The EU AI Act imposes escalating obligations through August 2027, with penalties up to **€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover**. European pioneers like **Erste Group Bank** (Central Europe) are already building AI governance agents on platforms like Databricks, replacing ticket-based compliance with conversational compliance assistants. Spain's AESIA released 16 detailed compliance guides from its AI regulatory sandbox — the most comprehensive national implementation guidance so far.

## 3. European champions are building the agentic enterprise now

The evidence across five major European companies reveals distinct but converging approaches.

**BBVA** (Spain) represents the most aggressive deployment. Having rolled out ChatGPT Enterprise to **120,000 employees** — one of the largest corporate AI deployments globally — BBVA reports employees save an average of **3 hours per week**, with 80% of users accessing the platform daily. Their "Eight" strategy deploys interconnected agent solutions across advisory, risk, operations, and development. In Peru, one AI assistant reduced query handling from 7.5 minutes to approximately 1 minute. BBVA was among the first institutions globally to test GPT-5 and is exploring native banking within ChatGPT for its Italian and German digital banks. Their Data Mesh model distributes data management while "AI Factories" in Spain, Mexico, and Türkiye bring business teams and data scientists together.

**ING** (Netherlands) achieves a remarkable **90% pilot-to-production rate** versus the 30% industry average, built on a single centralised platform for all AI development. COO Marnix van Stiphout provides a concrete ratio benchmark: "When AI is introduced to an operations process, **25% fewer people** are generally required." Their agentic AI mortgage processing gives each human "a mini team" of agents handling extraction and checking, while the human retains final decision authority. Agentic AI-generated mortgages are planned for 2026, starting in Germany and the Netherlands.

**Siemens** (Germany) introduced a multi-agent orchestrator system deploying specialised agents across the entire industrial value chain, targeting **up to 50% productivity gains**. Their distinction between Industrial Copilots (user-facing) and AI agents (behind-the-scenes execution) provides a clear architectural template. At thyssenkrupp, the Industrial Copilot is being deployed globally for battery and hydrogen assembly lines. Siemens plans an AI agent marketplace on its Xcelerator platform, and NVIDIA is building the world's first industrial AI cloud in Germany with 10,000 GPUs serving BMW and Mercedes-Benz.

**Allianz** (Germany) deployed **Project Nemo** — seven specialised agents for claims processing that reduced processing time by **80%**, executing the full workflow in under 5 minutes. With approximately 400 GenAI use cases in production and a global partnership with Anthropic announced January 2026, Allianz exemplifies the insurance sector's agentic transformation. Chief Transformation Officer Maria Janssen articulates the governance principle: "AI agents support our teams by making recommendations, but the ultimate responsibility always rests with a claims professional." Their 'Data Value' metric quantifies financial impact per AI project — almost every third AI and data asset is linked to quantifiable dollar values.

**Schneider Electric** (France) is building what IDC analyst Amy Cravens calls "an agentic AI ecosystem that sets a new standard for sustainability management." Their EcoStruxure platform coordinates HVAC, security, lighting, microgrid optimisation, and industrial processes through AI agents. The Industrial Copilot reduces engineering time by **30–50%**, with plans to evolve into a fully agentic system. Their "Sera" AI agent powers resource advisory across buildings, data centres, and infrastructure. **Bosch** (Germany) has invested **€2.5 billion** in AI through 2027, with multi-agent systems already operational in manufacturing that have reduced scrap by **40%**.

## 4. The agentic employee: enthusiasm and anxiety coexist rationally

The human side of the agentic enterprise is defined by a paradox that only appears contradictory. **EY's** October 2025 survey of 1,100+ desk workers found **84% eager to embrace agentic AI** while **56% simultaneously worried about job security**. The **Writer/Workplace Intelligence** survey reported that 31% of employees are "sabotaging" their company's AI strategy — but this widely cited statistic deserves scrutiny. The definition of "sabotage" included using non-approved tools, refusing training, and not reporting security leaks. Multiple analysts pushed back: Writer is an AI vendor with commercial interest in framing resistance as a problem, and the sample actively sought generative AI users. Career expert Patrice Williams Lindo offers a more nuanced reading: "It's not always malicious. It's often protective."

The resolution is straightforward: workers simultaneously embrace productivity gains and fear structural displacement. This is the rational response of people who see a tool's power and correctly infer its implications. **The key variable is organisational communication.** EY found that at organisations clearly communicating their AI strategy, **92% report positive productivity impact** — a 30 percentage-point jump over organisations without clear communication. Yet **85% of workers are learning about AI agents outside of work**, and 83% say their knowledge is self-taught — a massive failure of organisational support.

Longitudinal productivity data is now emerging. An SSRN study (Sarkar, November 2025) found software output increased by **39% after AI agents** became the default platform, with a crucial nuance: **more experienced workers benefit more from agents** (one standard deviation higher experience = 6% higher acceptance rates), while junior workers benefit more from tool-only AI. This reversal has profound implications for workforce planning. Stanford HAI and MIT CSAIL studies show time savings of **65–86%** versus human-only workflows, while a legal technology trial across 12 firms found a **41% average productivity increase**.

Three human-role frameworks have emerged beyond the Big Three consultancies. **Accenture** maps three stages: AI Assist → AI Adviser → Autonomation. **Bain** defines four capability levels from LLM-powered copilots to multi-agent constellations. The **CIO/IBM taxonomy** is the most detailed: AI Agent Orchestrator, Human-Agent Collaboration Designer, AI Ethics & Governance Specialist, AgentOps Engineer, GTM Engineer, and AI Workforce Integration Strategist. Academic research (PACIS 2025) adds a critical finding: single-agent human-agent teaming shows weaker performance than human-only teams, but **multi-agent teaming demonstrates superior task allocation and resilience** — suggesting that the benefits emerge only at scale.

## 5. From task-doer to orchestrator: the defining competency shift

The most quoted insight of 2025 comes from May Habib, CEO of Writer, at the World Economic Forum: **"The enterprise was designed for a world where execution was the primary constraint. Today, execution is cheap, abundant and instantaneous. Now the constraint is orchestration."** This single sentence captures the mindset shift European CEOs must drive.

The "orchestrator" concept converges from three disciplines. In **management literature**, network orchestration (Dhanaraj & Parkhe, 2006) means "the set of deliberate, purposeful actions to create and extract value from the network." In **HR/talent**, it means dynamic org design — redeploying resources, reskilling, redesigning roles (Josh Bersin's "Supermanagers" concept). In **AI/technical literature**, it means coordinating autonomous agents across workflows (IBM watsonx Orchestrate, Salesforce Agentforce, ServiceNow AI Control Tower). The agentic enterprise forces all three meanings together: the leader orchestrates a hybrid ecosystem of humans and AI agents without direct hierarchical control.

**PwC's** workforce analysis proposes two structural models: the "diamond" (reducing junior roles as AI handles entry-level work) and the "hourglass" (expanding entry-level positions with AI-literate generalists to preserve the apprenticeship pipeline). The hourglass avoids starving the talent development funnel — a critical concern for European companies with strong apprenticeship traditions.

**Anthropic's 4D Framework** provides a practical measurement model for AI fluency: Delegation (knowing what to assign to AI), Description (communicating effectively with AI), Discernment (evaluating AI outputs critically), and Diligence (maintaining ethical standards). Scaled Agile's tiered model maps fluency expectations across four organisational levels: board/CEO, executive, functional leader, and employee — each with distinct orchestration responsibilities.

Some companies are making the shift explicit through policy. **Shopify** CEO Tobi Lütke mandated in April 2025 that employees must "demonstrate why artificial intelligence cannot fulfill their needs before requesting additional staff or resources." **Klarna** offers a cautionary counterpoint: having cut its workforce by 40% and proclaimed an AI chatbot did the work of 700 agents, the company later admitted that "cost unfortunately seems to have been a too predominant evaluation factor" as customer satisfaction dropped. Klarna reversed course, rehiring humans in a hybrid model. The lesson for European CEOs: orchestration means human-plus-AI, not AI-instead-of-human.

**IDC FutureScape 2026** predicts **40% of all Global 2000 job roles** will involve working with AI agents by 2026, redefining entry-, mid-, and senior-level positions. The WEF's December 2025 framework makes the redesign concrete: strip away organisational muscle memory (duplicate requests, redundant checks), redesign roles (a marketing analyst spending 60% of time pulling data becomes a "revenue impact strategist"), and redesign career growth ("sideways becomes the new upward"). RACI matrices require fundamental revision — as the WEF warns, "your responsibility assignment framework falls apart the moment agents take action."

## 6. European labour law reshapes agentic design — and may be an advantage

Europe's regulatory environment is often framed as a constraint, but evidence suggests it may force better design. **Germany's Mitbestimmung** (co-determination) gives works councils explicit rights over AI deployment. The Works Constitution Act, updated in 2021 to reference AI directly, requires employers to inform works councils about AI system introduction (§90), grants works councils the right to consult experts (§80), and triggers co-determination when AI can monitor employee behaviour (§87). The Hamburg Labour Court ruled in January 2024 that works councils have no co-determination right over voluntary ChatGPT use via private accounts — but company-deployed AI systems clearly trigger full co-determination rights.

**Deutsche Telekom** demonstrates the co-determination advantage. Their AI Manifesto, co-created by management and the Group Works Council, establishes that employees must be informed when AI is used, AI will not be used for surveillance, personnel decisions remain human, and works councils hold **veto rights** on new AI deployments. Rather than slowing adoption, this created an accelerator: "AI Roadshows" featuring tech executives and works council representatives side by side built trust faster than top-down mandates.

**France** provides a sharper regulatory signal. A 2025 court ordered the suspension of an AI project and imposed penalties of €1,000 per day for 90 days plus €5,000 in damages to the works council — for proceeding without proper consultation. Companies with 50+ employees must inform and consult works councils before introducing new technologies.

The **EU AI Act's Article 4** — the AI literacy obligation, in force since February 2025 — requires all providers and deployers of AI systems to ensure "a sufficient level of AI literacy" among staff. This applies to all AI systems regardless of risk level, extends to contractors and service providers, and while it carries no standalone fine, non-compliance becomes an aggravating factor escalating penalties for other violations. By August 2026, full high-risk system obligations take effect, covering recruitment, candidate screening, performance assessment, and promotion decisions. Employers must provide human oversight by competent individuals with authority to intervene, maintain automated logs for at least six months, and inform worker representatives.

The **right to disconnect** gains entirely new urgency in an agentic world. When AI agents operate 24/7, escalating at 2am or producing overnight outputs requiring review, the boundary between work and rest dissolves. France, Spain, Belgium, Luxembourg, and Ireland have enacted various provisions; Eurofound data shows teleworkers are twice as likely to exceed the 48-hour working time limit. Organisations must design explicit "handoff protocols" and "batching mechanisms" so that human supervisors are not dragged into always-on patterns by their always-on AI teammates.

The **Nordic model** offers a third path. Nordic firms deployed AI solutions **20% faster** than the European average in 2025. Denmark's enterprise AI adoption rate of approximately **28%** is double the EU average. Sweden's February 2026 AI Strategy endorsed €1.5 billion in additional investment, emphasising that "Sweden's labour market model and transition systems enable rapid and common solutions." The first Nordic AI Union Summit (Oslo, 2026) brought together union decision-makers specifically on agentic AI — a worker-led digital transformation approach unique to the region. The UK, by contrast, takes a principles-based approach with no comprehensive AI legislation, though the EU AI Act's extraterritorial reach means UK firms serving EU markets will drift toward the higher standard.

## Conclusion: what the evidence demands of European CEOs

Five insights emerge from this research that go beyond existing consultant narratives.

First, **the definition war matters strategically.** Whether you adopt Salesforce's "agentic enterprise," Microsoft's "Frontier Firm," Forrester's "Agentic Business Fabric," or Capgemini's "human-AI chemistry" shapes how your organisation thinks about the transformation. Forrester's warning against the "agent as employee" metaphor deserves serious attention — agents are cognitive assets in a portfolio, not headcount substitutes.

Second, **the data readiness gap is more severe than the skills gap.** While the 89/6 upskilling gap gets the headlines, only one-third of organisations have data ready for agentic AI. Without solving data foundations, agent deployment produces hallucinating, non-compliant systems at scale. The EDPS's warning about reducing individuals to data points is not hypothetical — it describes the default trajectory.

Third, **experienced workers benefit most from agents, while junior workers benefit most from tools.** This SSRN finding inverts common assumptions and has profound implications for workforce planning, apprenticeship models, and the "diamond vs. hourglass" structural choice.

Fourth, **European co-determination is a design advantage, not a barrier.** Deutsche Telekom's AI Manifesto, ING's governance-first platform (90% pilot-to-production vs. 30% average), and the Nordic labour partnership model all suggest that institutionalised stakeholder engagement produces faster, more durable adoption than top-down mandates — Klarna's reversal being the cautionary counterexample.

Fifth, **the 40% cancellation prediction should discipline ambition.** Gartner's forecast that over 40% of agentic AI projects will fail by 2027 means European CEOs should focus on operating model redesign and governance architecture before scaling agent deployment. The companies succeeding — BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Schneider Electric — share one trait: they redesigned work around agents rather than inserting agents into existing work.
