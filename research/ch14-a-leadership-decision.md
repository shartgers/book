# Research Brief — Chapter 14 — A Leadership Decision

**Chapter purpose:** Conclusion and call to action. The choice every leadership team faces; the cost of waiting; what the Agentic Organisation looks like in the near future; who is building it today. Punchy, direct, memorable.
**Word budget:** 6% of book (~2,700 words at 45k)
**Key audiences:** European CEOs of mid-to-large organisations; sceptical, data-driven, time-poor.

---

## 1. The Choice (Framing and Evidence)

Evidence supporting the chapter's core argument that AI transformation is a binary leadership decision — lead or be left behind — and that the CEO must own it personally.

---

### 1.1 The binary framing — "Lead or lag"

**Claim:** AI transformation is no longer optional; CEOs face a binary outcome — lead or cease to exist.
**Source:** McKinsey, "How the Best CEOs Are Meeting the AI Moment," January 2026; McKinsey, "Lead or Lag: CEOs Must Embrace AI at Full Speed," 2026.
**Key details:** Eric Kutcher, McKinsey's North America Chair, states: "CEOs who take this on will take their organizations to a different level. And CEOs who sit and wait — their companies aren't going to exist. They're not going to thrive. It's that binary in terms of importance." Kutcher also frames AI as "80 percent business transformation and 20 percent tech transformation," which is "different from how most people have thought about it." This positions the transformation as fundamentally a leadership and organisational challenge, not a technology problem.
**Quote (Kutcher):** "You can't just rely on the technology — back to my 80/20. You have to take a step back and reimagine the process."
**Use for:** Opening framing; the choice is organisational, not technical.

---

### 1.2 AI is no longer an option

**Claim:** Global CEO consensus is that AI transformation has moved from discretionary to existential.
**Source:** Publicis Sapient CEO Nigel Vaz, January 2025; BCG AI Radar, January 2026.
**Key details:** Vaz: "I don't think AI is an option anymore and it is going to pervasively transform every aspect of every business." BCG reports that nearly three-quarters (72%) of CEOs now identify as their organisation's key decision-maker on AI — double the share from the previous year. Four out of five CEOs are more optimistic about AI ROI than a year ago.
**Use for:** Reinforcing the "no choice" framing; connect to Ch 2 (Leadership Is Not Optional).

---

### 1.3 "Don't delegate the revolution" — Paul Hudson, Sanofi

**Claim:** CEOs who delegate AI strategy to technical staff lose years and achieve nothing; the CEO must own AI personally.
**Source:** McKinsey interview with Paul Hudson, CEO of Sanofi, 2025–2026; Semafor, "How Paul Hudson Runs Sanofi's AI Fight Club," September 2025.
**Key details:** Hudson's principle: "You don't delegate the revolution." When delegation occurs, "five years go by, nothing happens." He admits he has "no interest in AI" and doesn't claim to "understand it" technically, but recognises that AI can synthesise insights better than traditional organisational approaches. Hudson established cross-functional "AI Fight Club" teams pairing non-AI experts to drive disruption from within. Result: Sanofi redeployed nearly €1 billion in real time using AI, reduced out-of-stock situations by 80%, and improved asset utilisation by over 10 percentage points in 2024.
**Quote (Hudson):** "You don't delegate the revolution."
**Use for:** Powerful CEO-voice quote for the choice section; shows that non-technical CEOs can and must lead; connects to Ch 2 thesis.

---

### 1.4 Decision triggers — what prompts CEOs to act

**Claim:** The decision to prioritise AI is increasingly triggered by competitive pressure, board accountability, talent flight, and regulatory deadlines rather than pure opportunity.
**Source:** PwC 29th Global CEO Survey, January 2026; BCG AI Radar, January 2026; EY CEO Outlook, January 2026.
**Key details:**
- **Competitive pressure:** 42% of CEOs cite "transforming fast enough to keep pace with technological change" as their top concern (PwC).
- **Job stability:** Half of CEOs believe their job stability depends on getting AI right in 2026 (PwC/WEF).
- **Board pressure:** More than half of non-CEO respondents believe the CEO or board should resign if the company loses market share due to an inadequate AI strategy (PwC/WEF).
- **Revenue confidence:** CEO confidence in revenue growth has fallen to a five-year low (30%), down from 38% in 2025 and 56% in 2022 (PwC).
- **Investment momentum:** Companies plan to double AI spending in 2026 (from ~0.8% to ~1.7% of revenues), with 94% continuing regardless of immediate returns (BCG).
**Use for:** Building urgency; the triggers are already firing across Europe.

---

### 1.5 Avoiding fatalism — "it's not too late, but the window is narrowing"

**Claim:** Laggards can still catch up, but only if they act within a narrow and closing window — roughly 18 months from early 2025.
**Source:** MAccelerator, "The 18-Month AI Window," 2025; BCG, "The Widening AI Value Gap," 2025.
**Key details:** The compounding mechanisms that widen the gap include: (a) network effects — AI systems gain value as they integrate more data and infrastructure; (b) reinvestment flywheels — leaders reinvest AI gains into new products and capabilities rather than just cutting costs; (c) talent gravity — the best AI talent migrates to organisations that are already scaling. What can be caught up later: some technology choices and tooling. What compounds and becomes irreversible: data assets, organisational culture, talent pipelines, and customer trust.
**Use for:** Balancing urgency with agency; "the window is narrowing, but it is still open for those who act now."

## 2. Cost of Waiting (Quantified and Narrative)

Evidence on the measurable and experiential costs of delay — competitive, talent, regulatory, and strategic.

---

### 2.1 The widening value gap — 2× revenue, 3.6× TSR

**Claim:** The performance gap between AI leaders and laggards is widening on every major financial metric.
**Source:** BCG, "AI Leaders Outpace Laggards," September 2025; BCG, "The Widening AI Value Gap," 2025.
**Key details:** "Future-built" companies (5% of firms globally) achieve: 1.7× revenue growth; 3.6× three-year total shareholder returns; 1.6× EBIT margin — versus laggards. Leaders also plan to spend more than twice as much on AI, creating a virtuous cycle. Currently, 60% of companies are classified as "laggards" reporting minimal value, 35% are "scalers" beginning to generate value, and only 5% are "future-built."
**Quote (BCG):** AI leaders achieve "double the revenue growth and 40% greater cost reductions" in areas where they apply AI.
**Use for:** Central quantification of the cost of waiting; connect to the "5% vs 95%" framing from Ch 1.

---

### 2.2 PwC's "defining divide"

**Claim:** Only 12% of CEOs report that AI has delivered both cost and revenue benefits; the gap between those 12% and the rest is accelerating.
**Source:** PwC 29th Global CEO Survey, January 2026 (4,454 CEOs across 95 countries).
**Key details:** 56% of CEOs say they've seen no significant financial benefit from AI to date. Companies that have scaled AI with strong foundations — including Responsible AI frameworks and enterprise-wide integration — are 3× more likely to report meaningful financial returns. Companies applying AI widely to products, services, and customer experiences achieved nearly 4 percentage points higher profit margins.
**Quote (Mohamed Kande, PwC Global Chairman):** "A small group of companies are already turning AI into measurable financial returns, while many others are still struggling to move beyond pilots. That gap is starting to show up in confidence and competitiveness — and it will widen quickly for those that don't act."
**Use for:** Powerful quote for the "cost of waiting" section; data-rich, CEO-credible source.

---

### 2.3 Talent: the brain drain accelerates

**Claim:** Europe is losing its best AI talent to organisations and regions that are already scaling, and the gap in leadership AI fluency is a critical bottleneck.
**Source:** Euronews, "The AI Brain Drain: Why Europe Can't Keep the Talent It Trains," January 2026; VeritaHR, "AI Talent Race in 2026," 2026; EE News Europe, "AI Leadership Gap Threatens European Competitiveness," 2025–2026.
**Key details:**
- AI job demand surging: +109% in Germany, +120% in the UK, +204% in Ireland in a single year.
- Nearly 40% of employers in finance and manufacturing cannot fill AI positions.
- Leadership gap: 63% of business leaders across UK, France, and Germany are unprepared to lead in an AI-enabled world. Only 49% of UK leaders describe themselves as AI-skilled (23% in France, 17% in Germany).
- "Convergent leaders" (AI-fluent + inclusive) report 93% productivity improvements versus 34% under non-convergent leaders.
- Only 28% of leaders plan for technology's long-term impact on employees.
**Use for:** European-specific cost of waiting; talent compounds — you can't hire your way out of a two-year deficit.

---

### 2.4 Regulatory: the EU AI Act as forcing function

**Claim:** The EU AI Act's August 2026 deadline for high-risk AI system compliance creates a forcing function — those who have been building responsible AI will be ready; those who haven't will face penalties and catch-up costs.
**Source:** EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689); HJ Automations compliance guide, 2026; EU AI Risk compliance resources, 2026.
**Key details:**
- **August 2, 2026:** Full compliance deadline for high-risk AI systems (risk management, technical documentation, human oversight, post-market monitoring).
- **Penalties:** Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited AI practices; up to €15 million or 3% for high-risk system violations; up to €7.5 million or 1.5% for procedural violations.
- **Beyond fines:** Building conformity infrastructure requires 6–12 months minimum — companies starting now are already behind. Third-party assessments cost €10,000–€100,000 per system.
- Companies that have invested in Responsible AI and governance (Allianz, BBVA, ING) will convert compliance into competitive advantage; laggards will be forced to comply defensively, wasting resources on catch-up rather than innovation.
**Use for:** European-specific urgency; compliance as opportunity (connect to Ch 9) vs compliance as penalty.

---

### 2.5 The compounding cost — what's reversible and what isn't

**Claim:** Some elements of AI capability can be acquired later (technology, tooling); others compound and become progressively harder — or impossible — to catch up.
**Source:** BCG, "The Widening AI Value Gap," 2025; MAccelerator, 2025; Dual Boot Partners, "Compounding AI Advantage," 2025–2026.
**Key details:**
- **Reversible (can catch up later):** Specific technology choices, cloud migration, tool selection, model fine-tuning.
- **Compounding (hard to catch up):** Data assets and proprietary training data; organisational culture and AI fluency; talent pipelines and institutional knowledge; customer trust and willingness to engage with AI-driven products; agent orchestration maturity and workflow redesign.
- **The reinvestment flywheel:** Leaders don't just save costs — they reinvest AI gains into new products, capabilities, and R&D, creating self-reinforcing competitive advantages that widen each cycle.
**Use for:** Making the cost of waiting tangible and specific; "the real cost isn't the technology you haven't bought — it's the capability you haven't built."

## 3. Three Years From Now (2027–2028)

Evidence on what the enterprise landscape will look like in the near future — technology, organisation, market, and regulation.

---

### 3.1 Gartner: 15% of decisions autonomous, 33% of software agentic by 2028

**Claim:** By 2028, a significant portion of enterprise decisions and software applications will be AI-driven and autonomous.
**Source:** Gartner, Strategic Technology Trends 2025; Gartner press releases, 2025–2026.
**Key details:**
- **15% of day-to-day work decisions** will be made autonomously through agentic AI by 2028, up from essentially 0% in 2024 — the fastest enterprise technology adoption curve since cloud infrastructure.
- **33% of enterprise software applications** will include agentic capabilities by 2028, compared to less than 1% in 2024.
- Autonomous decisions include: operational approvals, customer routing, scheduling, data processing, and risk assessment — high-volume, bounded decisions where mistakes are low-stakes and reversible.
- **73% of enterprise IT leaders** are already piloting or planning autonomous agent deployments for 2025.
- **Caveat:** Over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end of 2027 due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls.
**Use for:** Vivid "three years from now" scenario; balance optimism with realism (the 40% failure rate reinforces need for leadership).

---

### 3.2 BCG: 250% increase in AI decision-making authority; 76% view agents as coworkers

**Claim:** Organisations expect a dramatic increase in AI decision-making authority, and executives already view agents as colleagues, not tools.
**Source:** BCG and MIT Sloan Management Review, "The Emerging Agentic Enterprise," 2025; BCG, "What Happens When AI Stops Asking Permission?" 2025.
**Key details:**
- **250% more respondents** expect AI to have greater decision-making authority within three years.
- Currently only 10% of organisations have given AI decision-making powers; this is expected to rise to 35% within three years.
- **76% of executives** view agentic AI more as a coworker than a tool.
- **66% of leading organisations** anticipate operating model changes; 58% expect governance structure changes within three years.
- **35% of organisations** are already using agentic AI; another 44% plan to deploy soon.
**Use for:** "Three years from now" section; the shift from tool to teammate is already underway.

---

### 3.3 Deloitte: the agentic enterprise by 2028

**Claim:** By 2028, agentic AI will have evolved from human-supervised automation to autonomous systems making proactive decisions.
**Source:** Deloitte, "Agentic Enterprise 2028," 2025–2026; Deloitte, "Agentic AI Strategy," 2026.
**Key details:**
- Human roles shift from active participant to reviewer and monitor as agents gain autonomy.
- Market size: Autonomous AI agent market projected to reach $8.5 billion by 2026, $35 billion by 2030, potentially $45 billion with proper orchestration.
- Global agentic AI market expected to reach $103.6 billion by 2032.
- Only 14% of organisations currently have deployable solutions; 11% are using agentic systems in production.
- Agents may be viewed as a "silicon-based workforce" complementing human workers.
**Use for:** Scale of the shift; connects to Ch 3 (vision of the Agentic Organisation).

---

### 3.4 McKinsey: the agentic organisation as the next paradigm

**Claim:** The agentic organisation — where humans work alongside virtual and physical AI agents to create value at scale — represents the largest organisational shift since the industrial and digital revolutions.
**Source:** McKinsey, "The Agentic Organization: Contours of the Next Paradigm for the AI Era," 2025–2026; McKinsey, "The Future of Work Is Agentic," 2026; McKinsey, "One Year of Agentic AI: Six Lessons," 2026.
**Key details:**
- Agentic AI actively executes tasks by perceiving reality, deciding on actions with judgment, and executing them — then learning from results to improve. This is fundamentally different from reactive generative AI.
- Critical lesson from 50+ implementations: success depends on reimagining entire workflows, not just optimising the agent. Some organisations have rehired people after agent deployments failed.
- The transformation is happening "relatively quickly" and represents a "surprisingly near-term eventuality."
- Transitions that traditionally took 5–7 years will now take 1–3 years.
**Use for:** Connecting the book's central thesis (the Agentic Organisation) to the conclusion; "this is not a five-year plan — it's a one-to-three-year imperative."

---

### 3.5 The "slow-motion stress test" for CEOs (2026–2027)

**Claim:** The next 18 months represent a defining test of CEO leadership — those who default to tighter control will paradoxically fall behind.
**Source:** Fortune, "The Next 18 Months of the Agentic Era Will Feel Like a Slow-Motion Stress Test for CEOs," February 2026.
**Key details:** Most CEOs will make the same critical mistake: defaulting to tighter control and oversight in the face of uncertainty, which paradoxically slows the organisation. Companies that instead embrace genuine autonomy, agility, and trust in human-agent collaboration will pull ahead. Only 1% of companies have reached maturity despite nearly universal investment. The article frames 2026–2027 as the period where leaders and laggards permanently separate.
**Use for:** Urgency without hysteria; connects to the book's trust framework (Ch 8) and the idea that control is the enemy of speed.

## 5. Tone, Structure, and Closing

Guidance on how to land the conclusion — structure, call to action, and candidate closing lines.

---

### 5.1 Structural model — "Rewired" and comparable books

**Source:** McKinsey, "Rewired: The McKinsey Guide to Outcompeting in the Age of Digital and AI," 2023.
**Key details:** "Rewired" ends with three detailed case studies of companies that successfully completed digital transformations — proof that the playbook works. The book's implicit call to action: it is "for leaders who are ready to roll up their sleeves and do the hard work." The structure works because it provides closure (the journey is complete), proof (others have done it), and momentum (now it's your turn).
**Use for:** Structural guidance; our conclusion should mirror this: recap the thesis, show the builders, issue the call.

---

### 5.2 Recommended conclusion structure

Based on all research, the conclusion should follow a four-beat structure:

1. **The choice** (~500 words): Frame the binary — lead or lag. Use the McKinsey "80/20" quote and the Hudson "don't delegate" quote. Make it personal: "This is not a technology decision. This is a leadership decision."
2. **The cost of waiting** (~600 words): Quantify the gap (2× revenue, 3.6× TSR, 4pp margin). Name what compounds (data, culture, talent, trust) vs what can be caught up (tooling). Use the PwC/Kande quote.
3. **Three years from now** (~600 words): Paint the vivid picture — 15% of decisions autonomous, 33% of software agentic, agents as coworkers. Use two scenarios: the leader (Agentic Organisation) vs the laggard (still piloting). McKinsey's "1–3 years, not 5–7."
4. **Who is building it — and the call to action** (~1,000 words): Name the European builders (BBVA, ING, Siemens, Allianz, Klarna, Schneider, EQT). Name the Trailblazer profile. Issue specific calls: run the diagnostic (Ch 12), convene the board (Ch 13), choose one pilot, build the playbook (Ch 11). End on European advantage. Final line.

---

### 5.3 Candidate closing lines and calls to action

**Option A (challenge):** "The Agentic Organisation is not a prediction. It is being built — in Madrid, in Amsterdam, in Munich, in Stockholm, in Paris. The only question is whether you are building it, too."

**Option B (European pride):** "Europe has always built things that last — institutions, industries, infrastructure. The Agentic Organisation is the next thing to build. And Europe's CEOs are the ones to build it."

**Option C (binary):** Kutcher's framing: "CEOs who take this on will take their organisations to a different level. CEOs who sit and wait — their companies aren't going to exist."

**Option D (personal):** Hudson's simplicity: "You don't delegate the revolution."

**Recommended:** Combine Option A (the geography-specific European image) as the penultimate line, with Option D (Hudson's punch) as the very last line — or vice versa. The conclusion should feel European, specific, and direct.
