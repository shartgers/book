# AI transformation advances through four tiers — and skipping the first two explains why most fail

**The bottom line: A four-tier model — Individual → Team → Department → Organisation — aligns with how AI adoption actually unfolds in practice, even though no major consultancy formally frames it this way.** Evidence from McKinsey, BCG, MIT, and Microsoft consistently shows that individual fluency precedes organisational maturity, that teams are the critical but overlooked intermediate unit, and that **70–95% of AI transformations fail** largely because leaders invest in enterprise-level ambitions before building individual and team foundations. European CEOs face additional complexity: mandatory AI literacy obligations under the EU AI Act (effective February 2025), works council consultation requirements, and a regulatory culture that demands trust-first deployment — but this regulatory scaffolding, properly understood, can accelerate rather than hinder tier-by-tier adoption.

---

## How existing frameworks map to four tiers

No major consultancy publishes a model that explicitly follows the Individual → Team → Department → Organisation arc. McKinsey uses a five-stage deployment spectrum (Nascent → Emerging → Developing → Expanding → Mature), Gartner offers five maturity levels (Awareness → Active → Operational → Systemic → Transformational), Deloitte describes four levels (Starters → Pathseekers → Transformers → High-outcome organisations), and BCG distinguishes Laggards, Leaders, and Cutting-edge Leaders. All assess maturity at the organisational level, and most use **four to five stages** with naming conventions that include "tiers," "levels," "stages," "waves," and "maturity curves."

Two frameworks break the mould. **Harvard Business Publishing's AI-First Leadership model** explicitly starts with individual foundational knowledge before progressing to AI-first mindset, AI-related skills, and real-time integration. **Microsoft's Frontier Firm framework** (2025) describes three phases — personal AI assistant, digital colleague (team-level), and Frontier Firm (enterprise-level) — making it the closest vendor model to the four-tier structure.

What is consistent across all models is the progression arc: **awareness or experimentation → piloting or active use → scaling or integration → enterprise transformation**. What is unique about the four-tier framing is its explicit recognition of the team as a distinct adoption unit, filling a gap that most frameworks skip. McKinsey's "Superagency" report (January 2025) provides the strongest empirical support: **91% of employees already use generative AI at work**, but only **1% of companies report AI maturity** — proving that individual adoption massively outpaces organisational readiness. MIT's study of the "GenAI Divide" found that the strongest enterprise deployments "began with power users, employees who had already experimented with tools like ChatGPT," and that empowering line managers rather than centralised AI labs accelerated adoption. BCG's **10-20-70 principle** reinforces this: AI success is 10% algorithms, 20% data and technology, and **70% people, processes, and culture**.

The failure evidence is stark. BCG reports **74% of companies** struggle to achieve value from AI (survey of 1,000 CxOs across 59 countries). MIT found **95% of enterprise generative AI pilots** fail to deliver measurable returns (based on 300+ initiatives, though with a strict success definition). RAND found AI projects fail at **twice the rate** of non-AI IT projects (80%+). McKinsey's own estimate is that **fewer than 10% of generative AI use cases** make it past the pilot stage, and over **80% of companies** report no material contribution to earnings. The consistent thread: top-down, enterprise-first approaches that bypass individual fluency and team-level norms run aground on the "learning gap" — the distance between what AI can do and what the organisation's people actually know how to do with it.

## Tier 1: The individual as foundation

### Defining AI fluency

The most formalised definition comes from the **4D AI Fluency Framework** developed by Professors Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller (University College Cork, Ireland) in collaboration with Anthropic. They define AI fluency as "the ability to work effectively, efficiently, ethically, and safely within emerging modalities of Human-AI interaction," built on four competencies: **Delegation** (knowing what to assign to AI), **Description** (communicating effectively with AI), **Discernment** (evaluating outputs critically), and **Diligence** (maintaining ethical standards). The framework identifies 24 observable behaviours. Anthropic's research found that augmentative conversations — treating AI as a thinking partner — exhibit **more than double** the fluency behaviours compared to transactional interactions, making the distinction between "AI as thinking partner" and "AI as search tool" empirically measurable.

Prompt literacy, a subset of AI fluency, has been defined in the CAST model (Criteria, Audience, Specifications, Testing) by educators including Heidi Hayes Jacobs. Research published in *Frontiers in Education* (2024) confirmed that higher-quality prompt engineering skills **predict the quality of LLM output**, establishing prompt literacy as a trainable, assessable competency.

### How skill creation and sharing spread through organisations

The most striking Tier 1 evidence involves network effects and champion programmes. **Citi** built an internal network of **4,000+ "AI Accelerators"** across 182,000 employees in 84 countries, reaching over **70% adoption**. **PwC Netherlands** scaled from 300 AI enthusiasts to all 6,000 employees in roughly one year using organisational network analysis to identify naturally influential people — not just technically skilled ones — as champions. **HSBC** launched a five-tier AI Ambassador Network (from Business AI Ambassador to Executive AI Ambassador), with ambassadors facilitating hackathons globally. **Adobe** selected 50 ambassadors across every geography and sales role.

Shared prompt libraries are emerging as institutional IP. Companies using structured prompt libraries report teams become **40% more productive** by eliminating redundant prompt creation, and organisations with these libraries achieve **85% employee AI adoption** compared to 23% for individual-only usage. McKinsey's Lilli platform includes a saved-prompts sidebar with categories enabling firm-wide knowledge sharing.

### Governance without stifling experimentation

Only **28% of organisations** have a formal, comprehensive AI use policy (ISACA), and just **36% of employees** using generative AI report their organisation has a formal policy (EisnerAmper, 2025). Leading companies adopt a tiered approach: **green** (clearly approved uses like summarising meeting notes), **yellow** (requires review, such as customer-facing or hiring-related uses), and **red** (prohibited, such as inputting confidential data into public tools). McKinsey paired its Lilli rollout with **"RAI-Ready" boot camps** — two-hour sessions for every new agent owner — and quarterly town halls spotlighting near-misses to normalise learning from failure.

### Individual-level ROI: the data

The evidence for individual productivity gains is robust, though the macro impact remains debated.

**McKinsey's Lilli** is used by **over 75% of 43,000 employees** monthly, answering over **500,000 prompts per month**. McKinsey reports **30% time savings** on research and synthesis tasks. Each session eliminates roughly six minutes of manual document hunting, and research tasks that once took "weeks reduced to hours; hours to minutes."

The **Harvard/BCG "Jagged Frontier" study** (758 BCG consultants, 2023) found participants using GPT-4 completed **12.2% more tasks**, finished **25.1% faster**, and produced **over 40% higher quality** results. Below-average performers improved by **43%**. However, for tasks outside AI's capability frontier, consultants using AI were **19 percentage points less likely** to produce correct solutions — a critical validation finding.

The **Stanford/MIT customer support study** (5,179 agents at a Fortune 500 firm) found **14% average productivity increase**, with **34% improvement for novice workers**. Workers with two months' experience using AI performed as well as workers with six months' experience without it — demonstrating AI as a knowledge-transfer mechanism. Manager escalations declined **25%**.

**GitHub Copilot** studies show developers completing tasks **55.8% faster** in controlled settings, with field experiments at Microsoft showing **12.9–21.8% more pull requests** per week. Accenture's randomised controlled trial found **90% of developers felt more fulfilled** and reported an **84% increase** in successful builds.

A cautionary note: an eight-month HBR study (February 2026) found AI users "worked faster, took on a broader scope of tasks, and extended work into more hours of the day" — suggesting productivity gains may manifest as expanded scope rather than reduced hours.

## Tier 2: Teams are where adoption lives or dies

### Superusers and the two-in-the-box model

Superuser identification follows behavioural signals, not job titles. Effective champions come from non-technical functions and are identified through **organisational network analysis** (mapping informal influence), self-selection, and natural emergence. Three archetypes dominate: the Explorer (discovers use cases), the Translator (makes AI accessible), and the Strategist (connects AI to business outcomes).

The **"two-in-the-box" model** — co-leadership by a business domain expert and a technology/AI expert — is used at **Sun Life** (cross-functional teams of 8–12 co-led by business and technology owners), **Intel**, and **P&G/JLL** partnerships. Sun Life's CIO Laura Money notes these teams are "much more productive" because they eliminate the pattern of IT "throwing technology over the fence." For team-level AI agent coordination, emerging orchestration patterns include sequential (chained refinement), concurrent (parallel processing), handoff (dynamic delegation), and hierarchical (supervisor-worker). The critical lesson from practitioners: **"Most 'agent failures' are actually orchestration and context-transfer issues."**

### Psychological safety is the strongest predictor of AI adoption success

The **MIT Technology Review/Infosys survey** (500 business leaders, December 2025) is the most directly relevant study: **83% of executives** say psychological safety measurably improves the success of AI initiatives, and **84%** report direct links to tangible business outcomes. Yet **22% of leaders** admit they have hesitated to lead an AI project for fear of blame if it fails, and only **39%** rate their organisation's psychological safety as "very high."

Amy Edmondson, who defined psychological safety and ranks #2 on Thinkers50 (2025), co-authored "How to Foster Psychological Safety When AI Erodes Trust on Your Team" (HBR, February 2026), arguing that **AI adoption should be treated as a team development effort, not just a technology upgrade**. Google's Project Aristotle (180+ teams, 2012–2014) established psychological safety as the #1 predictor of team effectiveness, above dependability, structure, meaning, and impact. While the original study was internal, its findings were validated by the 2019 DORA State of DevOps report across the software industry.

A Nature study (2025, 381 South Korean employees) found that AI adoption has a **significant negative impact on psychological safety**, which in turn increases depression levels — with ethical leadership moderating this effect. The Slack Workforce Index (Fall 2024) found **48% of workers are uncomfortable admitting AI use** to their manager, fearing they'll appear "incompetent, lazy, or cheating." Companies with explicit AI permissions see workers **6× more likely** to experiment, and trained workers are **up to 19× more likely** to report productivity improvements.

Team norms like **"Have you asked the AI?"** are emerging as standard practice, alongside dedicated Slack/Teams "AI Wins" channels, meetings opening with quick AI wins, and structured "AI hack weeks" with no expectation of success.

### Team metrics: throughput rises but quality needs watching

A Nielsen Norman Group meta-analysis found an **average 66% productivity improvement** across customer service (13.8% more inquiries/hour), business writing (59% more documents/hour), and programming (126% more projects/week). However, a Cortex engineering report found that while AI coding assistants delivered a **20% increase** in pull requests per author, incidents per pull request **jumped 23.5%** and change failure rates **increased 30%**. The Faros AI Productivity Paradox Report (10,000+ developers, 1,255 teams) found **no significant correlation** between team-level AI adoption and company-level improvements in DORA metrics — suggesting downstream bottlenecks absorb team-level gains.

Pilot-to-production rates remain a critical barrier. IDC reports that for every 33 AI proofs-of-concept launched, only 4 graduate to production — an **88% failure rate**. The Concentrix/Everest Group (2025) found only **27%** successfully moved generative AI from testing to implementation.

## Tier 3: Department-level transformation redesigns workflows, not just tools

### From standalone agents to swarms

McKinsey's **Agentic AI Mesh Architecture** (published by QuantumBlack) describes a composable, distributed architecture where agents progress from **solo copilot-style tools** to **multi-agent collaboration** (negotiating, verifying, task-sharing) to **swarms of specialised agents** coordinating autonomously across systems. The architecture layers orchestration, memory, integration, governance, and interface components. A key structural insight: a human team of **two to five people can supervise an "agent factory" of 50–100 specialised agents** running an end-to-end process.

**PwC's finance workflow model** illustrates departmental redesign: Agent 1 extracts invoice information, Agent 2 pulls the relevant contract, Agent 3 compares terms and flags discrepancies, Agent 4 drafts a resolution email — only then does a human specialist intervene. This can **reduce cycle times by up to 80%** while improving audit trails.

### Minimum Viable Organisations in practice

The MVO concept — an organisation operating with minimal human oversight supported by agent swarms — lacks a single authoritative academic definition but is gaining traction. **msg advisors** (German consultancy) defines it as "a process model for continuous advancement, helping create a highly adaptive organisation" where individual units have digital tools, know-how, and mindset to independently evolve. Sam Altman's "one-person billion-dollar company" vision and Microsoft's Frontier Firm concept represent the consumer and enterprise versions respectively.

MVOs are emerging earliest in **financial services** (autonomous procurement, compliance review), **customer service** (PwC predicts 95% of customer interactions will be AI-powered), **legal** (Harvey AI, valued at $3 billion, with AI cutting complaint response drafting from 16 hours to 3–4 minutes), and **logistics** (Gartner predicts 25% of supply chain decisions by AI-driven swarms by 2030). **Unilever's** autonomous procurement agents negotiate with suppliers independently, delivering **up to $250 million in annual savings**.

However, Carnegie Mellon research found that even the best-performing AI agents **fail to complete real-world office tasks 70% of the time**, and the HurumoAI experiment (a startup run entirely by AI agents) produced fabricated deliverables and cost overruns. MVOs remain sector-specific and narrow-domain for now.

### Department-level case studies with metrics

**The Kore.ai wealth management example** involves a global wealth management firm serving 120,000 employees. Phased deployment began with content creation tools (Email Wizard, Marketing Muse) and progressed to client management, where **Meeting Prep reduced preparation time from 2–3 hours to 15 minutes** by consolidating email, CRM, and portfolio data. Results include a **300% rise in document findability** (from 20% to 80% accessibility), a **22% increase in employee satisfaction**, and a 12% reduction in time spent on information retrieval.

European examples are particularly instructive. **Siemens** (Erlangen Digital Lighthouse Factory) deployed AI across 100+ use cases, achieving a **69% boost in productivity** and **42% reduction in energy use** over four years. **Airbus** trained 10,000+ engineers on GitHub Copilot, resulting in a **40% improvement in simulation cycle times**. **TRY** (Norway) reduced staff time on repetitive tasks by **30%** and accelerated proposal creation by **40%** using Claude. **Celonis** (Munich), the process intelligence leader with 1,400+ enterprise customers, enables companies like PepsiCo to unlock "millions of dollars" through AI-powered process mining. The **EY European AI Barometer 2025** reports that 56% of European organisations have increased profits or reduced costs through AI, with an average financial impact of **€6.24 million per firm**.

In HR, **Unilever** reduced time-to-hire from six months to eight weeks and cut interview assessment time by **70,000 person-hours annually**. In marketing, **JP Morgan Chase/Persado** AI-generated copy achieved a **450% increase in ad click-through rates**. In finance, IBM benchmarks show mature AI adopters complete the annual budget cycle **33% faster** and cut accounts payable costs per invoice by **25%**.

### Who leads and how departments avoid silos

Department-level AI is typically led by the **Chief AI Officer** (emerging role providing centralised leadership while enabling decentralised innovation), the **Chief Data Officer** (positioned by Deloitte as "both the architect and guardian"), or the functional/business-unit leader with support from a centralised governance function. The NACD governance framework recommends accountability remain with the functional leader, supported by a centralised AI technology and governance leader. AI initiatives typically require **3–5% of annual revenue** for meaningful transformation, yet only **11% of boards** have approved an annual AI budget.

## Tier 4: The organisational North Star and what makes enterprise transformation succeed

### AI steering committees and governance structures

Effective AI steering committees include representation from legal, ethics/compliance, privacy, information security, R&D, product management, operations, and business unit leads. The NIRS charter model specifies seven officers including an Executive Sponsor (CEO), Enterprise Architecture Representative, and Enterprise Risk Lead, plus rotating members for diverse perspectives. Committees typically set strategic direction with day-to-day reviews delegated to smaller working groups. Cadence ranges from bi-quarterly charter reviews to monthly operational meetings.

European board-level AI governance lags. Glass Lewis research (2025 proxy season) found only **20.7% of large-cap European companies** had formal AI policies in place, 18% referenced AI risks in annual reports, and **61.3% had no AI policy disclosed**. Communications (54.6%) and IT (38.5%) sectors lead; financials trail at 24.1%. Critically, **9 in 10 European investors** rejected the notion that it is "too early" to hold boards accountable for AI.

### The McKinsey trust-revenue finding: what it actually says

The precise statistic from McKinsey's "Why Digital Trust Truly Matters" (September 2022, survey of 1,333 executives across 27 industries in 20 countries) is that **digital-trust leaders are 1.6× more likely** than the global average to see revenue and EBIT growth rates of at least 10%. "Digital-trust leaders" are defined as companies whose employees follow codified data, AI, and ethics policies and engage in at least half of best practices for AI, data, and cybersecurity. A subsequent McKinsey finding from the State of AI 2024 survey, reported in *Fortune* (October 2025), states that companies where generative AI accounts for at least 10% of earnings are "committed trust-builders" and are **twice as likely to see 10%+ revenue growth**. Both findings reinforce that trust is a precondition for AI-driven value, not a byproduct.

### The global retailer and other enterprise examples

The McKinsey "global retailer" example comes from "Agents for Growth" (November 2025). A global retailer sees demand for a product surge in one region while inventory piles up in another; **within seconds**, a team of AI agents reallocates ad spend, adjusts pricing, reroutes stock, and refreshes creative assets to match shopper intent. McKinsey describes this as "not a fantasy scenario; it's the new frontier." The retailer is anonymous, and the example is illustrative rather than a named case study.

More specific results come from a **European insurer** in the same article: AI agents personalising campaigns across hundreds of microsegments produced **conversion rates 2–3× higher** and **25% shorter call times**, deployed in 16 weeks. Knowledge agents centralised 1,000+ documents; coaching agents reviewed **95% of sales calls** versus 3% previously. A US airline achieved **210% improvement** in targeting at-risk customers and an **800% rise in customer satisfaction**.

### Scaling: what breaks and what works

What breaks at the department-to-enterprise boundary includes data fragmentation, pilot purgatory (70–90% of pilots never reach production), organisational silos, governance vacuums, and infrastructure that cannot handle AI-driven demand (**83% of leaders** believe AI demand will cause data infrastructure to fail without major upgrades within 24 months). The Centre of Excellence evolves through three models: **centralised** (strong governance, risk of bottlenecks) in the first 12 months; **hub-and-spoke** (central standards, distributed execution) at 12–24 months; and **federated** (autonomous pods with shared governance) at 24–36 months. An AI CoE is already established in **37% of large US companies**.

McKinsey's "Rewired" analysis of 200+ large-scale transformations found companies captured, on average, only **31% of expected revenue lift** and **25% of expected cost savings** — but organisations that invested in cultural change saw **5.3× higher success rates** than those focused only on technology.

## What leaders must do differently at each tier

### The leadership behaviour shift

At **Tier 1**, the CEO's role is to be a visible user of AI, model experimentation, and signal psychological safety. BCG's AI Radar 2026 (640 CEOs, 2,360 leaders) shows that C-level executives deeply engaged with AI are **12× more likely** to be among the top 5% of companies winning with AI. Only **15% of CEOs** qualify as "trailblazers" who have personally upskilled and championed AI adoption across nearly three-quarters of their workforce. Half of CEO respondents believe their job stability depends on getting AI right.

At **Tier 2**, the CEO becomes a connector — linking early adopters across teams, celebrating peer-to-peer learning, and establishing light governance. This is where most transformations stall. A Typeface Signal Report (2025) found 61% of AI use remains at the individual level, without collaborative platforms. Middle managers are the bridge: BearingPoint research shows they must become "orchestrators of AI-human collaboration," yet only **35% of companies** have structured change management programmes.

At **Tier 3**, the CEO shifts to strategist and governor — approving department-level investments, ensuring cross-department coordination, and addressing resistance. The O'Reilly 2024 survey found only 26% of AI initiatives advanced beyond pilot phase, with 74% stalling due to operational and organisational barriers.

At **Tier 4**, the CEO is the architect of transformation — driving operating model redesign, replacing legacy systems, and articulating AI as fundamental to corporate strategy. Active executive sponsors make firms **1.8× more likely** to scale AI effectively (BCG).

### The most common mistakes

The readiness gap is the single biggest leadership failure. **Huble research** found 57% of leaders say they are AI-ready, but **only 8% actually are**. Multiverse's study of 810 leaders and 1,190 employees revealed that **45% of organisations** categorised as "AI Beginners" considered AI "fully implemented." Gartner found **66% of CEOs** say their executive teams lack AI confidence.

The three critical mistakes are: **(1)** over-investing in enterprise-level (Tier 4) initiatives before building individual and team foundations — jumping to AI strategy committees and enterprise platforms while employees lack basic AI fluency; **(2)** treating all tiers with the same governance — applying enterprise-level controls to individual experimentation stifles learning, while applying startup-level informality to department-wide deployments creates risk; and **(3)** ignoring skill creation and sharing — treating AI as a technology deployment rather than a capability-building exercise. BCG data shows that less than one-third of companies have upskilled even one-quarter of their workforce in AI.

## The European difference: regulation as accelerator

### EU AI Act creates mandatory Tier 1 obligations

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) makes AI literacy a legal requirement. **Article 4** mandates that organisations promote AI literacy among all staff interacting with AI systems — effective **2 February 2025**. This creates a regulatory floor for Tier 1 that does not exist in the US or Asia. High-risk AI systems (including those used in HR recruitment, credit scoring, and critical infrastructure) face extensive obligations by August 2026, including risk management, human oversight, and conformity assessments. Penalties reach **up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover** for prohibited practices.

GDPR Article 22 establishes a general prohibition on decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or significant effects, with mandatory safeguards including the right to human intervention, the right to express one's point of view, and the right to contest decisions. This directly shapes Tier 3 and Tier 4 deployment, requiring human-in-the-loop design for any automated decision-making affecting employees or customers.

### Works councils shape the pace and process of adoption

In **Germany**, §87(1) No. 6 of the Works Constitution Act (BetrVG) grants works councils **co-determination rights** over technical equipment that could monitor employee behaviour — the employer cannot proceed without consent. A Hamburg Labour Court ruling (January 2024) clarified that if AI tools are used through company accounts (enabling monitoring), co-determination is triggered. Siemens, Deutsche Telekom, and IBM Germany have negotiated "agile company agreements" on AI using ethical frameworks. In **France**, a landmark Nanterre Court ruling held that deploying AI tools even in "pilot phase" on select employees required **prior works council consultation** — meaning European companies cannot run stealth pilots without employee representation engagement. In the **Nordic countries**, co-determination rights are deeply embedded, with Norway recognising a dedicated "data shop steward" role in the 2022–25 Main Agreement.

EU AI Act Article 26(7) requires employers to inform employee representatives "in a clear and comprehensive manner" before deploying high-risk AI systems. Best practice: engage in constructive dialogue **well in advance** of acquiring any AI system.

### How European firms can turn regulation into competitive advantage

The European approach — framing AI within trustworthy AI principles (human agency, transparency, fairness, accountability) — aligns naturally with tier-by-tier adoption. Mandatory AI literacy creates budget justification for Tier 1 investment. Works council consultation builds the psychological safety essential for Tier 2. GDPR's human-in-the-loop requirements enforce the governance guardrails that prevent Tier 3 and Tier 4 failures. European companies like Siemens ("AI with Purpose"), Bosch (measured, augmentation-focused messaging), and the 13-company European AI Initiative (Airbus, ASML, AXA, BNP Paribas, Deutsche Bank, L'Oréal, Lufthansa, Mercedes-Benz, Orange, Porsche, Siemens, Spotify, Volkswagen) demonstrate that responsible deployment and competitive ambition are not mutually exclusive. Eurostat (2025) shows EU AI enterprise adoption reached **20%** (up from 13.5% in 2024), with Denmark at 42%, Finland at 37.8%, and Sweden at 35%.

## Why transformations stall — and what successful companies do differently

Transformations most commonly stall at the **Tier 2–Tier 3 boundary** — the transition from team-level experimentation to department-level workflow redesign. The core challenge is organisational, not technical. Pilots sit in innovation labs or IT groups with limited sponsorship from finance or operations. Nobody with operational authority has ownership. Data quality degrades moving from clean pilot environments to messy production reality (Gartner: 85% of AI projects fail due to poor data quality). Middle management resistance compounds the problem: managers fear role elimination, lack AI fluency, and see AI adoption as an extra burden rather than an enabler.

Successful companies differ in five ways. They **tie AI directly to revenue-generating workflows**, not peripheral experiments. They ensure **active executive sponsorship** beyond mere approval — leaders personally use AI tools and communicate regularly. They **invest in people before technology**, following BCG's 10-20-70 split. They create **clear kill/scale criteria** for pilots, avoiding indefinite experimentation. And they build **cross-functional teams from day one**, breaking the handoff between data science and deployment.

The gap between individual adoption and organisational maturity — what McKinsey calls the "superagency gap" — is the defining challenge. Employees are 3× more likely than leaders expect to use AI for 30%+ of daily work. The path through the four tiers is not optional; it reflects how adoption actually unfolds. The companies that recognise this — investing in individual fluency, building psychologically safe teams, redesigning departmental workflows, and only then scaling enterprise-wide — are the ones that will capture the **$2.6–$4.4 trillion** in annual value McKinsey estimates generative AI can unlock.

## Conclusion: what the evidence demands

The four-tier model is not just a theoretical framework — it is the empirically observed pattern of successful AI adoption. Every major consultancy's failure data points to the same conclusion: organisations that skip individual fluency and team norms to pursue enterprise-level transformation are building on sand. The **1% maturity rate** McKinsey reports is not a technology problem; it is a sequencing problem.

For European CEOs, three insights stand out. First, the EU AI Act's mandatory AI literacy obligation is not a compliance burden — it is a **strategic accelerator for Tier 1**, providing regulatory justification for the individual fluency investment that most organisations underweight. Second, works council engagement, often seen as a friction point, builds the **trust and psychological safety** that MIT/Infosys research shows is essential for Tier 2 success. Third, the department-level MVO vision — agent swarms with minimal human oversight — is real and emerging in finance, procurement, legal, and logistics, but the **70% agent task-failure rate** means human governance remains non-negotiable.

The sequence matters. Individual fluency creates the foundation. Team norms and psychological safety create the permission to experiment. Department-level workflow redesign creates measurable business impact. And enterprise-wide scaling creates sustained competitive advantage. The CEOs who understand this progression — and resist the temptation to skip tiers — will be the ones who close the gap between AI's promise and its impact.
