# The Agentic Organisation: Comprehensive Research Foundation for a European CEO Business Book

**The largest organizational paradigm shift since the industrial revolution is underway, and European CEOs are dangerously underprepared.** Only 5% of companies achieve AI value at scale, 95% of enterprise AI implementations fail to deliver sustained impact, and 66% of board members admit limited-to-no AI knowledge. Yet the companies that get this right—BBVA, Siemens, Allianz, ING—are achieving 2–3x conversion improvements, 80% reductions in processing time, and hundreds of millions in measurable value. This research report synthesizes the landscape across 10 core topics to inform a definitive European-focused book on leading the transformation toward becoming an Agentic Enterprise.

## 1. Defining the Agentic Enterprise: what the major consultancies are saying

### McKinsey's landmark framing

McKinsey published "The Agentic Organization" in September 2025, calling it **"the largest organizational paradigm shift since the industrial and digital revolutions."** They define what this book calls the Agentic Enterprise as one that "unites humans and AI agents—both virtual and physical—to work side by side at scale at near-zero marginal cost." Their five pillars are: Business Model, Operating Model, Governance, Workforce/People/Culture, and Technology/Data.

A critical McKinsey data point: **AI task completion ability doubled every 7 months since 2019 and every 4 months since 2024**, reaching approximately 2 hours of autonomous work as of late 2025. By 2027, AI systems could potentially complete 4 days of work without supervision. McKinsey envisions organization charts pivoting toward "agentic networks" where "one manager with a small human team could orchestrate hundreds of agents acting autonomously in a workflow cutting across silos, running 24/7."

Their June 2025 CEO Playbook identifies the **"Gen AI Paradox"**: nearly 8 in 10 companies report using gen AI, yet just as many report no significant bottom-line impact. Approximately 90% of transformative use cases remain stuck in pilot mode. The remedy: a central "agentic factory" responsible for identifying workflows, managing redesign, and scaling, supported by an "agentic AI mesh" architecture.

### BCG's "frontal cortex" metaphor

BCG frames the evolution elegantly: **predictive AI is the "left brain" (logic), generative AI is the "right brain" (creativity), and agentic AI is the "frontal cortex" (executive function—planning, judgment, orchestration)**. Their 10/20/70 rule is a cornerstone insight: algorithms account for just 10% of effort, technology backbone 20%, and **people and processes 70%**.

BCG's joint research with MIT Sloan (November 2025, surveying 2,102 executives) reveals that **76% of executives now view agentic AI as a "coworker" rather than a tool**, 35% of companies already use agentic AI, and 95% of employees in organizations with extensive agentic AI adoption report improved job satisfaction. Perhaps most striking: organizations expect a **250% increase in AI decision-making authority within 3 years**.

BCG's agent autonomy progresses through four tiers: Shadow Mode (humans validate all outputs), increasing supervised autonomy, agent-led with oversight, and Full Autonomy (human out of the loop, reserved for mature, low-risk environments).

### Deloitte, Accenture, Gartner, and Forrester

Deloitte describes agentic AI as **"the operating logic of the future"** and coins the term "agentification" for the race to transform enterprise processes. Their prediction: 25% of companies using GenAI will launch agentic AI pilots in 2025, growing to 50% by 2027. Deloitte's research found that **80% of work in deploying AI agents was consumed by "unglamorous tasks"**—data engineering, stakeholder alignment, governance, workflow integration.

Accenture's Technology Vision 2025 emphasizes that **"systems will only ever be as autonomous as they are trustworthy."** Their platform research shows companies aligning AI, platforms, and business strategies achieve **2.2x revenue growth and 37% EBITDA lift**.

Gartner places AI agents at the **Peak of Inflated Expectations** on their 2025 Hype Cycle, while GenAI slides into the Trough of Disillusionment. Their starkest warning: **over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027** due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls. Only approximately 130 of thousands of agentic AI vendors are "real"—the rest are engaged in "agent washing."

Forrester introduces the dual identity of an agent: **Agent as a Skill (discrete cognitive capability) and Agent as a Product (platform-resident with dependencies, governance, telemetry)**. This creates two parallel roadmaps—a Skills Roadmap and a Foundations/Platform Roadmap—that must be synchronized.

### The fundamental distinction: tool-level vs. organizational-level agentic AI

Using AI agents as tools means optimizing existing processes. Building an Agentic Enterprise means redesigning how decisions are made, how work flows, and how humans and machines collaborate. As McKinsey frames it: **the CEO must ask not "How do we add AI?" but "How do we want decisions to be made, work to flow, and humans to engage in an environment where software can act?"** BCG's complementary framing: "AI is transforming the way work gets done, but it hasn't fundamentally altered the way most companies operate—yet. That's about to change."

## 2. Why bottom-up fails and top-down is non-negotiable

### The 95% failure rate is a transformation problem, not a technology problem

The failure data converges dramatically across sources. The **MIT NANDA Report (2025)** finds 95% of enterprise generative AI implementations fail to deliver sustained, measurable impact. **BCG** reports only 5% of 1,250+ firms are achieving AI value at scale, with 60% achieving no material value at all. The **RAND Corporation** puts AI project failure at over 80%—double non-AI IT failure rates. **S&P Global** reports 42% of companies scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the year before.

Bain & Company coined the critical concept of the **"micro-productivity trap"**: a proliferation of proofs of concept and isolated use cases delivering modest, localized efficiency gains that fail to scale. Their assessment is unequivocal: *"Grassroots experimentation with AI tools sparks innovation and cultural momentum—all good—but it does not self-organize into enterprise-wide impact. Without clear direction from the top, these efforts remain fragmented, siloed, and ultimately shallow."*

An alarming additional dimension: **1 in 3 workers actively sabotage corporate AI rollouts**, and 41% of millennial and Gen Z employees engage in non-compliance. Meanwhile, 75% of organizations report being at or past the change saturation point.

### The CEO is the single strongest predictor of AI success

Organizations with strong executive sponsorship achieve **AI success rates 6x higher** than those where leadership delegates to IT or middle management. BCG's AI Radar shows **C-level executives deeply engaged with AI are 12x more likely** to be among the top 5% of companies winning with AI. In 2025, **72% of respondents identify the CEO as the primary decision-maker on AI**, up sharply from one-third the year before.

Eric Kutcher, McKinsey North America Chair, captured the essence: **"This is probably the biggest, most complex business transformation—but it's 80% business transformation and 20% tech transformation. That's different from how most people have thought about it."**

BCG identifies "Trailblazer" CEOs (15% of the total) who spend 6+ hours weekly upskilling on AI, commit 3x the investment, and invest 2x in workforce upskilling. Half of all CEOs believe their job stability depends on getting AI right. Among non-CEOs, more than half believe the CEO or board should resign if the company loses market share due to inadequate AI strategy.

### The CEO mental model shift that the book must drive

Most CEOs currently frame AI as an IT project, a cost center, an experiment, or a tool to bolt onto existing processes. The required mental model reframes AI as **strategic transformation, operating model redesign, and competitive necessity**. Bain's concept of "zero-based process design" captures this: true impact requires redesigning processes from scratch with AI at the core—not layering AI on top of legacy workflows.

The most compelling analogy is the **"horseless carriage" to "automobile"** shift. Organizations are putting AI on top of existing processes (horseless carriage thinking) when they need to redesign everything from scratch (automobile thinking). As Mark Bonchek frames it: "Until we make an exponential shift in our mental model from spreadsheets to algorithms, AI tech will only give us an incrementally better spreadsheet."

## 3. The five elements: Products, People, Process, Technology, Data

### Products and customer journeys

AI is creating an entirely new category—**AI-native products** that could not exist without AI: ChatGPT, Spotify's Discover Weekly, autonomous vehicles, real-time neural translation, GitHub Copilot. The most critical shift is from static, linear customer journeys to dynamic, adaptive, AI-powered journeys that update in real time.

The discovery landscape itself is transforming. Bain reports **~80% of consumers now rely on "zero-click" AI results in 40%+ of searches**, with organic web traffic reduced 15–25%. This is, as Adobe puts it, "the most consequential shift in discovery since the search bar."

**80% of Netflix viewing hours** come from AI-powered recommendations, not search. **35% of Amazon purchases** come from personalized suggestions. The B2B world is following: 73% of B2B buyers now want personalized, B2C-like experiences.

### People and culture

McKinsey's "Superagency" report finds the biggest barrier to scaling AI is not employees—who are ready—but **leaders, who are not steering fast enough**. Employees are already using AI 3x more than their leaders realize. The World Economic Forum projects **170 million new jobs by 2030 with 92 million displaced**, and 59% of the global workforce will need reskilling by 2030.

McKinsey's five-step change management framework moves employees from "experimenters" to "accelerators": craft a North Star based on outcomes (not tools), build trust with governance, reimagine workflows toward AI teams, identify and activate superusers, and build a continual learning ecosystem. Companies investing in trust-enabling activities are **2x more likely to see 10%+ revenue growth**.

The most powerful internal example is McKinsey's own "Lilli" AI platform: **92% usage by global staff, 74% regular usage**, saving 30%+ time on information gathering. The cultural hack: leadership asking "Have you asked Lilli?" in every meeting.

### Process transformation across business functions

Agentic AI is transforming every major business function with measurable results:

- **Marketing**: Campaign creation and execution sped up **15-fold** at Fortune 250 companies. A European insurer achieved **2–3x higher conversion rates** and **25% shorter call times** in just 16 weeks.
- **Sales**: Companies with agentic AI report **35% reduction in customer acquisition costs**, **28% improvement in deal velocity**, and **40% faster rep ramp-up time**. A US homebuilder trained AI on 500,000+ transcripts and **tripled conversion-to-appointment rates**.
- **Customer service**: Salesforce's own deployment handles **32,000 conversations per week** with **85% autonomous resolution**—exceeding Gartner's 2029 prediction of 80% four years early. Projected **$50 million in annual cost savings**. Klarna's AI handled 2.3 million conversations monthly—equivalent to 700 agents—but later partially reversed course, re-hiring humans for complex emotional cases.
- **Software development**: GitHub Copilot now has **20 million users**, generates **46% of code** written by developers, and delivers **55% faster task completion**. Pull request time reduced from 9.6 days to 2.4 days. However, DORA 2025 reports individual speed gains don't always translate to organizational improvements.
- **Finance**: PwC reports procure-to-pay AI agents slash cycle times by **up to 80%**. KPMG predicts **80% of finance will be automated within three years**. AP adoption jumped fourfold to 29% in a single year.
- **HR**: AI adoption in HR climbed from **19% (2023) to 61% (2025)**. LinkedIn's Hiring Assistant automates up to 80% of the recruitment workflow. Paradox helped Chipotle reduce time-to-hire by 75%.

### Technology: the MCP revolution and skills-based architecture

The **Model Context Protocol (MCP)**, launched by Anthropic in November 2024, is becoming the "USB-C for AI"—a universal standard for connecting AI agents to enterprise tools and data. OpenAI adopted MCP in March 2025, Google in April, Microsoft in May. By December 2025, Anthropic donated MCP to the **Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation**, co-founded with OpenAI and Block, with AWS, Google, and Microsoft as supporting members. Metrics: **97 million monthly SDK downloads, 5,800+ MCP servers, 300+ MCP clients**.

The complementary **Agent2Agent (A2A) protocol** from Google handles agent-to-agent communication. Together, MCP (agent-to-tool) and A2A (agent-to-agent) form the two-protocol stack for enterprise agentic systems.

The emerging enterprise architecture is a seven-layer stack: Infrastructure → Data Foundation → Foundation Models → RAG & Knowledge → Skills/Tools (MCP) → Agent Frameworks & Orchestration → Governance & Observability. Key platforms include **Kore.ai** (enterprise agent orchestration, Forrester Leader), **Microsoft Copilot Studio** (deep Microsoft ecosystem integration with MCP), **Salesforce Agentforce** (CRM-native agents), **n8n** (open-source workflow automation with native MCP), and developer frameworks like LangChain, CrewAI, and AutoGen.

### Data as strategic asset

The World Economic Forum reports **fewer than 1 in 5 organizations have high maturity in any aspect of data readiness**, yet 72% of business leaders say they will prioritize data foundations as their fastest-growing AI investment. Gartner finds **57% of organizations estimate their data is not AI-ready**. Only 12% of organizations globally report having truly AI-ready data.

McKinsey emphasizes **"walled data gardens" as competitive advantage**—proprietary data becomes the key differentiator when foundation models commoditize. The practical path to data readiness follows a 120-day roadmap: establish unified AI/data foundation (Days 0–30), introduce governance/policy controls (Days 30–90), begin secure AI operationalization (Days 90–120).

## 4. The four-tier transformation model

The transformation journey progresses through four tiers, each requiring different governance, culture, tools, and ROI measurement:

**Tier 1 — Individual level**: Personal AI fluency—prompting, validating outputs, ethical use. McKinsey's Lilli saves 30%+ time on information gathering per person. Key shift: from "using AI as a search tool" to "AI as a thinking partner." Governance is personal responsibility for data handling and recognizing hallucinations.

**Tier 2 — Team level**: Teams identify "superusers" and build team norms ("Have you asked the AI?" becomes standard protocol). Google's Project Aristotle showed psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team performance. Teams evolve from stand-alone AI agents to coordinating agent groups. The "two-in-the-box" approach pairs business and technology leads to co-define new working modes.

**Tier 3 — Department level**: End-to-end workflow reimagination within a function. McKinsey's phased approach: stand-alone agents for discrete tasks → agent groups for full workflows → agentic swarms for autonomous operations. Some departments evolve toward **Minimum Viable Organizations (MVOs)**—agent swarms with few human overseers. The Kore.ai wealth management case sits here: meeting prep time for advisors reduced from **2–3 hours to 15 minutes**, with a 300% rise in document findability and 22% employee satisfaction increase.

**Tier 4 — Cross-functional/organizational level**: Enterprise-wide transformation with an AI oversight committee, enterprise "North Star," and cross-functional task forces. McKinsey describes a global retailer detecting demand surges in one region and inventory pileup in another, where AI agents reallocate ad spend, adjust pricing, reroute stock, and refresh creative assets within seconds. Companies investing in trust-enabling activities at this level are **2x more likely to achieve 10%+ revenue growth**.

## 5. The three angles: Value, Capabilities, Trust

### Value creation

BCG's Deploy-Reshape-Invent (DRI) framework offers the clearest value taxonomy. **Deploy** off-the-shelf tools for 10–15% productivity boost. **Reshape** functions end-to-end with AI workflows. **Invent** new business models and revenue streams. BCG data shows AI-mature companies generate **72% of their AI value in core functions** (operations, marketing, sales), not support functions. Roland Berger PE data quantifies specific value: procurement AI saved 7% across indirect spend in under 10 weeks, dynamic pricing generated $5M extra margin in one quarter, and AI forecasting decreased errors by 25%.

### Capabilities required

Beyond technology, organizations need cross-functional teams, agile operating models, experimentation culture, and new roles: Agent Orchestrators, AI Product Managers, MLOps Engineers. McKinsey's insight: the critical "domain leader" (N-2/N-3 executive) who translates vision into operational change is "probably the single most critical role any business needs for its AI transformations." Only 6% of companies have begun upskilling in a "meaningful way" despite 89% acknowledging the need.

### Trust and governance

Multiple frameworks exist—IBM's five Pillars of Trust, Microsoft's six Principles, Deloitte's Trustworthy AI, NIST AI RMF—but the EU AI Act is the defining trust framework for European companies. The risk-based classification system (unacceptable, high-risk, limited, minimal) creates clear compliance obligations. Penalties reach **€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover**.

For European companies, compliance can become competitive advantage. As Allianz demonstrated by signing the EU AI Pact in November 2024—proactive compliance builds trust moats. Gartner finds that in 57% of high-maturity organizations, business units trust and are ready to use AI versus only 14% in low-maturity organizations.

## 6. The European context: pragmatism, regulation, and distinctive advantage

### How European companies differ

EU enterprise AI adoption reached **19.95% in 2025** (Eurostat), up from 13.5% in 2024—a dramatic acceleration. But Europe's private AI investment gap remains significant: the US invested **$109 billion** in 2024, 12x China and 24x the UK. Europe produces only 3 of the world's top AI models versus 40 in the US and 15 in China.

However, Europe's approach is distinctively pragmatic and compliance-first. ING's Chief Analytics Officer Bahadir Yilmaz captures the philosophy: *"The conversation is no longer about if AI works. It's about understanding where it works and, more importantly, where it matters to the client."* European insurers lead in responsible AI talent, accounting for **47% of specialists in data ethics, governance, and risk** despite representing 36% of total AI talent.

A CEPR study of 12,000+ European firms found AI adoption increases labor productivity by **4% on average** with no evidence of reduced employment in the short run—a powerful counterargument to displacement fears.

### EU AI Act timeline and implications

The Act follows a risk-based approach with four tiers. Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI applied February 2, 2025. GPAI transparency requirements took effect August 2, 2025. Full application of high-risk system requirements comes **August 2, 2026** (potentially extended to December 2027 via the Digital Omnibus). Organizations need immediate action: complete AI inventory with risk classification, clarify supply chain role, implement risk management systems, establish AI literacy training (already required since February 2025), and conduct fundamental rights impact assessments.

### Regional nuances

- **UK**: No dedicated AI Act—relies on principles-based regulation with five SMART principles. AI Safety Institute rebranded as AI Security Institute. 75% of UK financial firms now use some form of AI. Maintaining regulatory flexibility versus EU's prescriptive approach, but UK businesses selling into EU must comply with the EU AI Act regardless.
- **Netherlands/Belgium**: ING headquartered in Amsterdam is the standout European AI case study, with 90% pilot-to-production rate. Strong ecosystem around Eindhoven (ASML, NXP, Philips) for hardware AI. High digital infrastructure maturity and multilingual workforce.
- **DACH**: Siemens is the undisputed leader in industrial AI. Germany's Bundesnetzagentur designated as AI Act regulator with "AI Service Desk" for SME support. Industry 4.0 concept originated in Germany (2011)—now evolving into AI-native manufacturing. The Mittelstand represents both the largest opportunity and challenge.
- **Nordics**: Norway ranks #1 in Europe and #3 globally for AI adoption (46.4%). Denmark is the top EU AI adopter. Nordic Digital Transformation Market projected to reach $115 billion by 2030. However, only **6% of Nordic organizations are "AI Achievers"** and the Cognizant momentum score is negative versus global average—more cautious but higher data readiness.

### Private equity and mid-market

AI is establishing as the **"third pillar of value enhancement"** alongside financial engineering and operational excellence. Swedish PE firm EQT developed "Motherbrain," an AI platform for deal sourcing. 48% of M&A professionals now use AI in due diligence (up from 20%). FTI Consulting identifies three plays: transform the core business model, centralize AI orchestration at the firm level, and build AI transformation into the buy-side/sell-side narrative. AI usage among mid-sized European businesses grew **60% year-over-year**.

## 9. Adjacent concepts: readiness, governance, CoE, and KPIs

### AI readiness frameworks
Major assessment models include Cisco's AI Readiness Index (six pillars: Strategy, Infrastructure, Data, Governance, Talent, Culture), Microsoft's five-stage maturity model, Gartner's five-level AI Maturity Model, and Deloitte's four-level framework. The AI Readiness Index 2024 places Western Europe at 69.56 (2nd globally after North America at 82.60), with France (79.36) leading, followed by UK (78.88), Netherlands (77.23), Germany (76.90), and Finland (76.48). Over **60% of European firms are still at the earliest stages** of AI maturity.

### Center of Excellence models
Three operating models dominate: Centralized CoE (early-stage maturity), Hub-and-Spoke/Federated (most recommended, mid-to-late maturity), and Embedded/Decentralized (mature organizations with established standards). The typical progression is centralize-then-decentralize: build the CoE, set standards and governance, then shift to an advisory model as maturity grows. Key success factor: demonstrate measurable outcomes within first 60–90 days.

### Measuring AI transformation success
Critical KPIs span four categories: **Financial** (cost savings, revenue impact, ROAI), **Operational** (process efficiency, error reduction, throughput), **Adoption** (tool usage rates, employee sentiment, workflows redesigned), and **Strategic** (CSAT/NPS, decision quality, innovation velocity). Common pitfalls include pilot tunnel vision, over-indexing on model metrics while ignoring business outcomes, short-term ROI obsession (the MIT "95% failure" study measured ROI at only 6 months post-pilot), and failing to establish baselines. Gartner finds **45% of high-maturity organizations keep AI projects operational 3+ years versus only 20% of low-maturity**.

### Board governance gap
**66% of boards report limited, minimal, or no AI knowledge**. Only 15% of boards receive AI-related metrics. Fewer than 25% have board-approved AI policies. Only 14% of boards discuss AI at every meeting. Yet **88% of organizations use AI in at least one business function**. This gap between AI adoption velocity and governance maturity represents the single greatest enterprise risk in the AI era. McKinsey recommends boards determine their "AI posture," codify governance frameworks, and demand specific metrics: ROI by business unit, % of processes AI-enabled, workforce reskilling progress, regulatory alignment, override rates.

## 10. Hartgers.co brand voice analysis

*Note: The hartgers.co website was not accessible through automated fetching tools. This analysis is constructed from Stephan Hartgers' published thought leadership articles, interviews, LinkedIn content, and professional profiles.*

### Voice attributes

**Strategic-Pragmatic**: Bridges high-level strategy with actionable execution. Frames innovation as structured work, not blue-sky thinking. *"What we see now is a strategic framework emerging. There can be tools or concepts applied—the innovation thesis—that really sets out where to innovate and where not to."*

**Framework-Oriented**: Consistently introduces frameworks, stages, and categorizations to make complex topics digestible. Breaks down trends into enumerable stages. Uses "three trends" or "stages of maturity" as organizing principles.

**Measured and Authoritative**: Avoids sensationalism and marketing language, preferring measured analysis with qualifying language ("what we see now is...") rather than absolute claims. Draws authority from McKinsey and KPMG pedigree.

**Business-Value Anchored**: Every technology discussion grounded in business outcomes. *"If it has value for the customer, it also has value for the organisation."*

**Conversational Authority**: Accessible, almost conversational tone while maintaining executive credibility. Uses relatable scenarios, parentheticals, and everyday metaphors.

**Transformation-Minded**: Consistently positioned at the intersection of old-to-new. *"Adaptability has always been a key attribute in the pursuit of survival."*

**Cross-Functional Thinker**: Frames problems across business, IT, data, and organizational design—never just one silo. Aligns "business, organisation, IT and data."

### Tone spectrum
- Formal ↔ Informal: **4/10** (moderately informal, professional but accessible)
- Authoritative ↔ Collaborative: **7/10** (leans authoritative, speaks from experience)
- Academic ↔ Practical: **3/10** (highly practical, anchored to real-world scenarios)
- Technical ↔ Business: **3/10** (business-heavy; technology is the enabler, business value is the headline)
- Optimistic ↔ Cautious: **6/10** (balanced-optimistic with execution caveats)

### How the author speaks to C-suite audiences
Leads with competitive positioning and business challenge—never leads with the technology. Uses "where to play, how to win" language (borrowed from strategy consulting).
