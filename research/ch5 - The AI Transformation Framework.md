# Three angles for every AI investment: Value, Capabilities, and Trust

**The most important finding for European CEOs is stark: 95% of generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable return, yet the 5% that succeed generate 10–25% EBITDA gains and 1.5× higher revenue growth.** The difference between these two groups is not technology spend — it is how systematically organisations evaluate AI investments across three interlocking dimensions: the value an initiative creates, the capabilities required to deliver it, and the trust infrastructure needed to sustain it. This Value–Capabilities–Trust framework provides a decision architecture that maps directly onto the challenges European CEOs face: a €2.7 trillion GDP opportunity by 2030, the world's first comprehensive AI regulation now entering enforcement, and a capability gap where **89% of executives acknowledge their workforce needs AI skills but only 6% have begun meaningful upskilling**. What follows is a comprehensive evidence base — organised by each angle and their intersection — drawn from 2024–2026 data across consulting research, European statistics, and PE/mid-market practice.

## 1. VALUE: Where AI actually creates returns — and why most companies miss them

### Beyond Deploy-Reshape-Invent: how firms categorise AI value

BCG's Deploy-Reshape-Invent taxonomy remains influential, but several alternative frameworks now compete for CEO attention. McKinsey's "State of AI" reports (2024–2025) frame AI value across **four major pools** — customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D — which together account for 75% of the estimated **$2.6–$4.4 trillion** in annual generative AI economic potential. Bain's Technology Report 2025 introduces four agentic AI maturity levels: from LLM-powered information retrieval (Level 1) through single-task agentic workflows (Level 2) to cross-system orchestration (Level 3) and multi-agent constellations (Level 4). Tech-forward enterprises that scaled Levels 1–2 achieved **10–25% EBITDA gains** in 2023–2024. Bain's key insight: "diffuse" deployment yields only microproductivity, while deep embedding in functional workflows compounds gains exponentially.

BCG's own framework has expanded beyond DRI into three explicit value levers: new revenue, cost reduction, and productivity gains — with potential ROI of **10–15× in under three years**. Deloitte tracks AI value through technology budget share, which surged from **8% of revenue in 2024 to 14% in 2025**, with AI capturing **36% of digital initiative budgets**. For PE specifically, FTI Consulting identifies three value plays: transforming the core business model, centralising AI orchestration at the firm level, and building AI into sell-side narrative and buy-side diligence. The OECD published a sectoral AI intensity taxonomy in December 2024, classifying industries by AI human capital demand, innovation (patents), and actual usage across 16 countries including multiple EU members.

Each taxonomy reveals a different truth. McKinsey's is function-oriented. Bain's is maturity-oriented. BCG's DRI is horizon-oriented. FTI's is transaction-oriented. CEOs benefit from using more than one: function-level analysis identifies where to start, maturity staging determines how ambitious to be, and horizon thinking balances the portfolio.

### The measurement problem: ROAI and the failure landscape

The evidence on AI failure rates is sobering but nuanced. The most cited statistic — that **95% of generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable return** — comes from the MIT NANDA study ("The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025"), based on 300+ initiatives across 52 organisational interviews and 153 senior leader surveys. RAND Corporation found **80% of AI projects fail to reach meaningful production** (August 2024, from interviews with 65 experienced data scientists). BCG's global study of 1,000 C-level executives (October 2024) found **74% of companies have yet to show tangible value**, with only 4% achieving enterprise-wide AI at scale. McKinsey's 2025 survey revealed only **5.5% of respondents attribute more than 5% of EBIT to AI**. Gartner predicted 30% of generative AI projects would be abandoned after proof-of-concept by end of 2025.

These statistics reflect different scopes and definitions but converge on a single pattern: the gap between pilot and production is where most value dies. Large enterprises take **9 months on average to scale** AI from pilot versus 90 days for mid-market firms (MIT NANDA 2025). The measurement challenge has six specific pitfalls identified across sources: (1) the attribution problem — AI projects bundled with broader initiatives make isolation impossible; (2) intangible benefits — 64% of respondents say AI enables innovation but only 39% report EBIT impact; (3) time horizon mismatch — costs arrive immediately while benefits materialise over 12–24 months; (4) the "celebrate too early" trap — BCG warns companies claim success before bottom-line benefits materialise; (5) hard versus soft ROI confusion — lumping direct cost savings with innovation acceleration and risk reduction; and (6) pilot-to-scale disconnect — a use case delivering 40% efficiency in a controlled environment may yield 8% at enterprise scale once integration complexity, data quality variation, and change management friction are factored in.

The emerging best practice is ROAI (Return on AI Investment), measured iteratively: track improvement over a baseline across time savings, task reduction, accuracy improvement, and adoption rates. Set expected ROAI targets before deployment, measure longitudinally over model iterations, and assess both leading indicators (adoption, quality) and lagging KPIs (EBIT, revenue lift). BCG recommends charging **general managers — not CIOs** — with AI targets, because value accountability must sit where P&L responsibility lives.

### Quantified value cases with attribution

The evidence base for specific AI returns has strengthened considerably since 2024. In **procurement**, BCG (February 2025) documents **15–45% overall cost reduction** depending on category, with up to 30% reduction in manual work. A leading beverage company achieved **80% agency cost savings** through generative AI in procurement negotiations. A DAX-40 German company saved **10 hours per RFP** using AI-generated procurement documents. Contract reviews dropped from two days to 20 minutes. A global heavy equipment manufacturer realised **$45 million in savings** on direct material sourcing via an AI-powered source-to-pay platform. These figures exceed Roland Berger's benchmark of 7% procurement savings significantly.

In **customer service**, Klarna (Sweden) provides the most publicly documented European AI transformation: its AI assistant handles **2.3 million customer inquiries monthly**, reduced resolution time by 80%, decreased repeat inquiries by 25%, and delivered **$40 million in profit improvement** in 2024 alone — equivalent to 853 FTEs on a $2–3 million investment. Cumulative savings reached $60 million including marketing and SaaS consolidation. BCG documented a global asset manager reducing customer support operating expenses by **one-third (~$100 million)** through AI automation.

In **supply chain and forecasting**, McKinsey estimates AI-driven demand forecasting reduces errors by **20–50%**, product unavailability by up to 65%, warehousing costs by 5–10%, and administration costs by 25–40%. Danone achieved a **30% reduction in lost sales** through AI-powered demand modelling. General Mills saved **more than $20 million since FY2024** through AI supply chain optimisation. Lennox Residential improved service levels by **16%** and inventory turns by **25%** using ML-based forecasting. In manufacturing, BCG documented an oil and gas company achieving **70% error reduction and 40% maintenance cost reduction** through generative AI-enhanced operations. Colgate-Palmolive's predictive maintenance on a single motor saved 192 hours of downtime and 2.8 million tubs of production.

European-specific value data is increasingly robust. McKinsey estimates generative AI could drive Europe's productivity growth at **up to 3.1% per year through 2030**, adding **€2.7 trillion (20%) to Europe's combined economic output**. An EIB/BIS working paper (2026) found AI adoption increases labour productivity by **4% on average** across 12,000+ European firms, driven by capital deepening rather than job displacement. EY's European AI Barometer 2025 reports that **56% of European organisations report cost savings or profit increases from AI**, with an average financial benefit of **€6.24 million per firm**. Schneider Electric, with approximately 100 AI applications in operation, is projected by Morgan Stanley to realise **~€400 million in annual savings**.

### How leading companies prioritise AI use cases

The dominant prioritisation approach remains the **Impact-Feasibility Matrix**, which plots AI use cases on business impact versus implementation feasibility to create four quadrants: quick wins (high impact, high feasibility), strategic bets (high impact, low feasibility), selective opportunities (low impact, high feasibility), and defer (low impact, low feasibility). Maersk used this matrix to prioritise from 50+ identified use cases. L'Oréal mapped its AI portfolio to the value chain and discovered overconcentration in marketing.

Microsoft's **BXT (Business-Experience-Technology) framework** adds a third dimension, evaluating business value, user experience and desirability, and technical feasibility — each scored 1–5 — to classify use cases as Shelve, Research, Incubate, or Accelerate. DataProCorp's Strategic Scoring Model closely mirrors Value-Capability-Trust: Business Value (weighted 50%), Feasibility (30%), and Strategic Fit/Risk (20%), with weights adjusted by AI maturity — early-maturity companies weight feasibility higher, regulated industries weight risk higher. Harvard Business Review (January 2026) published "Manage Your AI Investments Like a Portfolio" by Hoque, Nelson, Davenport, and Scade, recommending a dual lens: an advancement pipeline with clear gates through which projects must pass, and a whole-portfolio dashboard showing balances across risk/return, time horizon, capability areas, and mission alignment.

BCG's portfolio allocation guidance is notably aggressive: set ambitious goals via top-down diagnostics rather than bottom-up pilots, charge general managers with targets, redesign entire workflows rather than siloed activities, and curate data as needed per workflow rather than pursuing enterprise-wide data perfection. Their research shows cross-functional teams earn **3× higher ROI** from AI, and AI high performers are **3× more likely to report strong senior leadership ownership**.

## 2. CAPABILITIES: The 70% that determines success or failure

### What capability-based strategy means for AI

Every major consulting firm now defines AI capabilities across five to seven dimensions. McKinsey's "Rewired" framework identifies six: **strategy, talent, operating model, technology, data, and adoption/scaling**. Of 25 organisational attributes tested, workflow redesign showed the biggest correlation with EBIT impact from generative AI — yet only 21% of organisations have fundamentally redesigned workflows around it. Gartner evaluates AI readiness across seven areas: strategy, product, governance, engineering, data, operating models, and culture, distributed across five maturity levels. MIT CISR's Enterprise AI Maturity Model (December 2024) demonstrates that enterprises in maturity stages 1–2 perform below industry-average financial performance, while stages 3–4 perform well above average — capabilities build cumulatively and compound returns.

### BCG's 10/20/70 rule and what the 70% actually requires

BCG's 10/20/70 principle — **10% algorithms, 20% technology, 70% people and processes** — originated from analysis of hundreds of AI transformations and was most clearly articulated by Vinciane Beauchene, BCG Managing Director: "The companies that capture the most value from AI follow the 10-20-70 rule." BCG reiterated in 2025: "Algorithms account for 10% of the work in AI transformation, the tech backbone 20%. The remaining 70% comes from people and processes."

The 70% breaks down into eight practical categories: **change management programmes** clarifying ownership and decision flows; **role-specific training** beyond tool usage to evolving job descriptions; **workflow redesign** — the single biggest correlate with EBIT impact per McKinsey; **governance frameworks** for AI decision-making protocols; **communication infrastructure** including regular internal updates on AI initiatives; **manager coaching** to enable leadership-driven adoption; **user support** through help desks and champions networks; and **incentive alignment** — shifting what gets rewarded to encourage AI adoption.

Case studies illustrate what investing in the 70% looks like at scale. Unilever trained **23,000 employees in AI usage by end of 2024** across 500+ AI projects, establishing AI Champions and structured upskilling pathways across its supply chain as part of an €800 million productivity programme. Siemens identified **300+ generative AI use cases** in manufacturing, hired approximately 30,000 software engineers, and launched a cross-functional generative AI programme — with CEO Roland Busch personally championing the transformation. The Carlyle Group established structured AI training as part of onboarding and created an "AI Champions Council" with representatives across departments serving as internal advocates.

For mid-market companies, the allocation may shift to approximately 75/15/10 (proportionally more investment in people) since they lack the technology platforms that larger enterprises have already built. For regulated industries, the mix may be 65/20/15 (higher algorithm spend for compliance-built-in tools).

### Critical roles and the domain leader gap

The AI talent landscape has evolved rapidly. Ten main types of AI engineers are now recognised, with four roles proving most critical for AI scaling. The **AI Product Manager** bridges business and technology, commanding $130K–$220K+ in the US (Glassdoor reports average total compensation of $244,774). The **MLOps/AI Platform Engineer** averages $165K with 9.8× growth in job postings over five years — the critical bottleneck for scaling AI in production. The **Agent Orchestrator/Prompt Engineer** saw demand surge 135.8% in 2025, with specialised agent-system engineers commanding $175K–$250K. The **Chief AI Officer**, a role that did not exist five years ago, averages $351,766 with top earners exceeding $643K.

In Europe, senior AI developers in Western and Nordic countries earn an average of $106K per year — roughly 30–50% below US equivalents — while salaries have climbed 20–35% since 2023. Generative AI job posting growth has been explosive: Ireland **+204%**, UK **+120%**, Germany **+109%**, France **+91%** (PwC/EIT, year to March 2025).

The most underappreciated role is the **domain leader at the N-2/N-3 level** — the business process owner two or three levels below the CEO who understands both the operational detail and the strategic context. McKinsey's finding that workflow redesign has the biggest effect on EBIT impact implies that these domain leaders, not technologists, determine whether AI transforms a process or merely automates the edges of it. BCG's recommendation to charge general managers with AI targets — not CIOs — reflects the same insight. In successful AI transformations, these leaders own the use case selection, define the success metrics, drive workflow redesign, and manage adoption within their teams.

### The training paradox and European capability gaps

BCG's 2024 survey revealed the starkest capability gap statistic: **89% of business leaders acknowledge their workforce needs improved AI skills, yet only 6% had begun upskilling meaningfully**. This paradox is confirmed across sources: Kyndryl's 2025 People Readiness Report found 71% of global leaders say workforces are not AI-ready while only 14% are actively upskilling; BCG's "AI at Work" study showed only 14% of frontline employees had received training versus 44% of C-suite; and 46% of executives cite talent skill gaps as the number one reason AI development pace is too slow (McKinsey US CxO Survey, October–November 2024).

European-specific data sharpens the picture. **45% of the EU population (age 16–74) lacks basic digital skills**, let alone AI literacy. EU AI talent has doubled between 2016 and 2023 but still represents only **0.41% of the EU workforce**. By 2030, up to 6.5% of the EU workforce may need to transition to new occupations. Women represent **less than 25% of AI engineers** across Europe, falling to 11% in some cities. France faces brain drain to the US, while Germany and Switzerland are net talent magnets. Germany alone has a deficit of more than **137,000 IT specialists**.

Yet Europe has structural advantages: **30% higher per-capita concentration of AI professionals than the US**, with 70% holding Master's or PhD degrees (versus more undergraduate-heavy profiles elsewhere). Thirty-five percent of all AI-related Master's programmes globally are offered by EU universities. The policy response is substantial: France committed **€10 billion** through its "France and AI" plan, Portugal's AI Portugal 2030 targets upskilling 1.3 million workers, and the EIT Deep Tech Talent Initiative has trained **1 million people** in deep tech including AI.

Critically, **Article 4 of the EU AI Act**, in force since February 2025, creates a legal mandate for AI literacy — requiring providers and deployers to ensure adequate AI training for all staff operating or overseeing AI systems. This transforms upskilling from a strategic choice to a compliance obligation across Europe.

### Organisational models: how AI teams actually work

Three dominant operating models have emerged. The **centralised Centre of Excellence** consolidates talent, sets enterprise-wide standards, and maximises knowledge reuse — best for early-stage AI maturity. The **federated model** distributes AI talent to individual business units, promoting innovation but risking duplication and inconsistency. The **hub-and-spoke model** (recommended by most sources as the optimal steady state) maintains a central CoE defining standards, governance, and shared services while business units execute use cases locally with feedback loops. Microsoft's guidance suggests starting centralised, monitoring for organisational inflection points (approval delays, knowledge bottlenecks), then transitioning to an advisory model as maturity grows.

Common failure modes when capabilities are missing include: scattered pilots with no common standards, data silos preventing model generalisation, legacy infrastructure creating integration friction, employee resistance from insufficient change management, and duplicated efforts across teams. McKinsey found that 52% of AI high performers have documented processes to take AI to production, versus only 34% of others — a process gap, not a technology gap.

## 3. TRUST: Europe's distinctive and enforceable advantage

### Four frameworks and what they share

The major trustworthy AI frameworks converge on five common themes — fairness, transparency, safety, privacy, and accountability — but differ in emphasis and operational approach. **IBM's Five Pillars** (Explainability, Fairness, Robustness, Transparency, Privacy) are technically oriented, supported by open-source toolkits and governed by an AI Ethics Board co-chaired by Dr. Francesca Rossi. **Microsoft's Six Principles** (Fairness, Reliability and Safety, Privacy and Security, Inclusiveness, Transparency, Accountability) are uniquely distinguished by elevating inclusiveness as a separate pillar, operationalised through a Responsible AI Standard with 14 goals, an Office of Responsible AI, and the Aether Committee advisory body. **Deloitte's Trustworthy AI framework** spans six dimensions (Fair and Impartial, Transparent and Explainable, Robust and Reliable, Respectful of Privacy, Safe and Secure, Responsible and Accountable) applied across the full AI lifecycle from ideation through MLOps. The **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** operates at a different level of abstraction — four process functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) with seven trustworthy AI characteristics — designed as a governance architecture rather than a principles statement.

The distinction between terms matters for precise communication. **Ethical AI** is values-based and philosophical — it asks "what is the right thing to do?" **Responsible AI** is process-based and tactical — it asks "how do we demonstrate fairness?" **Trustworthy AI** is outcome-based and technical — it establishes confidence in reliability, security, and explainability. The EU's preferred term is "trustworthy AI" (lawful, ethical, and robust), while industry increasingly uses "responsible AI" as the broadest umbrella.

### EU AI Act: enforcement begins with real consequences

The EU AI Act, the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, entered into force on 1 August 2024 with staggered enforcement. **Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI and AI literacy requirements took effect in February 2025.** Obligations for general-purpose AI models apply from August 2025, with Member States required to designate competent authorities by the same date. The majority of the Act — including high-risk system obligations, transparency rules, and national enforcement — applies from **August 2026**. Full applicability for AI embedded in regulated products extends to August 2027.

The risk classification system creates four tiers. **Unacceptable-risk AI** (banned since February 2025) includes social scoring, subliminal manipulation causing significant harm, emotion recognition in workplaces and schools, and untargeted facial image scraping. **High-risk AI** (applying August 2026) covers biometrics, critical infrastructure safety, education access and evaluation, employment decisions, credit scoring, insurance risk assessment, and law enforcement applications. Providers of high-risk systems must establish risk management systems, maintain technical documentation, enable human oversight, meet accuracy and robustness standards, conduct conformity assessments, apply CE marking, and report serious incidents. Deployers must conduct **fundamental rights impact assessments** that often overlap with GDPR's data protection impact assessments.

Penalties are significant: up to **€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover** for prohibited AI practices — exceeding GDPR's maximum of €20 million or 4%. However, implementation is uneven. As of early 2026, only **3 EU Member States have designated both notifying and market surveillance authorities**, 10 have partial designations, and **14 have yet to designate any competent authority** — creating significant enforcement uncertainty.

A major complication emerged in November 2025 with the European Commission's **Digital Omnibus Regulation** and AI Omnibus proposals, which could postpone high-risk AI obligations, simplify documentation for smaller companies, and narrow GDPR definitions. These are controversial — criticised by civil society as a rollback influenced by industry lobbying — and are now in trilogue. Their outcome could materially reshape the regulatory landscape.

The interaction between GDPR and the AI Act creates specific friction. Fundamental rights impact assessments overlap with data protection impact assessments but differ in scope and procedure. GDPR Article 9 prohibits processing special category data, while AI Act Article 10(5) explicitly allows it for bias detection in high-risk systems with safeguards — creating potentially contradictory requirements. Sector-specific rules compound the complexity: MiFID II governs algorithmic trading, DORA imposes ICT risk management obligations on financial services, the Medical Device Regulation applies to AI in healthcare, and NIS2 cybersecurity requirements overlay AI in critical infrastructure.

### Trust correlates with adoption, revenue, and retention

The evidence linking trust to commercial outcomes is increasingly robust. **Gartner's June 2025 research** (Q4 2024 survey, 432 respondents across the US, UK, France, Germany, India, and Japan) confirmed that **in 57% of high-maturity organisations, business units trust and are ready to use new AI solutions, compared with only 14% of low-maturity organisations**. High-maturity organisations keep AI projects operational for 3+ years at a rate of 45% versus 20% for low-maturity peers. Ninety-one percent of high-maturity organisations have appointed dedicated AI leaders. As Gartner Senior Director Analyst Birgi Tamersoy stated: "Trust is one of the differentiators between success and failure for an AI or GenAI initiative."

Consumer data reinforces this. Relyance AI's December 2025 survey found **50% of consumers will choose a transparent company even over the lowest-priced alternative**, with 76% willing to switch to the first company that proves AI transparency. Deloitte's 2025 Connected Consumer study found consumers who view tech providers as excelling in both innovation and data responsibility **spend 62% more annually on tech devices**. The IPA/Financial Times research found companies that measure trust as a **board-level KPI are 3× more likely to report stronger profits** — though this is correlation, not proven causation. Cisco's 2024 research showed approximately 75% of consumers will not purchase from companies they do not trust with their data.

In Europe specifically, **53% of generative AI users believe adoption would increase with proper government regulation** (Deloitte European Trust Survey, 30,252 respondents across 11 countries). EY's European AI Barometer 2025 found **70% of employees have a positive attitude toward AI** (up from 63% in 2024), but only 52% believe their employer has a clear ethical framework. The positive attitude gap between management (82%) and non-executive employees (63%) — a 19 percentage-point spread — suggests trust must be built at every level, not just communicated from the top.

### Allianz and the EU AI Pact as trust signals

Allianz SE joined the EU AI Pact on 27 November 2024, committing to three actions: adopting an AI governance strategy, mapping high-risk AI systems under the Act, and promoting AI literacy among staff. Allianz has approximately 400 generative AI use cases as of December 2024, including an Enterprise Knowledge Assistant for customer service agents and agentic AI for food spoilage claims automation with human-in-the-loop. Over 200 companies have signed the EU AI Pact, including Deutsche Telekom, IBM, Lenovo, Nokia, PwC, Salesforce, and Telecom Italia — with over half pledging additional commitments beyond the three core actions. These signatories are positioning themselves ahead of mandatory compliance, using early adoption as a competitive signal.

### Board oversight is rapidly evolving but still immature

Board-level AI oversight has tripled in 12 months. EY's Fortune 100 analysis (2025) found **48% specifically cite AI risk as part of board oversight** (up from 16% in 2024), and **40% assign AI oversight to at least one board-level committee** (up from 11%). Yet **66% of directors report limited to no knowledge or experience with AI** (McKinsey, 2025), and fewer than 25% of companies have board-approved, structured AI policies (NACD 2025). Only 15% of boards receive AI-related metrics. MIT CISR's 2025 research found organisations with digitally and AI-savvy boards **outperform peers by 10.9 percentage points in return on equity**.

## 4. Using the three angles together: decision architecture for AI investments

### How existing frameworks map to Value-Capability-Trust

The Value-Capability-Trust (VCT) framework is not an isolated construct — it has direct parallels across major consulting and practitioner models. The table below shows the convergence:

| **Established framework** | **Value dimension** | **Capability dimension** | **Trust dimension** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Microsoft BXT | Business value | Technology feasibility | Experience (user adoption/trust) |
| IDEO DFV | Viability | Feasibility | Desirability (user trust) |
| DataProCorp 3-axis scoring | Business Value (50% weight) | Feasibility (30%) | Strategic Fit/Risk (20%) |
| NIST AI RMF | Map (value context) | Measure (capabilities) | Govern + Manage (trust) |
| BCG Build for the Future | Business impact, revenue/cost | AI maturity (41 dimensions) | Trust Protocol, Responsible AI |
| Deloitte integrated approach | Business value, ROI | Technical readiness, data | Trustworthy AI (6 dimensions) |

The VCT framework's distinctive contribution is making **trust an explicit, co-equal dimension** rather than a downstream compliance check. Most existing frameworks treat risk or governance as a modifier of value and feasibility decisions already made. The VCT approach elevates trust to a gating criterion — no initiative proceeds unless it clears all three thresholds. This is particularly relevant for European companies operating under the EU AI Act, where trust deficits carry regulatory penalties of up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover.

### Scoring and gating in practice

A practical VCT scorecard evaluates each AI initiative on three 1–5 scales. **Value** assesses business impact (revenue, cost, productivity), strategic alignment, time-to-value, and scalability — weighted 50% for growth-oriented companies or 40% for regulated industries. **Capability** assesses data readiness, talent availability, technology infrastructure, and organisational maturity — weighted 30% for mature organisations or 40% for early-stage ones. **Trust** assesses regulatory compliance risk, ethical implications, customer and employee acceptance, and governance readiness — weighted 20% for unregulated industries or 30% for high-risk sectors.

The weighted scoring model — Overall Score = (Value × weight_v) + (Capability × weight_c) + (Trust × weight_t) — produces four natural quadrants when plotted. **Quick wins** score high on all three and should be prioritised immediately. **Strategic bets** score high on Value but lower on Capability or Trust and require deliberate capability building or governance investment before scaling. **Capability builders** score moderate on Value but serve to develop organisational AI muscles. **No-go zones** score low on Trust regardless of Value — these must be deferred until governance infrastructure exists.

BCG's Trust Protocol for agentic AI provides a graduated autonomy model that maps onto the Trust dimension: Tier 1 (Shadow Mode — agent suggests, human acts), Tier 2 (Supervised Autonomy), Tier 3 (Bounded Autonomy), and Tier 4 (Full Autonomy). BCG notes: "The greatest barrier to scaling agentic AI isn't technology, it's trust." This framework gives boards a concrete vocabulary for discussing how much autonomous authority any AI system should have.

### Sequencing: the crawl-walk-run logic

Organisations consistently face the question of whether to pursue quick wins, build capability, or invest in trust infrastructure first. The evidence supports a specific sequencing logic. Georgian Partners, an AI-focused venture capital firm, documented that portfolio companies following a **crawl-walk-run approach** consistently outperform those that skip stages. In the **crawl phase**, organisations build foundational AI literacy, validate data quality, and establish basic governance. In the **walk phase**, they deploy pilots with clear success metrics, build cross-functional teams, and establish human-in-the-loop protocols. In the **run phase**, they execute complex automation, scale enterprise-wide, and progressively increase AI autonomy.

The World Economic Forum (June 2025) recommends AI portfolio diversification analogous to financial portfolio theory: spreading investments across **short-term efficiency gains** (core), **medium-term business model innovation** (adjacent), and **long-term transformational AI** (frontier). The key discipline is continuous rebalancing — doubling down on high-impact projects, pivoting where needed, and divesting from underperforming initiatives.

The tension between Value and Capability is the most common bottleneck. High-value but low-capability scenarios — classified as "Research" in Microsoft's BXT framework — should not be abandoned but rather parked with active capability-building plans. The tension between Value and Trust is the most dangerous: high-value, high-risk deployments in areas like credit scoring, employment screening, or medical triage carry regulatory and reputational consequences that can dwarf the anticipated value. BCG's graduated autonomy approach provides the resolution: start in Shadow Mode, earn autonomy through demonstrated accuracy, and never bypass trust gates regardless of Value pressure.

### What CEOs and experts say about multi-dimensional AI evaluation

European CEO perspectives converge on urgency tempered by uncertainty. The Oliver Wyman Forum European CEO Survey (September 2025, n=77) found **97% view AI as a business opportunity rather than a risk**, yet 45% express fear of being left behind and nearly half cite "not moving fast enough" as a top concern. SAP CEO Christian Klein at Davos (January 2025) framed Europe's challenge as coordination: "It's not even the lack of financing — it's a question of who owns it. Who actually gives the order to say we digitise Europe, and we are not doing it in silos?" Siemens CEO Roland Busch emphasised infrastructure: "Just count the amount of data centres sitting in the United States and being built in the United States versus Europe."

IBM's 2025 CEO Study (2,000 CEOs globally) found 61% actively adopting AI agents and preparing for scale, with 72% viewing proprietary data as key to unlocking value. IBM Vice Chairman Gary Cohn stated bluntly: "Leaders who aren't leveraging AI and their own data to move forward are making a conscious business decision not to compete." KPMG's Global CEO Outlook (2025, n=1,350) found **71% rank AI as a top investment priority** (up from 64% in 2024), with 67% now expecting ROI in 1–3 years — a significant acceleration from the majority expecting 3–5 years in 2024.

PwC's CEO Survey carried perhaps the starkest warning: **40% of respondents indicated their companies won't survive the next decade** if they don't chart a new path amid AI-driven change. This existential framing elevates the VCT framework from a project-selection tool to a strategic survival mechanism.

## 5. The European and PE/mid-market lens: where VCT meets context

### Europe's AI productivity paradox

Europe faces a distinctive paradox: structural advantages in talent and regulation coexist with a significant adoption and investment gap. Private AI investment tells the story most clearly — the US invested approximately **$110 billion in 2024**, more than 5× Europe's ~$11 billion. European organisations lag US counterparts by **45–70% on AI adoption** (McKinsey). Only about **20% of European firms have adopted AI at scale** versus 31% in North America. Stanford's AI Index notes Europe produced only 3 notable AI models in 2024 versus China's 15 and America's 40.

Yet the productivity potential is enormous and may exceed that of the US in relative terms. An IMF working paper (April 2025) estimated Europe's cumulative total factor productivity gains from AI at approximately **1.1% over five years — roughly 60% higher than Acemoglu's estimates for the US**. McKinsey projects generative AI could drive European productivity growth at up to 3.1% annually by 2030, with sovereign AI potentially unlocking **up to €480 billion in annual value by 2030**. Eurostat data shows EU enterprise AI adoption jumped to **13.5% in 2024** — a 5.5 percentage-point increase from 2023, suggesting acceleration.

Country-level variation is significant. Germany's AI adoption rose from 13.3% to approximately **27%** between 2023 and 2025, with manufacturing usage doubling from 17.3% to 31%. France committed €10 billion to AI development and grew its unicorn count from 7 to 42 between 2015 and 2024. The Nordics lead on several metrics: Sweden has the highest late-stage deep-tech funding as a share of GDP (0.5%), and Norway faces potential AI productivity gains of up to 5% in optimistic scenarios. The Netherlands leads Europe in the belief that AI impacts work (71% of respondents).

### Trust as specifically European competitive advantage

The "Brussels effect" argument — that EU regulation becomes a de facto global standard — has mixed evidence for AI so far. Unlike GDPR, which spawned comparable privacy laws worldwide, the AI Act has produced **limited direct copycats**: only Canada and Brazil are drafting similar risk-based frameworks, while the UK, Australia, Japan, and Singapore take lighter approaches. However, de facto compliance effects are emerging as major US technology companies build EU AI Act compliance frameworks into their global products — Microsoft has published compliance tools in its Purview platform and created an AI Act compliance blueprint for Denmark.

The commercial trust premium is more concrete. Deloitte's Connected Consumer study found consumers viewing providers as strong on both innovation and data responsibility **spend 62% more annually**. In B2B contexts, procurement teams in government and critical infrastructure increasingly demand conformity assessments upfront, creating a market advantage for early compliers. The IPA/Financial Times research linking trust-as-board-KPI to 3× profit likelihood, while correlational, suggests the premium is significant. Pew Research (2025) found more people globally **trust the EU to regulate AI than trust the US or China** — a reputational asset for EU-headquartered companies operating internationally.

European employee attitudes provide additional evidence. EY's Barometer found **61% of European employees expect the EU AI Act to have a positive influence** on their organisation — suggesting regulation is not resisted but welcomed as a framework for responsible adoption. Yet only 52% believe their employer has a clear ethical framework for AI, indicating a gap between regulatory aspiration and corporate execution that early movers can exploit.

### PE and mid-market: where VCT becomes due diligence

Private equity has moved decisively into AI as a value-creation lever. Bain's 2025 Global PE Report found nearly **20% of portfolio companies have operationalised generative AI use cases** with concrete results, while **83% of PE investors want to see AI data readiness** in potential investees — but only 40% of targets meet this threshold. PE deal volume in AI targets increased **49% year-over-year** in H1 2025. EY found two-thirds of PE clients had implemented at least one AI initiative in their portfolio by 2024, with AI-driven margin increases of **more than 10%** achievable in the medium term.

**EQT's Motherbrain** stands as the leading European PE case study. Founded in 2016 and now embedded across EQT Group (€224 billion under management), Motherbrain scans approximately **50 million companies** from 40+ data sources using ML for scoring and prediction. Its team of approximately 30 professionals operates across three divisions: Labs (consulting portfolio companies), Platform (engineering), and Research (knowledge acquisition). Motherbrain has directly sourced investments including Peakon (acquired by Workday) and AnyDesk. At its March 2025 Capital Markets Day, EQT stated its ambition to "build the most AI-literate investment organisation in the world" — and became the first PE company to hold an AI patent.

FTI Consulting's three plays for PE value creation map directly onto the VCT framework. **Play 1** (reshape core business model) is a Value play targeting 5–25% EBITDA gains. **Play 2** (centralise AI orchestration at firm level) is a Capability play moving from decentralised models (40% of PE firms) toward centralised architectures. **Play 3** (build AI into diligence narratives) is a Trust play — AI-implemented portfolio companies can benefit from an "AI multiplier" on purchase price, with one digital publishing case expanding total addressable market estimates by up to **3×**.

The mid-market challenge is acute. Eurostat data shows a steep size-dependent adoption gap: large enterprises (250+ employees) have approximately 48–75% AI adoption, medium enterprises (50–249) approximately 28%, and small enterprises (10–49) approximately 17%. German Mittelstand data is particularly striking: one study found **94% of German Mittelstand firms have not implemented AI**, with leadership hesitation and skills shortages — not GDPR — cited as primary barriers. Over 60% of German SMEs cite missing employee skills as the main obstacle.

Yet mid-market companies have a structural speed advantage: MIT NANDA found they scale AI from pilot in **90 days versus 9 months for large enterprises**. Solutions are increasingly accessible: low-code/no-code platforms improve accessibility in 78% of reviewed studies, standard solutions cover 80% of needs faster and more cost-effectively than custom builds, and realistic time savings of **20–30% in sales, administration, and service** are achievable through AI assistants. The EU's requirement for each Member State to establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox by August 2026, combined with European Digital Innovation Hubs providing AI skills and training services for SMEs, creates an increasingly supportive ecosystem.

For PE firms applying VCT in due diligence, the framework translates directly into investment criteria. The **Value** dimension assesses EBITDA uplift potential, TAM expansion through AI-enabled business models, and competitive positioning. The **Capability** dimension evaluates data readiness (only 40% of targets meet PE expectations), talent bench, technology infrastructure, and organisational AI maturity. The **Trust** dimension assesses EU AI Act compliance readiness, GDPR posture, governance structures, and reputational risk — increasingly a dealbreaker in regulated sectors.

## Conclusion: from framework to action

The Value-Capabilities-Trust framework works not because it introduces novel concepts but because it structures a conversation that most organisations are having chaotically. The evidence is clear on several points that should reshape executive thinking.

**Value is real but brutally concentrated.** The gap between the 5% of companies creating substantial value at scale and the 95% still in pilot purgatory is not narrowing — it is widening. BCG's research shows future-built companies invest **64% more of their AI budget** than laggards, creating a compounding advantage that will be extremely difficult to reverse. The implication: half-measures in AI investment produce worse outcomes than either full commitment or deliberate waiting.

**Capabilities — specifically the 70% that is people and process — remain the binding constraint.** Workflow redesign, not algorithm sophistication, shows the strongest correlation with EBIT impact. The 89%/6% upskilling gap reveals that most organisations recognise this truth in principle but fail to act on it in practice. Article 4 of the EU AI Act now makes this failure not just strategically costly but legally non-compliant.

**Trust is Europe's distinctive lever — but only if executed, not merely claimed.** The 57% versus 14% trust-adoption gap (Gartner) demonstrates that trust is not an obstacle to AI adoption but an enabler of it. The Digital Omnibus proposals introduce uncertainty, but the direction of travel toward regulated AI is irreversible globally. European companies that build trust infrastructure early create a moat that deepens with each regulatory cycle.

**The three angles interact dynamically.** High Value without Capability produces expensive pilots that never scale. High Capability without Trust produces technically impressive systems that regulators, customers, or employees reject. High Trust without Value produces governance theatre. Only the intersection of all three produces sustainable AI-driven competitive advantage. For the European CEO, this is not a philosophical observation — it is a decision architecture for every AI investment in the portfolio.
