# Chapter 11 Research: Building Your Agentic Organisation

## Overview

This research supports **Chapter 11 — Building Your Agentic Organisation**, the main "how-to" playbook chapter. It synthesises external research on the three-phase transformation journey (Foundation → Activation → Scale) and on developing agentic employees at each phase. Organised by phase and by theme so the writer can draw directly from this material.

## 1. Foundation Phase: Governance, Data, Literacy

### 1.1 AI Governance in the Foundation Phase

**What "AI governance" means at Foundation.** Before any pilot launches, the organisation needs minimum viable governance: policies, risk classification, ownership, and board reporting. Deloitte found that only 21% of organisations have rigorous governance in place even as deployment accelerates (Deloitte, 2026). Gartner projects over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end of 2027, primarily due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls.

**Governance elements to establish early:**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **AI inventory and risk classification** | Map all AI use cases to the EU AI Act's four risk tiers (unacceptable, high-risk, limited, minimal). This is now a legal requirement. |
| **Ownership and accountability** | Assign a senior executive (Chief AI Officer or equivalent) with cross-functional mandate. Allianz operates with Chief Data Officers per operating entity overseeing formal Data Maturity assessments across 16 dimensions. |
| **Tiered autonomy model** | Risk-adjusted boundaries — agents handle low-risk decisions independently, escalate high-impact actions to humans. Autonomy expands as reliability improves. |
| **Comprehensive logging** | Capture every agent action for inspection, audit, and compliance. Essential for EU AI Act high-risk system requirements (effective August 2026). |
| **Circuit breakers** | Real-time monitoring that enforces boundaries and can halt agent actions when needed. |
| **Pre-deployment assessment** | Check data permissions, system access, and risk before any agent goes live. |
| **Shadow agent policy** | Address the reality that employees are quietly deploying unapproved autonomous tools without IT oversight. |
| **Board reporting cadence** | 66% of boards report limited-to-no AI knowledge. Only 15% receive AI-related metrics. Governance must include regular board-level AI reporting from day one. |

**Companies that started early.** Allianz signed the EU AI Pact in November 2024 — proactive compliance as competitive advantage. Their governance framework centres on data quality, traceability, and responsible AI controls across 70+ countries, with a Global Business Glossary, Global Data Model, and Data Catalogue for tracking data lineage from source to target systems (Allianz, 2025).

### 1.2 Data Readiness

**The 120-day imperative.** CIO.com argues CIOs must establish AI and data foundations within a 120-day window to build organisational sovereignty at speed in 2026. This compressed timeline reflects the shift from experimental AI pilots to operational, production-grade deployments.

**Practical 120-day roadmap (adapted from multiple sources):**

| Days | Focus | Key actions |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **0–30** | Establish unified AI/data foundation | Conduct data foundation audit; define governance ownership model; perform architecture health check; assess data quality |
| **30–60** | Introduce governance and policy controls | Establish cross-functional data governance council (IT, legal, finance, operations); define data lineage and cataloguing standards; implement access controls and quality gates |
| **60–90** | Build interoperable foundations | Deploy semantic data models and ontologies; connect disparate data sources; build reusable data products with ownership, lineage, and quality metadata |
| **90–120** | Begin secure AI operationalisation | Run first data pipelines for AI workloads; test governance in practice; validate regulatory compliance (GDPR, EU AI Act) |

**Data reality check.** The World Economic Forum reports fewer than 1 in 5 organisations have high maturity in any aspect of data readiness. Gartner finds 57% of organisations estimate their data is not AI-ready. Only 12% globally report having truly AI-ready data. Without clean, connected, contextual data, even well-funded transformation initiatives will stall.

**Failure modes.** The 2026 Enterprise Data AI-Readiness Framework (Coalesce) identifies five critical pillars: (1) prepare organisations for managing AI agents, (2) transform governance with real-time observability, (3) converge analytics and decision-making into unified intelligence, (4) accelerate engineering through agent-orchestrated automation, (5) build interoperable data foundations with semantic context. Skipping any of these creates structural weaknesses that surface at Scale.

**McKinsey's "walled data gardens" concept.** Proprietary data becomes the key differentiator when foundation models commoditise. Companies that invest in unique, well-governed data assets during Foundation create durable competitive advantage.

### 1.3 AI Literacy

**What "literacy" includes.** AI literacy is not just prompting. The EU AI Act (Article 4, effective February 2, 2025) requires providers and deployers of AI systems to ensure their staff possess a "sufficient level of AI literacy." This is context-dependent with no universal benchmark — different roles require different competencies.

**Core literacy dimensions (synthesised from multiple frameworks):**

1. **Basic knowledge** — what AI is, what it can and cannot do, types of AI (predictive, generative, agentic)
2. **Prompting and interaction** — how to work effectively with AI systems, iteration and refinement
3. **Validation and judgment** — recognising hallucinations, assessing output quality, knowing when to trust and when to intervene
4. **Ethics and security** — data handling responsibilities, bias awareness, privacy implications
5. **Attitude and openness** — willingness to experiment, growth mindset toward AI collaboration

**How leading companies measure literacy.**

- **Anthropic's 4D AI Fluency Framework** (January 2026): Identifies 24 specific behaviours defining safe human-AI collaboration, with 11 directly observable in tool interactions. Analysis of 9,830 conversations found 85.7% exhibited iteration and refinement behaviours, with augmentative (thought-partner) use showing more than double the AI fluency behaviours compared to quick exchanges.
- **UMU's five-dimensional model**: Evaluates basic knowledge, attitude/openness, problem-solving, prompt skills, and security/compliance. A global retailer using this assessment achieved 47% improved recruitment accuracy and 56% improved job alignment.
- **ETS's Futurenav Adapt AI**: Three-component approach — self-report reflection, adaptive knowledge reasoning, and real-world scenario application.
- **Key benchmark**: While 70% of professionals use AI weekly, only 56% feel confident selecting the right tool for their needs (Go1, 2026).

**"Have you asked the AI?" as cultural norm.** McKinsey's own Lilli platform achieved 92% usage by global staff, 74% regular usage, saving 30%+ time on information gathering. The cultural mechanism: leadership asking "Have you asked Lilli?" in every meeting. This is the simplest and most powerful Foundation-phase literacy intervention — make AI the default first step for information gathering.

**Mandatory vs voluntary.** Foundational training on three core areas — when to use AI, how to use it effectively, and what AI actually is — increases employee AI usage 3x (Go1, 2026). Only 11% of L&D leaders feel fully prepared for skills demands, which is driving adoption of diagnostic assessment tools. Companies using AI literacy assessments report faster curriculum alignment and stronger executive sponsorship.

**Role of leadership in modelling.** BCG identifies "Trailblazer" CEOs (15% of total) who spend 6+ hours weekly upskilling on AI. These CEOs commit 3x the investment and invest 2x in workforce upskilling. The CEO's personal fluency with AI is the single strongest signal the organisation receives about whether literacy matters.

### 1.4 Agentic Employee at Foundation

**Embedded learning.** Move from classroom training to micro-learning triggered during actual work. AI agents can autonomously deliver coaching interventions based on real-time performance data (Training Journal, 2026). The goal is not "training events" but a continuous learning layer woven into daily workflows.

**Skill-sharing incentives.** The most valuable agentic employees will be those who build reusable skills and share them with colleagues. At Foundation, organisations should:
- Create recognition programmes for employees who build and share AI workflows
- Allocate dedicated time for experimentation (e.g. "AI Fridays," innovation sprints)
- Provide platforms for skill libraries (prompt templates, workflow automations, best practices)
- BBVA's model: Four horizontal innovation programmes — Discussion Club, Quick Studies, Innovation Sprints, and X Program — operating at different levels of transversality and time commitment

**Baseline fluency goals.** Define what "Foundation-ready" literacy looks like for each role family — not a universal bar, but role-appropriate competency. A customer service team managing a chatbot needs different training than an IT department operating an AI agent. Document compliance efforts for EU AI Act Article 4 requirements.

**How to avoid "optional" adoption.** Shopify's model is the clearest example: CEO Tobi Lütke mandated that "reflexive AI usage is now a baseline expectation." Teams must demonstrate why AI cannot do a job before requesting headcount. AI usage added to performance reviews. This top-down mandate, combined with bottom-up enablement, prevents the "nice to have" trap.

### 1.5 European Specifics for Foundation

**Works councils and social dialogue.** IndustriAll Europe adopted a comprehensive trade union strategy on AI at the workplace (November 2024). Works Councils have rights to information and consultation on new technologies impacting the workforce, particularly regarding data privacy and algorithmic bias. Key concerns include:
- Health, safety, and psychosocial risks from AI-driven work
- Deskilling of workers with intermediate qualifications and wage polarisation
- Algorithmic management and AI use in workplace decisions

Practical implication: European organisations must factor 2–4 months of works council consultation into Foundation timelines. This is not optional — it is a legal and cultural requirement in most EU member states. Companies that treat social dialogue as a governance feature (not an obstacle) build stronger workforce buy-in.

**GDPR constraints.** GDPR protections are essential to safeguarding worker rights during AI deployment. Data used for AI training, monitoring, and personalisation must comply with GDPR principles (purpose limitation, data minimisation, transparency). The proposed Digital Omnibus regulation risks weakening some GDPR protections — organisations should track this.

**EU AI Act training obligations.** Article 4 obligations are already in effect (since February 2, 2025). Companies must ensure AI literacy for all persons involved in operating and using AI systems, including external contractors. Non-compliance can result in fines up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

## 2. Activation Phase: Strategy Setting, CoE, Pilots, First Wins

### 2.1 Strategy Setting with the AI Transformation Framework

**How organisations set strategy at Activation.** BCG's core principle is "targets over tools" — elevating AI from a digital side project to a core performance agenda tied to growth, cost, and productivity. Strategy must be designed backward from a vision of "perfect": define P&L impact, map required data and capabilities, and establish quarterly milestones.

**Linking Foundation readiness to strategic priorities.** Digital initiatives deliver 15–20% ROI independently, but combined with AI reach 30–35% returns (BCG, 2026). This means organisations that skipped Foundation (data, governance, literacy) will see significantly lower returns from Activation investments. The four layers of the AI Transformation Framework (Vision, Products, Operating Model, Foundation) provide the strategy alignment structure:

- **Vision**: What does our AI-enabled future look like? What business outcomes do we target?
- **Products**: Which customer journeys, products, and services do we transform first?
- **Operating Model**: How do we staff, organise, and operate teams that blend humans and agents?
- **Foundation**: Is our data, technology, and enablement infrastructure ready to support the strategy?

**Common mistakes at strategy setting:**
- Starting with technology ("let's use GPT") rather than business outcomes
- Setting strategy without data readiness assessment — the "horseless carriage" trap
- Treating AI as an IT initiative rather than a business transformation
- Not aligning C-suite around a shared North Star

### 2.2 Centre of Excellence

**Three operating models:**

| Model | Best for | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **Centralised CoE** | Early-stage maturity | Core team sets standards, provides all expertise, governs all AI efforts. Risk: bottleneck if it doesn't transition. |
| **Hub-and-Spoke (Federated)** | Mid-to-late maturity | Central hub for standards and governance; embedded AI specialists in business units; community of practice across the organisation. Deloitte research shows 40% faster implementation timelines. |
| **Embedded (Decentralised)** | Mature organisations | AI capabilities fully integrated into business units. CoE becomes advisory. Requires well-established standards and governance. |

The typical progression is **centralise-then-decentralise**: build the CoE, set standards and governance, then shift to advisory as maturity grows.

**CoE charter essentials:**
- **Executive sponsorship**: Chief AI Officer or equivalent, plus cross-functional steering committee (IT, legal, risk, business units, ethics). Organisations with active executive sponsorship are 3.3x more likely to report value creation exceeding costs.
- **Core team composition**: AI/ML Engineers, Data Scientists, AI Ethicists, AI Product Managers, AI Governance Specialists, Business Translators (the "domain leaders" McKinsey identifies as "probably the single most critical role")
- **Mandate**: Identify workflows, manage redesign, and scale. McKinsey's "agentic factory" concept.
- **Success metrics**: Demonstrate measurable outcomes within first 60–90 days.
- **Scope**: 3–5 priority use cases initially (PE-backed companies use this narrower scope to accelerate).
- **Organisations with well-structured AI CoEs are 2.6x more likely to successfully scale AI initiatives.**

**Failure modes:**
- CoE as bottleneck — too centralised for too long, all requests queue
- Lack of business ownership — CoE becomes a "data science lab" disconnected from P&L
- No transition plan — never moving from centralised to hub-and-spoke
- Under-resourcing — treating CoE as a side project rather than core infrastructure

**BBVA's AI Factory as exemplar.** Founded 2019, the AI Factory operates through local hubs in Spain, Mexico, and Turkey with 400+ professionals. Multidisciplinary teams combine data science, ML engineering, solution architecture, analytics, product, design, and business roles — plus specialists in digital law, regulation, privacy, and security. Team configurations vary based on deployment context: customer-facing (requiring UX/design), internal systems (requiring engineering), or enhancement of other programmes. Four horizontal innovation programmes drive knowledge sharing: Discussion Club, Quick Studies, Innovation Sprints, and X Program.

### 2.3 Pilots: Selection, Execution, and Escaping Purgatory

**The pilot purgatory problem.** Between 87–95% of AI pilots never reach production. MIT's 2025 analysis of 300 enterprise deployments found 95% delivered zero P&L impact despite $30–40 billion invested. By mid-2025, 42% of companies had abandoned most AI initiatives — up from 17% in 2024.

**Root causes of pilot failure:**
- Cost underestimation: organisations underestimate scaling costs by 250–400%; 85% miss AI cost projections by over 10%
- Infrastructure gaps: lack of proper data pipelines, compliance controls, security guardrails
- Workflow redesign failure: treating AI as bolt-on rather than transformational
- No clear success criteria defined before launch
- No gating process: pilot → production decision is ad hoc

**Pilot selection criteria (aligned with Value/Capability/Trust and Four Tiers):**

| Criterion | Questions to answer |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Value** | What is the measurable business impact? Is it tied to P&L? Can we quantify it before we start? |
| **Capability** | Do we have the data, technology, and skills to execute? What is the gap? |
| **Trust** | What is the risk profile? Does it require human-in-the-loop? What are the regulatory implications? |
| **Tier alignment** | Is this an Individual, Team, Department, or Cross-functional use case? Start lower, build upward. |
| **Replicability** | Can the solution be templated and applied to other contexts? |

**Success criteria and metrics to define before launch:**
- Resolution rates, time saved, error reduction, employee satisfaction
- Clear P&L impact target (cost saving or revenue contribution)
- Adoption rate within the pilot group
- Governance compliance (audit readiness, bias checks, human review completion)
- Time to production readiness

**How to avoid pilot purgatory — what winners do differently:**
1. **Executive commitment**: C-level sponsors with skin in the game
2. **Business-led, not tech-led**: Domain leaders co-own the pilot alongside technical teams ("two-in-the-box")
3. **Clear gating process**: Defined criteria for pilot → production decision, including scaling cost estimate
4. **Workflow redesign, not bolt-on**: Redesign the end-to-end process, not just automate a task
5. **Fast cycle times**: Allianz built and deployed Project Nemo in under 100 days
6. **Structured feedback loops**: BCG's 10/20/70 rule — devote 10% to ML models, 20% to technology, 70% to people and processes

**Allianz Project Nemo as pilot exemplar.** Launched July 2025 in Australia, Nemo automates low-complexity insurance claims with seven specialised AI agents handling planning, coverage checks, weather validation, fraud screening, payout calculation, and audit. Processing time reduced by ~80% for eligible claims. Built in under 100 days. Scalable, modular design for other use cases. Human-in-the-loop for all payout decisions — governance by design.

### 2.4 First Wins and Storytelling

**Choosing early use cases.** The research consistently recommends structured, repeatable processes with clear before/after metrics: IT help desks, customer service, data processing, software development, claims processing, procurement. These generate credible data for the scaling narrative.

**Internal storytelling.** First wins must be narrated, not just measured. The story should answer:
- What was the problem? (Relatable business pain)
- What did we do? (Simple explanation of the AI solution)
- What changed? (Quantified impact — time, cost, quality, employee experience)
- What did we learn? (Honest reflection, including what didn't work)
- What's next? (Path from this win to the next, building momentum)

**Board storytelling.** Only 15% of boards receive AI-related metrics. First wins are the vehicle for changing this. Present: ROI of the pilot, path to scaling, governance compliance, and the strategic narrative of Foundation → Activation → Scale.

**Scaling the first win.** Successful companies template their first wins for replication: document the process, the team model, the governance approach, the metrics, and the lessons. This "playbook within a playbook" becomes the CoE's most valuable asset.

### 2.5 Blended Human–Agent Teams

**Design principles for human-agent collaboration:**

1. **Agents as team members, not tools**: Move from static job descriptions to dynamic, role-based architecture where AI agents are first-class team members.
2. **Transparent identity**: Microsoft's responsible agent design requires clear visual and linguistic markers differentiating agents from humans (e.g. "Approval Management Agent," not a human name).
3. **Clear handoffs and boundaries**: Design explicit handoff protocols between humans and agents to prevent role overlap.
4. **Psychological safety**: Google's Project Aristotle showed it is the #1 predictor of team performance. Even more critical when agents join the team.
5. **Structured interaction mechanisms**: Co-planning, co-tasking, action guards, escalation paths, and long-term memory systems.

**Five strategic human roles in AI workflows (Sherbaum, 2026):**

1. **Human as Trainer** — refines AI behaviour through labelled interactions
2. **Human as Fallback** — handles the "hard 20%" when AI confidence drops
3. **Human as Reviewer** — essential for regulated/high-stakes domains
4. **Human as Verifier** — validates output quality and compliance
5. **Human as Orchestrator** — sets goals, defines boundaries, coordinates multiple agents

**Superusers and champions.** McKinsey's five-step change model: identify and activate superusers who become internal champions and trainers. These individuals bridge the gap between early adopters and the mainstream workforce. Typically 5–10% of the workforce.

**"Two-in-the-box" model.** Pairs a business leader and a technology leader to co-own the AI initiative. The business leader ensures alignment with outcomes; the technology leader ensures feasibility and quality. This pairing is the basic unit of the blended team at the leadership level.

**Hybrid Intelligence Team (HIT) framework.** Proposes five distinct AI agent roles: Generator, Critic, Synthesiser, Verifier, and Coherence Checker. Human leadership must maintain narrative coherence to prevent information overload and cognitive fragmentation across different AI reasoning styles.

### 2.6 Agentic Employee at Activation

**From individual fluency to team coordination.** At Activation, the agentic employee shifts from personal AI use to team-level collaboration. Key transitions:
- From using AI as a personal tool → coordinating with agents as part of a team workflow
- From consuming skills → creating and sharing skills with colleagues
- From ad hoc prompting → building reusable workflows and templates

**First "orchestrator" and "specialist" roles.** Activation is where the three agentic employee profiles (supervisor, specialist, augmented frontline) begin to emerge in practice:
- **Supervisors**: Project managers who coordinate AI agents handling scheduling, reporting, resource allocation
- **Specialists**: Compliance officers, underwriters, analysts who review flagged cases agents escalated
- **Augmented frontline**: Customer success reps, field service workers whose agents handle data gathering, letting them focus on human-centric work

### 2.7 Case Material for Activation

| Company | Activation evidence |
|---------|-------------------|
| **BBVA** | AI Factory of 400+ professionals with multidisciplinary teams; 300 embedded "AI wizards" in business units; 3,000+ custom bots created; 80% daily engagement across 120,000 employees |
| **ING** | 500-strong analytics team; 90% pilot-to-production rate (vs industry 30%); AI-powered chatbots automate 75% of customer queries; 25% productivity gains when AI introduced to operations |

## 3. Scale Phase: Cross-Functional Transformation and Operating Model Redesign

### 3.1 Cross-Functional Transformation

**Breaking silos.** At Scale, AI transformation moves beyond departments to cross-functional, enterprise-wide workflows. McKinsey describes a global retailer where AI agents detect demand surges in one region and inventory pileup in another, then reallocate ad spend, adjust pricing, reroute stock, and refresh creative assets within seconds. This requires:

- **Enterprise North Star**: A shared AI vision that transcends departmental boundaries, aligned to business outcomes (revenue, cost, risk, customer experience)
- **Cross-functional task forces**: Teams composed across functions to own end-to-end workflows, not just functional outputs
- **Budget and talent reallocation**: Shifting resources from siloed initiatives to enterprise-level transformation programmes
- **Governance escalation**: AI oversight committee with enterprise-wide mandate

**What typically breaks at Scale:**
- **Politics**: Department heads resist ceding control of "their" AI initiatives
- **Incentives**: Individual and departmental metrics conflict with enterprise-wide optimisation
- **Measurement**: No agreed enterprise KPIs; each function measures differently
- **Change saturation**: 75% of organisations report being at or past the change saturation point. 1 in 3 workers actively sabotage corporate AI rollouts; 41% of millennial and Gen Z employees engage in non-compliance

### 3.2 Operating Model Redesign

**From function-centric to workflow-centric.** The agentic operating model replaces traditional functional hierarchies with outcome-oriented, cross-functional workflows. McKinsey's vision: "one manager with a small human team could orchestrate hundreds of agents acting autonomously in a workflow cutting across silos, running 24/7."

**McKinsey's six shifts for the agentic organisation:**

1. **Workflows** — Reimagine work as AI-first across end-to-end domains
2. **Leadership** — Rethink leadership roles and capabilities for enterprise-wide AI transformation
3. **Talent** — Redesign roles and profiles across the entire enterprise
4. **Skilling and Culture** — Build a culture of continuous reinvention beyond AI fluency
5. **Structure** — Reshape organisational structure to centre on value creation with clear accountabilities
6. **HR and Talent Systems** — Transform people management to become the engine of AI transformation

**Accountability and performance management in the scaled model.** Humans transition from execution to supervision. Work shifts from linear, predictable workflows to autonomous systems that adapt in real-time. New accountability structures needed:
- Agent performance dashboards alongside human performance management
- Outcome-based metrics rather than activity-based metrics
- Override rates as a governance KPI (how often do humans override agent decisions?)
- Shared accountability models for human-agent teams

**Agentic networks.** BCG's autonomy progression: Shadow Mode (humans validate all outputs) → Supervised Autonomy → Agent-Led with Oversight → Full Autonomy (human out of the loop, reserved for mature, low-risk environments). The expected trajectory: organisations anticipate a 250% increase in AI decision-making authority within 3 years (BCG/MIT Sloan, 2025).

### 3.3 Scale Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Evidence | Mitigation |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Over-centralisation** | CoE becomes bottleneck; innovation slows | Transition to hub-and-spoke; embed capabilities in business units |
| **Under-investment in capability** | BCG's 10/20/70 rule: 70% of effort should go to people and processes, but most organisations under-invest here | Explicit budget allocation: 70% people/process, 20% tech, 10% algorithms |
| **Trust and adoption at scale** | Only 14% of low-maturity organisations report business units trusting AI (vs 57% in high-maturity). 32% of employees expect workforce decreases | Invest in trust-enabling activities; transparent communication; upskilling as change imperative |
| **Change saturation** | 75% at or past saturation point; 1 in 3 sabotage rollouts | Sequence transformation waves; avoid "big bang"; celebrate wins; provide psychological safety |
| **Pilot purgatory at scale** | 90% of transformative use cases stuck in pilot (McKinsey) | Gating process, workflow redesign, executive ownership, CoE transition |
| **ERP disconnect** | Companies invest in AI at the expense of supporting enterprise resource planning systems needed for scaling | Parallel investment in core systems and AI capabilities |

### 3.4 Agentic Employee at Scale

**Workforce-level agentic capability.** At Scale, the three agentic employee profiles (supervisor, specialist, augmented frontline) are not exceptions — they are the norm across the organisation. Key indicators:

- All employees have role-appropriate AI fluency (not just early adopters)
- Skill-sharing is an organisational capability, not individual behaviour — formal skill libraries, peer training programmes, recognition systems
- Continuous learning is embedded in work, not bolted on as training events
- New roles have emerged: AI architects, deployment strategists, AI conversation designers (Salesforce redeployed hundreds and hired thousands in roles that didn't exist)

**The compounding effect.** The most powerful dynamic at Scale: one person's innovation benefits the entire organisation. When employees create and share reusable skills, the network effect compounds. This separates high-performing organisations from the rest.

### 3.5 Metrics at Scale

**Enterprise KPIs (four categories):**

| Category | Metrics |
|----------|---------|
| **Financial** | Cost savings, revenue impact, ROAI, P&L contribution by AI, % of EBIT attributable to AI |
| **Operational** | Process efficiency, error reduction, throughput, cycle time reduction |
| **Adoption** | Tool usage rates, employee sentiment, % of workflows redesigned, % of workforce at target fluency |
| **Strategic** | CSAT/NPS, decision quality, innovation velocity, competitive positioning |

**Key benchmarks:**
- Companies investing in trust-enabling activities are **2x more likely to see 10%+ revenue growth** (McKinsey)
- Only 6% of organisations qualify as "AI high performers" (>5% of EBIT from AI) (McKinsey, 2025)
- 45% of high-maturity organisations keep AI projects operational 3+ years vs only 20% of low-maturity (Gartner)
- 21% of gen AI users report having fundamentally redesigned workflows — and this is the single most impactful attribute for EBIT impact (McKinsey, 2025)

**Board and ExCo reporting at Scale.** McKinsey recommends boards demand specific metrics: ROI by business unit, % of processes AI-enabled, workforce reskilling progress, regulatory alignment, override rates, and innovation pipeline health.

## 4. Phasing and Sequencing

### 4.1 Can Foundation and Activation Overlap?

Yes, with constraints. Certain Foundation elements are prerequisites for Activation:

**Must be in place before Activation begins:**
- Minimum viable governance (AI inventory, risk classification, basic policies)
- Data foundation sufficient for first pilot use cases (not full enterprise data readiness)
- Baseline AI literacy for pilot teams and their leadership
- Works council/social dialogue process initiated (in EU jurisdictions)
- EU AI Act Article 4 compliance (AI literacy obligations, effective since Feb 2025)

**Can proceed in parallel:**
- Enterprise-wide data platform build-out (continues through Activation and Scale)
- Broader workforce literacy programmes (expand from pilot teams to full workforce)
- Technology platform selection and architecture design

### 4.2 Typical Duration by Company Size and Sector

| Phase | Large enterprise (10,000+) | Mid-market (500–10,000) | PE-backed |
|-------|---------------------------|------------------------|-----------|
| **Foundation** | 4–6 months | 2–4 months | 4–8 weeks (faster decisions, less legacy) |
| **Activation** | 6–12 months | 3–6 months | 2–4 months (3–5 focused use cases) |
| **Scale** | 12–24 months | 6–12 months | 6–12 months |
| **Total** | 22–42 months | 11–22 months | 10–24 months |

Mid-market advantages: faster decision cycles, flatter organisations, less legacy tech debt. Budget guidance: 2–5% of annual revenue for year one; 93% should go to people and training, not just technology. PE-backed companies benefit from standardised playbooks — 70% of large PE funds now run dedicated AI CoEs.

### 4.3 Sequence by Four Tiers

| Phase | Primary tier alignment | Rationale |
|-------|----------------------|-----------|
| **Foundation** | Individual (Tier 1) | Personal AI fluency, governance awareness, data literacy. "Have you asked the AI?" becomes the norm. |
| **Activation** | Team → Department (Tier 2–3) | Pilots at team level, first departmental workflows. CoE coordinates across teams. Superusers and champions emerge. |
| **Scale** | Department → Organisation (Tier 3–4) | Cross-functional transformation, enterprise North Star, agentic networks, operating model redesign. |

This is not a rigid mapping — some organisations may run Tier 2 activities during Foundation (e.g. team-level experiments) or Tier 4 activities during Activation (e.g. if a cross-functional workflow is the chosen pilot). The alignment is directional, not prescriptive.

### 4.4 European Context: Regulatory Deadlines as Forcing Function

| Date | Regulatory milestone | Implication for phasing |
|------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Feb 2, 2025** | EU AI Act: prohibited AI practices + AI literacy obligations active | Foundation must include literacy compliance from day one |
| **Aug 2, 2025** | GPAI transparency requirements | Foundation must include model documentation and transparency |
| **Aug 2, 2026** | Full high-risk AI system requirements | Activation pilots involving high-risk AI must be compliant by this date |
| **Potential Dec 2027** | Extended deadline via Digital Omnibus | May provide additional runway, but do not count on it |

**Labour and social dialogue.** European phasing must factor in works council consultation periods (typically 2–4 months), collective bargaining implications of AI-driven role changes, and social dialogue requirements that are legally mandated in most EU member states. Companies that treat this as a feature (building trust and buy-in) rather than a cost (delaying timelines) achieve stronger workforce adoption.

### 4.5 When to Declare Phase Completion

**"Foundation Complete" indicators:**
- AI governance framework approved and operational (policies, risk classification, ownership)
- Data foundation sufficient for priority use cases (not perfect, but functional)
- Baseline AI literacy achieved for pilot teams and leadership (measured, not assumed)
- EU AI Act compliance obligations met (Article 4 literacy, prohibited practices audit)
- Works council/social dialogue initiated and aligned
- CoE established with charter, executive sponsor, and initial team

**"Activation Complete" indicators:**
- At least 2–3 pilots have moved to production with measurable P&L impact
- CoE operating at hub-and-spoke level (not still fully centralised)
- Blended human-agent teams operational in at least one department
- Governance scaled beyond pilot to cover production use cases
- Internal narrative established: first wins documented and communicated
- Board receiving regular AI metrics

**"Scale Ready" indicators:**
- Cross-functional workflows in production (not just departmental)
- Operating model redesign underway (roles, structures, accountability)
- Workforce-level AI fluency programme in execution
- Enterprise KPIs defined and tracked (financial, operational, adoption, strategic)
- Governance mature enough for increased agent autonomy

### 5.3 PE and Mid-Market Variations

| Element | Enterprise | PE / Mid-market |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Foundation** | 4–6 months, comprehensive | 4–8 weeks, focused on minimum viable governance and data for priority use cases |
| **CoE** | Dedicated team of 10–30+ | Lean team of 3–5, augmented by external advisors; may sit at PE firm level serving portfolio |
| **Pilots** | 5–10 parallel pilots | 3–5 focused use cases; faster decision cycles |

## 6. Key Frameworks and Checklists (New Material)

### 6.1 The 10/20/70 Resource Allocation Rule (BCG)

- **10%** of effort → machine learning models and algorithms
- **20%** of effort → data and technology backbone
- **70%** of effort → people and processes

Most organisations invert this. The playbook should make this allocation explicit at every phase.

### 6.2 McKinsey's Five-Step Change Management Framework

1. Craft a **North Star** based on outcomes (not tools)
2. Build **trust** with governance (companies investing in trust-enabling activities are 2x more likely to see 10%+ revenue growth)
3. **Reimagine workflows** toward AI teams (not bolt-on automation)
4. Identify and activate **superusers** (5–10% of workforce who become internal champions)
5. Build a **continual learning** ecosystem (not one-off training events)

### 6.3 BCG's Deploy-Reshape-Invent (DRI) Value Framework

| Level | Description | Typical impact |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **Deploy** | Use off-the-shelf AI tools | 10–15% productivity boost |
| **Reshape** | Redesign functions end-to-end with AI workflows | Significant operational transformation |
| **Invent** | Create new business models and revenue streams | Highest value but highest complexity |

AI-mature companies generate 72% of their AI value in core functions (operations, marketing, sales), not support functions. Most companies are stuck at "Deploy."

### 6.4 Pilot-to-Production Gating Checklist

Before moving from pilot to production, confirm:

- [ ] Business case validated with actual (not projected) pilot data
- [ ] P&L impact quantified and approved by finance
- [ ] Scaling cost estimated (remember: 250–400% underestimation is typical)
- [ ] Data pipeline production-ready (not prototype)
- [ ] Governance and compliance verified (EU AI Act risk classification, GDPR, sector rules)
- [ ] Human-in-the-loop design validated for risk level
- [ ] Change management plan for affected teams
- [ ] Template created for replication to other contexts
- [ ] CoE capacity confirmed for production support
- [ ] Monitoring and circuit breakers in place

## 7. Conflicts and Gaps with Existing Research

### 7.1 Potential Conflicts

- **Phase naming**: The book uses Foundation → Activation → Scale. External sources use varied terminology (Assess → Win → Build → Optimise; Foundation → Expansion → Optimisation → Innovation). The writer should be explicit that this is the book's proprietary phasing model, drawing on but not copying external frameworks.
- **CoE models**: Some sources recommend starting decentralised from the outset (especially for mature digital organisations). The book's recommendation to start centralised may not fit every context — worth a caveat for digitally mature organisations.
- **Duration estimates**: Sources vary widely. The writer should present ranges, not point estimates, and note that sector and company size drive enormous variation.

### 7.2 Gaps Requiring Further Research

- **Direct CEO interviews**: Original material from European CEOs who led AI transformations (BBVA, Siemens, ING, Allianz) would add unique value.
- **Works council case studies**: Specific examples of how European companies navigated works council consultation during AI transformation — limited in public research.
- **Sector-specific playbooks**: Banking, insurance, and manufacturing have distinct phasing needs. Research is general; sector-specific guidance would strengthen the playbook.
- **Agentic employee assessment tools**: How to formally assess and track agentic employee development at each phase — emerging vendor solutions exist (UMU, ETS, Anthropic's framework) but enterprise case studies are thin.
- **ING's Foundation-to-Scale journey**: ING's 90% pilot-to-production rate is exceptional; detailed research on *how* they achieved this (governance, CoE structure, culture) would be extremely valuable for the playbook.
