# Chapter 12 Research: Readiness — Diagnostics, Tools, and Prioritisation

**Chapter:** 12 — Readiness
**Part:** IV — The CEO's Playbook
**Target length:** ~1,800 words (~4% of book)
**Core argument:** Readiness is not a one-time audit but a disciplined way to prioritise: what to fix first, which gaps block progress, and how to sequence investments so the organisation can absorb and act on AI rather than deploy it in isolation.

## 1. Readiness Diagnostics and Tools

### 1.1 Major Assessment Frameworks

Several diagnostic frameworks have emerged from consultancies, technology companies, and research institutions. They share common dimensions but differ in depth, format, and audience.

**Cisco AI Readiness Index (2024–2025)**
- Six pillars: Strategy, Infrastructure, Data, Governance, Talent, Culture.
- Survey of 8,000+ AI leaders across 30 markets and 26 industries.
- Scores 0–100 across 49 distinct indicators.
- Categories: Pacesetters (86+), Chasers (61–85), Followers (31–60), Laggards (0–30).
- Only 13–14% of organisations qualify as Pacesetters.
- 2024 edition: Western Europe scores 69.56 (2nd globally after North America at 82.60). France leads at 79.36, followed by UK (78.88), Netherlands (77.23), Germany (76.90), Finland (76.48).
- Source: Cisco AI Readiness Index 2025, cisco.com

**Gartner AI Maturity Model & Roadmap Toolkit**
- Assesses seven areas: strategy, product, governance, engineering, data, operating models, culture.
- Five maturity levels: Awareness → Active → Operational → Systemic → Transformational.
- Provides gap analysis between current and target states with customised roadmap recommendations.
- Widely used in board-level conversations to benchmark against peers.
- Source: Gartner, gartner.com

**BCG AI Radar (annual, 2025: 1,803 executives; 2026: 2,360 executives)**
- Three CEO archetypes: Trailblazers (~15%), Pragmatists (~70%), Followers (~15%).
- Trailblazers spend 8+ hours weekly on personal AI upskilling; allocate 60% of AI budgets to workforce upskilling reaching 65% of workforce.
- 75% of executives rank AI as top-three priority, but only 25% report significant value.
- 60% of companies fail to define or monitor any financial KPIs for AI value creation.
- Trailblazers invest >50% of AI budget in agentic AI; 2x more likely to deploy agents end-to-end.
- Source: BCG AI Radar 2025 & 2026, bcg.com

**Deloitte State of AI in the Enterprise (2026 edition)**
- 42% of companies believe their strategy is highly prepared for AI, but feel less prepared operationally on infrastructure, data, risk, and talent.
- Only 21% have a mature governance model for autonomous AI agents, despite 74% planning agentic AI deployment within two years.
- Worker access to AI tools expanded 50% in 2025 (from <40% to ~60% of workforce).
- Only 25% of companies have moved 40%+ of pilots into production; 54% expect to reach this in 3–6 months.
- 34% using AI to "deeply transform" business; 30% redesigning key processes; 37% using AI at surface level only.
- Source: Deloitte State of AI in the Enterprise 2026, deloitte.com

**ServiceNow Enterprise AI Maturity Index (2025)**
- Survey of ~4,500 executives across 16 countries.
- Five pillars: AI strategy & leadership, data governance, talent & skills, workflows, agentic AI use.
- Average maturity scores declined 9 points year-over-year (20% drop vs 2024).
- Fewer than 1% scored above 50 on a 100-point scale.
- If all Forbes Global 2000 achieved Pacesetter maturity: estimated $113 billion in aggregate gross margin increase.
- Source: ServiceNow Enterprise AI Maturity Index 2025, servicenow.com

**MIT CISR Enterprise AI Maturity Model (2024–2025)**
- Four-stage model based on data from 721 companies and 9 case studies.
- Companies in stages 1–2 have financial performance below industry average.
- Companies in stages 3–4 have financial performance well above industry average.
- Greatest financial impact occurs at the stage 2 → stage 3 transition (from "building pilots and capabilities" to "scaled AI ways of working").
- New 2025 survey (N=152) found enterprises making significant progress.
- Source: MIT CISR, cisr.mit.edu (Weill, Woerner, Sebastian, Kaganer)

**Umbrex AI Transformation Readiness Diagnostic**
- Ten domains: Strategy & Value, Leadership & Governance, Data, Technology & Architecture, Talent & Skills, Culture & Change, Ethics/Risk/Compliance, Operating Model & Processes, Financials & Investment, Ecosystem & Partnerships.
- Emphasises "value-anchored" assessment grounded in evidence rather than checklists.
- Designed for repeatable scoring across business units.
- Source: Umbrex, umbrex.com

### 1.2 Assessment Formats and Duration

- **Self-assessment questionnaires:** Fastest (hours to days); typically scorecard-based; risk of optimism bias. Examples: Cisco online tool, Pendoah AI Readiness Scorecard.
- **Facilitated workshops:** 1–3 days; cross-functional leadership team; structured discussion with scoring. Produces heat maps and gap lists.
- **Full audit / external diagnostic:** 4–12 weeks; consultancy-led with interviews, data analysis, and benchmarking against industry peers. Output: detailed report with prioritised roadmap.
- **Continuous monitoring:** Embedded dashboards and periodic re-assessment (quarterly or semi-annually). Pacesetters are 3x more likely to track and measure AI investment impact (95% vs 32% of organisations overall — Cisco).

### 1.3 Outputs: Scorecards, Heat Maps, and Gap Lists

Effective diagnostics produce outputs the CEO and board can act on without being overwhelmed:

- **Maturity scorecards:** Pillar-by-pillar scores on a 1–5 or 0–100 scale. Allow quick identification of strongest and weakest dimensions.
- **Heat maps:** Visual representation of readiness across business units, functions, or geographies. Immediately shows where the organisation is "red" (critical gaps) vs "green" (ready).
- **Gap lists with prioritisation:** Ranked list of gaps by impact and urgency. Best practice: map gaps to dependencies so leaders know what unblocks what.
- **Radar/spider charts:** Overlay current state vs target state across dimensions. Useful in board presentations to show distance-to-goal.

### 1.4 Benchmarks and Interpretation

- **Cisco scoring:** Below 60 = Follower; 61–85 = Chaser; 86+ = Pacesetter. Only 13–14% reach Pacesetter status globally.
- **ServiceNow:** Average scores declined 9 points YoY; fewer than 1% above 50/100. Maturity is getting harder as AI complexity grows.
- **Data readiness benchmark:** Gartner finds 57% of organisations estimate their data is not AI-ready. WEF: fewer than 1 in 5 organisations have high maturity in any aspect of data readiness. Practical implication: if a company scores below ~40% on data readiness, it should fix data before scaling AI.
- **Governance benchmark:** Only 18% of European organisations report being highly prepared in gen AI risk and governance areas (Deloitte). Only 21% have mature governance for agentic AI (Deloitte 2026). Only 28% have CEO oversight of AI governance; only 17% have board-level oversight (McKinsey).

## 2. Prioritisation Before Scaling

### 2.1 From Gaps to Action: "What to Fix First"

The diagnostic reveals gaps; the CEO's question is "what do we fix first?" Three prioritisation methods dominate:

**Dependency ordering (critical path)**
Some readiness dimensions are prerequisites for others. The general sequence:
1. **Data** before models (without clean, accessible data, AI initiatives fail regardless of talent or strategy).
2. **Governance** before scale (without oversight, risk compounds as AI expands).
3. **Talent/skills** alongside technology (hiring and upskilling take time; start early).
4. **Strategy alignment** before broad investment (ensures AI efforts tie to business value).

McKinsey's strongest finding: out of 25 organisational attributes tested, **redesigning workflows** has the biggest effect on capturing EBIT value from gen AI — yet only 21% of organisations have done this. CEO oversight of AI governance shows the strongest correlation with bottom-line EBIT impact.

**The "Now, Next, Later" framework (Trigent)**
- **Now:** High-impact, well-scoped use cases delivering immediate measurable value without wholesale organisational overhaul.
- **Next:** Strategic bets on emerging capabilities (e.g., agentic AI workflows).
- **Later:** Experimental initiatives to monitor but deprioritise until readiness catches up.

**Value/Capability/Trust prioritisation (from Ch 8)**
Each readiness gap can be assessed through the three lenses:
- **Value:** Does fixing this gap directly unlock business outcomes (revenue, cost, customer satisfaction)?
- **Capability:** Is the organisation able to fix this gap with current resources and skills?
- **Trust:** Does the gap create compliance, ethical, or reputational risk if left unfixed?

Gaps that score high on value and trust but have feasible capability requirements should be fixed first. Gaps that require major capability building (e.g., hiring an entire data engineering team) require longer lead time and should be started early even if value payoff is later.

### 2.2 Avoiding "Readiness as Delay"

A critical warning: readiness diagnostics can become an excuse not to start. Practical guardrails:

- **Lightweight diagnostics first:** A CEO-led self-assessment (1–2 days) is enough to identify the top 3–5 blockers. Full audits should run in parallel with early AI initiatives, not instead of them.
- **"Good enough" thresholds:** Not every dimension needs to be perfect before starting. Data quality of 70% may be sufficient for a first use case. The goal is "ready enough to act" not "perfect across every dimension."
- **Time-boxed assessment:** Cap diagnostic duration at 4–6 weeks for a comprehensive external assessment. Anything longer becomes organisational procrastination.
- **Start where you are strong:** If talent is the strongest pillar but data is weakest, launch initiatives that are talent-intensive but data-light while fixing data infrastructure in parallel.

BCG data shows Trailblazer CEOs do not wait for perfect readiness — they invest in readiness and deployment simultaneously: 94% continue AI investment regardless of immediate returns.

### 2.3 Connection to the Ch 11 Playbook: Foundation → Activation → Scale

Readiness diagnostics serve as decision gates between playbook phases (Ch 11):

- **Foundation → Activation gate:** The diagnostic should confirm: (a) data infrastructure is accessible for first use cases, (b) governance guardrails are in place for pilots, (c) at least one executive sponsor and one domain leader are assigned, (d) one or two use cases have clear value metrics defined.
- **Activation → Scale gate:** The diagnostic should confirm: (a) pilot results demonstrate measurable value, (b) the operating model (CoE or hub-and-spoke) is functioning, (c) workforce upskilling has reached critical mass (>30% of target roles), (d) governance scales to multiple concurrent AI initiatives, (e) data quality and technology infrastructure support enterprise-level demands.

## 3. Evidence: Readiness Diagnostics Improve Outcomes

### 3.1 Pacesetter Performance Advantages

The evidence is clear that organisations with higher assessed readiness achieve materially better outcomes:

- **Cisco Pacesetters:** 4x more likely to have finalised AI use cases (77% vs ~19%). 3x more likely to track AI investment impact (95% vs 32%). 1.5x higher gains in profitability, productivity, and innovation (90%+ vs ~60%).
- **Cisco Pacesetters (specific metrics):** 99% have well-defined AI strategy (vs 58% overall). 76% have fully centralised data (vs 19%). 84% have end-to-end encryption with continuous monitoring (vs 30%).
- **MIT CISR:** Companies in maturity stages 3–4 (scaled AI) perform above industry average financially; stages 1–2 perform below average. The stage 2→3 transition (pilots to scaled ways of working) is the greatest value inflection point.
- **ServiceNow:** If all Forbes Global 2000 achieved Pacesetter maturity: $113 billion aggregate gross margin increase.
- **Gartner:** 45% of high-maturity organisations keep AI projects operational 3+ years vs 20% of low-maturity organisations. In 57% of high-maturity organisations, business units trust and are ready to use AI vs only 14% in low-maturity organisations.

### 3.2 The Cost of Skipping Readiness

- 95% of enterprise AI implementations fail to deliver sustained impact (MIT NANDA Report 2025).
- S&P Global: 42% of companies scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the year before.
- BCG: 60% of companies achieve no material value from AI. Most spread investments broadly across pilots → lower ROI.
- ServiceNow: Average maturity scores declined 9 points YoY — AI is getting harder, not easier, as complexity grows.

## 4. European Context

### 4.1 EU AI Act as a Readiness Dimension

For European companies, regulatory readiness is not optional — it is a core dimension of any assessment. The EU AI Act creates a five-phase compliance readiness framework:

1. **AI System Inventory:** Document all AI systems including purpose, functionality, data types, user populations.
2. **Risk Classification:** Classify using the Act's four tiers (prohibited, high-risk, limited, minimal).
3. **Gap Analysis:** Compare current state against requirements for quality management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, human oversight, testing.
4. **Remediation Planning:** Develop compliance roadmaps for each system.
5. **Evidence Pack Assembly:** Compile documentation proving conformity.

**Key deadlines:**
- February 2025: Prohibited AI practices enforceable.
- August 2025: General-purpose AI obligations in effect.
- August 2026: High-risk AI system requirements deadline.

**Penalties:** Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practices; €15 million or 3% for high-risk violations.

**European readiness gaps on compliance:**
- Only 18% of European organisations report being highly prepared in gen AI risk and governance (Deloitte).
- Only 36% of German organisations report preparedness for EU AI Act implementation (Deloitte).

### 4.2 European Companies' Most Common Readiness Gaps

Drawing from multiple surveys (Cisco, Deloitte, Lleverage, BCG), the most common gaps for European companies are:

1. **Data governance and quality:** Consistently the largest gap. Gartner: 57% of organisations globally estimate data is not AI-ready. WEF: fewer than 1 in 5 have high data maturity.
2. **Governance and risk management:** Only 18% highly prepared on gen AI risk/governance in Europe (Deloitte). Only 21% have mature governance for agentic AI.
3. **Talent and skills:** Fewer than 1 in 5 smaller organisations have required AI talent (global survey). Only 6% of companies have begun upskilling in a "meaningful way" (BCG/McKinsey).
4. **Pilot-to-production conversion:** 74% of European SaaS companies have initiated AI projects, but only 14% have reached full production with measurable revenue or cost savings (Lleverage, 150+ European tech leaders).
5. **CEO and board engagement:** Only 28% have CEO oversight of AI governance; only 17% have board-level oversight (McKinsey). 66% of boards report limited to no AI knowledge.

### 4.3 Turning Compliance into Competitive Advantage

European readiness assessments should frame EU AI Act compliance not as a burden but as a trust accelerator (connecting to Ch 9 — Europe's Distinctive Edge):

- Allianz signed the EU AI Pact in November 2024 — proactive compliance as competitive moat.
- European insurers lead in responsible AI talent: 47% of specialists in data ethics, governance, and risk (despite representing 36% of total AI talent).
- Accenture: companies aligning AI, platforms, and business strategies achieve 2.2x revenue growth and 37% EBITDA lift.
- Regulatory clarity reduces uncertainty: Deloitte found European organisations' gen AI preparedness increased as regulatory clarity improved.

## 5. Tools, Templates, and Practical Outputs for the CEO

### 5.1 Lightweight CEO-Level Diagnostic

A practical, one-page diagnostic the CEO can use (or delegate to the ExCo) should cover:

| Dimension | Key question | Score (1–5) |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Strategy | Do we have a documented AI strategy tied to business objectives? | |
| Data | Is our data accessible, governed, and quality-controlled for AI use cases? | |
| Technology | Can our technology stack support AI workloads at scale? | |
| Talent | Do we have the skills (or plan to build/hire them) for our AI ambitions? | |
| Governance | Do we have oversight, risk management, and compliance processes for AI? | |
| Culture | Is the organisation willing and able to adopt new ways of working? | |
| Value | Are our AI initiatives tied to measurable business outcomes? | |

**Interpretation:**
- Average score ≤2: Fix fundamentals before scaling. Focus on the lowest-scoring dimension first.
- Average score 2.1–3.5: Foundation phase — ready for targeted pilots, but close key gaps in parallel.
- Average score 3.6–4.5: Activation phase — scale what works, invest in remaining gaps.
- Average score >4.5: Scale phase — focus on cross-functional integration and continuous improvement.

### 5.2 The "Three Questions" Prioritisation

For CEOs who want a decision tool, not a full assessment:

1. **Where are we?** (Current maturity: which dimensions are strongest and weakest?)
2. **What blocks us?** (Which gaps are dependencies for other progress?)
3. **What do we fix first?** (Prioritise by: value unlocked × feasibility ÷ risk if delayed.)

### 5.3 Readiness Checkpoints Aligned to Playbook Phases

| Phase transition | Must-have readiness | Nice-to-have readiness |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Foundation → Activation | Data accessible for first use cases; governance guardrails for pilots; executive sponsor assigned; 1–2 use cases with value metrics | CoE or AI team formed; training programme launched; technology platform selected |
| Activation → Scale | Pilot results show measurable value; operating model (CoE/hub-and-spoke) functioning; >30% target roles upskilled; governance scales to multiple initiatives | Enterprise data platform operational; cross-functional playbooks in place; board-level AI reporting established |

## 8. Writing Notes for Chapter Author

- **Keep it short.** Target ~1,800 words. This is a practical, tools-focused chapter — not a repeat of Ch 7 (Foundations) or Ch 8 (Value, Capabilities, Trust).
- **Lead with the CEO question:** "Where are we, what do we fix first, and how do we prioritise before scaling?"
- **Anchor to the playbook (Ch 11):** Readiness diagnostics are the decision gates between Foundation → Activation → Scale. Make this connection explicit.
- **Use the lightweight diagnostic table** (Section 5.1) or a simplified version as a practical takeaway for the reader.
- **Warn against readiness as delay:** A short sidebar or paragraph on why readiness is a speed tool, not a brake.
- **European angle:** Frame EU AI Act compliance as a readiness dimension and trust accelerator, not a compliance burden (connects to Ch 9).
- **Connect to Value/Capability/Trust (Ch 8):** Show how the three lenses can be used to prioritise readiness gaps.
- **Data points to feature:** Cisco Pacesetters (13–14%, 4x more likely to finalise use cases); MIT CISR stage 2→3 inflection; McKinsey's workflow redesign finding; ServiceNow's $113B Pacesetter value estimate.
