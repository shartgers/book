# Research Brief — Chapter 06 — The Operational Layer

**Chapter purpose:** The Operating Model layer of the AI Transformation Framework: people, process, roles, skills, and org design. How to structure teams and roles for agentic work; from task-doer to orchestrator at scale. Why this layer is often the bottleneck when organisations pilot well but fail to scale.
**Word budget:** 6% of book (~2,700 words at 45k)
**Key audiences:** European CEOs of mid-to-large organisations; sceptical, data-driven, time-poor.

---

## 1. Roles and Skills for Agentic Work

Evidence supporting the chapter's argument that the Operating Model layer requires new roles, new skills, and a fundamental shift from task-doer to orchestrator.

---

### 1.1 The Agent Orchestrator — a new middle-management role

**Claim:** A new role — the Agent Orchestrator — is emerging as the defining middle-management function of the agentic era, managing synthetic AI talent rather than people.
**Source:** Arion Research LLC, "The 'Agent Orchestrator': The New Middle Manager Role of 2026," 2026.
**Key details:** The Agent Orchestrator handles agent provisioning (API connections, data access, permissions), monitoring, and coordination — analogous to onboarding and managing human employees, but compressed and more technical. The role is necessary because organisations are moving from "Software as a Service" to "Service as a Software," where intelligent agents actively execute workflows rather than just supporting them.
**Use for:** Section on new roles; connect to Ch 3 (supervisor profile) and Ch 4 (team/department tiers).

---

### 1.2 Agent Orchestration as "mission-critical capability" (BCG)

**Claim:** Agent orchestration is the "central nervous system" of agent-first organisations — a coordination layer managing multiple agents collaborating on complex tasks.
**Source:** BCG, "Orchestration: The New Mission Critical Capability in an Agentic Future," 2025–2026; published via BCGonTech / Medium.
**Key details:** Orchestration differs from Model Context Protocol (MCP); it is the coordination layer that enables accuracy, adaptability, repeatability, and scalability across multi-agent systems. BCG positions this as the capability that separates organisations that deploy agents from those that scale them.
**Use for:** Core argument that orchestration is an operating model capability, not a technology feature.

---

### 1.3 AI Product Manager — a new dedicated role

**Claim:** Organisations are creating dedicated AI Product Manager roles to own the roadmap for AI agent capabilities and govern agent lifecycle.
**Source:** BuiltIn job listings (2025–2026); Redreamality, "The Orchestrator's Era: The 2026 State of AI Agents in Product Management," 2026.
**Key details:** The AI Product Manager defines how builders create, govern, and scale AI agents. Responsibilities include designing multi-step reasoning workflows, managing agent governance and compliance frameworks, establishing performance metrics, and working directly with enterprise customers on use cases. Requires 3+ years of product management experience with deep AI/ML familiarity. By 2026, 85% of companies expect to customise autonomous agents for specific business needs, but only 1 in 5 have mature governance models — creating demand for this role.
**Use for:** Concrete role definition for the chapter; connect to Ch 8 (capabilities assessment).

---

### 1.4 The domain leader (N-2/N-3) — "the single most critical role"

**Claim:** Domain leaders — executives two to three levels below the CEO — are the single most critical role for AI transformation, yet most lack the skills to fill it.
**Source:** McKinsey, "Building the AI Muscle of Your Business Leaders," 2025–2026.
**Key details:** Domain leaders own end-to-end business processes or value streams and translate organisational vision into real change and value. They need traditional executive capabilities (customer needs, strategy, org design, operational performance) plus a "second muscle": being tech-savvy enough to develop AI-enabled transformation roadmaps, understand modern software delivery, and assess data infrastructure health. Currently, only **17% of Fortune 500 senior leaders' skills are technical**, and just **5% have held technical roles** — creating a massive capability gap at the exact level where strategy becomes execution.
**Quote (McKinsey):** The domain leader is "probably the single most critical role any business needs for its AI transformations."
**Use for:** Central argument — the Operating Model layer fails when domain leaders lack AI fluency; connect to Ch 8 (Capabilities) and Ch 12 (Readiness).

---

### 1.5 Skills shift — from producing to orchestrating

**Claim:** Over 70% of employer-sought skills will endure but be applied differently in partnership with AI — the shift is from producing first drafts to framing questions, validating outputs, and applying judgment.
**Source:** McKinsey, "A New Year's Resolution for Leaders: Redesign Work for People and AI," January 2026; McKinsey Global Institute, "A New Future of Work," 2025.
**Key details:** AI fluency demand has grown **nearly sevenfold in two years**, spreading beyond technical roles into management, finance, healthcare, and frontline services. Key complementary skills: critical thinking, adaptability, quality assurance, coaching, and decision-making. Only **6% of companies** have begun upskilling in a "meaningful way" despite 89% acknowledging the need.
**Use for:** Skills framework section; connect to Ch 3 (agentic employee mindset shift).

---

### 1.6 The Europe-specific reskilling challenge

**Claim:** Europe could require up to 12 million occupational transitions by 2030 — double the pre-pandemic pace — as demand shifts toward STEM, healthcare, and high-skill professions.
**Source:** McKinsey Global Institute, "A New Future of Work: The Race to Deploy AI and Raise Skills in Europe and Beyond," 2025.
**Key details:** Up to 30% of current work hours could be automated by 2030. Faster technology adoption with proactive worker redeployment could help Europe achieve **2–3% annual productivity growth** through 2030, compared to just **0.3% with slow adoption**. Companies are prioritising workforce retraining over hiring or subcontracting to meet skill needs. Executives report critical shortages in both technical and human skills (critical thinking, creativity, teaching, training).
**Use for:** European context; connect to Ch 9 (Europe's Distinctive Edge) and Ch 11 (Building playbook).

---

### 1.7 Reusable skills — the compounding effect

**Claim:** The most valuable agentic employees are those who create reusable AI skills (modular packages of procedural expertise) and share them with their teams — creating compounding organisational value.
**Source:** Conor Armstrong, "Stop Building Agents. Start Building Skills," Medium, February 2026; Microsoft, "Agent Skills" documentation, 2026; Glean, "Skills Launch," 2026.
**Key details:** A fundamental shift is emerging: organisations are moving from building separate agents for each use case to creating reusable skills — modular packages containing instructions, scripts, and resources that give agents specialised capabilities. Skills follow an open specification (SKILL.md with YAML frontmatter, optional scripts and references). Benefits: interoperability across products, consistency through repeatable workflows, and packaged expertise that captures domain knowledge as reusable components. The analogy: you'd rather hire an experienced tax professional than a mathematical genius to do your taxes — agents need domain expertise, not just intelligence.
**Use for:** Skills section; connect to Ch 3 (agentic employee creates and shares) and Ch 4 (compounding across tiers).

## 2. Process Redesign for Agentic Workflows

Evidence that process redesign — not technology deployment — is where AI value is created or lost.

---

### 2.1 The agent-centric enterprise — 2–10x productivity through radical workflow redesign

**Claim:** Organisations pursuing agentic AI must redesign entire workflows with AI agents as primary actors, not auxiliary tools. Layering AI onto human-centric workflows captures only marginal benefits.
**Source:** Ulla Kruhse-Lehtonen and Dirk Hofmann, "The Agent-Centric Enterprise: Why 2–10x Productivity Gains Demand Radical Workflow Redesign," Harvard Data Science Review, Issue 8.1, Winter 2026.
**Key details:** Despite 78% of companies claiming AI adoption, 80% report no measurable impact on earnings. The fundamental issue: AI is being layered onto workflows designed for humans, not machines. The authors introduce the Agent OS framework and A.G.E.N.T. playbook. Real-world examples include a global industrial firm that cut audit reporting time by **92%** and a B2B sales team that scaled strategic insight generation.
**Use for:** Opening argument — process redesign is the operating model imperative.

---

### 2.2 BCG: "Scaling AI Requires New Processes, Not Just New Tools"

**Claim:** Successfully scaling AI agents demands end-to-end process redesign around agents as default executors, not automation of discrete steps within existing workflows.
**Source:** BCG, "Scaling AI Requires New Processes, Not Just New Tools," 2026.
**Key details:** Three critical decisions for organisations deploying agents at scale: (1) Build vs Buy — balancing cost, speed, and customisation; (2) Platform vs Product — single agents or multi-agent networks, centralised or decentralised infrastructure; (3) Managing Change — maintaining openness to redesigning optimal end-to-end processes. An industrial goods company redesigned its quote-to-order process using four coordinated AI agents, achieving **30–40% labour cost reductions** while generating **tens of millions in additional revenue** through improved turnaround times.
**Use for:** Process redesign framework; concrete case study data.

---

### 2.3 Zero-based, outcome-driven process design

**Claim:** Organisations must adopt "zero-based, outcome-driven processes" — redesigning from scratch with AI at the core rather than automating legacy workflows.
**Source:** BCG, "Agents Accelerate the Next Wave of AI Value Creation," 2025; Bain & Company (research folder).
**Key details:** BCG emphasises this as a critical "now" action for CEOs. Agents should assume different roles: some executing purely transactional work autonomously, others supporting human teams on complex tasks. Performance gains: **30–50% business process acceleration**, **25–40% reduction in low-value work time**, **40–60% cuts in engineering and design lead times**.
**BCG's framing:** Embed business context and proprietary intelligence within shared AI platforms that provide "freedom within a frame."
**Use for:** Core process redesign concept; connects to Ch 5 (framework image — the "how" of the Operating Model layer).

---

### 2.4 Multi-agent orchestration patterns

**Claim:** Four foundational orchestration patterns are emerging for managing agent groups and swarms, each suited to different process contexts.
**Source:** Fast.io, "Multi-Agent Orchestration Patterns: Complete Guide 2026"; ShShell.com, "Building Agentic Control Planes," 2026.
**Key details:**

| Pattern | Description | Best for |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Hierarchical/Manager-Worker** | Orchestrator breaks goals into tasks, delegates to specialist workers | Complex end-to-end workflows |
| **Supervisor** | Central coordinator assigns tasks + separate supervisor evaluates quality | Enterprise deployments (mirrors human team structures) |
| **Pipeline** | Agents process work sequentially, each refining previous outputs | Linear processes (e.g., claims processing) |
| **Swarm** | Autonomous agents work in parallel with minimal coordination | High-volume, low-complexity tasks |

The supervisor pattern dominates enterprise deployments because it mirrors existing human team structures.
**Use for:** Practical framework connecting McKinsey's stand-alone agents → agent groups → agentic swarms progression; connect to Ch 4 (tiers).

## 3. Org Design for the Agentic Organisation

Evidence on how to structure the organisation — centralised, hub-and-spoke, embedded — and when to transition between models.

---

### 3.1 Three primary org design patterns

**Claim:** Three primary AI operating model structures are emerging, with the hybrid model increasingly favoured.
**Source:** Umbrex, "Design an AI Operating Model That Scales Responsibly," 2025–2026; Architecture & Governance Magazine, "Hub & Spoke: The Operating System for AI-Enabled Enterprise Architecture," 2025.

| Model | When to use | Strength | Risk |
|-------|-------------|----------|------|
| **Centralised** | Low AI maturity, scarce talent, high regulatory exposure | Standardisation, learning speed | Central team becomes bottleneck |
| **Federated (Hub-and-Spoke)** | Diverse business contexts, speed is critical | Ownership, business relevance | Fragmentation without guardrails |
| **Hybrid** | Mid-to-late maturity; combining standards with local delivery | Balance of control and agility | Complexity in governance |

The typical progression: centralise first (build standards, governance, talent pool), then decentralise delivery as maturity grows.
**Use for:** Org design section; connect to ING case study (centralised → positioning for federated).

---

### 3.2 The hybrid model in detail

**Claim:** Leading organisations adopt a hybrid approach: centralised strategy, standards, and governance combined with embedded product teams in business units.
**Source:** SeniorExecutive.com, "AI Is Now Strategy — Here's How Org Charts Must Change," 2026; Umbrex Chief AI Officer Playbook, 2025–2026.
**Key details — centralised elements:** Chief AI Officer reporting directly to CEO/COO with real decision authority; lean AI governance council managing strategy, investment, and risk; enterprise standards for security, monitoring, and regulatory compliance.
**Key details — embedded elements:** Product teams deployed within business units owning specific outcomes; local execution and workflow integration; domain-specific decision systems organised around repeatable themes (retention, risk triage, forecasting).
**Critical success factor:** Independent risk oversight must remain separate from development teams.
**Use for:** Practical org design guidance for CEOs.

---

### 3.3 Span of control — from managing people to orchestrating agents

**Claim:** Management span of control is fundamentally changing: one manager with a small human team could orchestrate hundreds of agents acting autonomously across workflows, running 24/7.
**Source:** McKinsey, "The Agentic Organization: Contours of the Next Paradigm for the AI Era," September 2025.
**Key details:** McKinsey envisions organisation charts pivoting toward "agentic networks" where traditional hierarchies give way to flexible configurations of human-agent teams. The human acts as "Director of Intent" — setting goals, defining boundaries, reviewing outputs — while an Orchestrator agent directs parallel execution swarms. This mirrors the Ch 3 concept of the "supervisor" profile.
**Use for:** Connecting org design to the people shift; links to Ch 3 and Ch 4.

---

### 3.4 IBM: 78% of C-suite say agentic AI requires a new operating model

**Claim:** The majority of C-suite executives recognise that achieving maximum benefit from agentic AI requires a fundamentally new operating model — not optimisation of the existing one.
**Source:** IBM Institute for Business Value, "Agentic AI's Strategic Ascent," 2026.
**Key details:** **78% of C-suite executives** say achieving maximum benefit from agentic AI requires a new operating model. By 2027, twice as many executives expect AI agents to make autonomous decisions. **86% expect process automation and workflow reinvention** to be more effective because of AI agents by 2027. The paradigm shifts from "tech runs tasks" to "tech runs operations and talent runs tech." Companies excelling in three key AI adoption areas are **32 times more likely** to achieve top-tier business performance.
**Use for:** CEO-level urgency on operating model change; opening or closing argument.

---

### 3.5 Deloitte: the Human-Agentic Workforce model

**Claim:** The transformation of work requires five key shifts to redesign roles, decision-making, and operating models for human-AI collaboration.
**Source:** Deloitte, "Work, Reworked: Leading with Agentic AI (Human-Agentic Workforce Model)," 2026.
**Key details:** Deloitte identifies five key shifts enabling organisations to redesign work for speed, scale, and impact. Three foundational changes are required: cultural readiness, technological infrastructure, and operational readiness. The framework emphasises that agentic AI is more than automation — it requires a complete reimagining of enterprise work systems. Only **21% of organisations** have rigorous governance mechanisms in place, even as deployment accelerates.
**Use for:** Framework reference for the full scope of operating model change.

---

### 3.6 AI Centre of Excellence progression model

**Claim:** Organisations typically progress through three CoE stages: centralised → hub-and-spoke → embedded, with the key success factor being measurable outcomes within 60–90 days.
**Source:** Research folder (book-research-claude.md, Section 9); Deloitte, "The Rise of the AI Operating Model," 2026.
**Key details:** Companies operationalising AI at scale see **up to 20% EBITDA uplift**, while those stuck in experimentation face fragmented governance and diminished value realisation. The CoE must align AI with business priorities, drive reuse and speed across functions, and embed governance by design.
**Use for:** Practical sequence for CEOs; connects to Ch 11 (Foundation → Activation → Scale).

## 4. Failure Modes — Why the Operating Model Is the Bottleneck

Evidence that the Operating Model layer — not technology — is where most transformations stall.

---

### 4.1 The 10/20/70 rule — most companies invest backwards

**Claim:** Successful AI transformation requires 70% of effort on people and processes, 20% on data/technology infrastructure, and only 10% on algorithms. Most organisations get this allocation backwards.
**Source:** BCG 10/20/70 rule (multiple publications, 2025–2026); Forbes, "Why AI's 10-20-70 Principle Should Matter To CEOs And Everyone Else," January 2026.
**Key details:** This is BCG's most cited AI transformation insight. The implication: when organisations invest primarily in technology (the 10% and 20%) and neglect the operating model (the 70%), they are structurally guaranteed to fail. The chapter should frame this as the core operating model message: the Operating Model layer (people, process, roles, skills, org design) is the 70%.
**Use for:** Central chapter argument; diagnostic for CEOs.

---

### 4.2 Pilot purgatory — the operating model gap

**Claim:** Nearly two-thirds of AI initiatives fail to advance beyond pilots, specifically because organisations redesign technology without redesigning workflows.
**Source:** Synvestable, "100 AI Transformations: Lessons Learned," 2025–2026; BCG, various.
**Key details:** Companies get stuck in "pilot purgatory" because they automate individual tasks rather than reimagining how work gets done. The micro-productivity trap (Bain) produces fragmented, siloed, shallow gains. The operating model is what bridges a successful pilot to scaled impact: who does the work, how the process runs, and how the organisation is structured must all change — not just the technology.
**Use for:** Core failure mode argument; connects to Ch 1 (95% failure rate) and Ch 4 (tiers — pilot ≠ scale).

---

### 4.3 Change management as a starting point, not an afterthought

**Claim:** Organisations that integrate change management from the planning stage — not as an afterthought — are significantly more likely to achieve lasting AI impact.
**Source:** BCG, "AI Fuels Field Service Transformation. Change Management Provides the Spark," 2026.
**Key details:** Winners integrate people and process considerations from planning stages. They use focused pilots with feedback loops and employee champions. They co-develop solutions with frontline teams from the start. The 10/20/70 rule's 70% — people and process — must begin at the beginning, not when technology is "ready."
**Use for:** Practical guidance; connects to Ch 11 (Activation phase).

---

### 4.4 Data readiness as operating model dependency

**Claim:** 63% of organisations lack adequate data management practices for AI; Gartner projects 60% of AI projects will be abandoned through 2026 due to poor data foundations.
**Source:** Synvestable compilation; Gartner, 2025.
**Key details:** Teams succeed when they spend at least **50% of project timeline on data preparation** before model development. This is an operating model issue, not just a technology issue: who owns data quality, how processes generate usable data, and how data flows across the organisation are operating model questions.
**Use for:** Connection to Ch 7 (Foundation layer) — frame data readiness as shared between Operating Model and Foundation layers.

Simultaneously, Siemens invests in Industrial Copilot (Hermes Award 2025 winner), Digital Native Factory (200% capacity increase), and the NVIDIA partnership for an "Industrial AI Operating System." ## 8. Frameworks and Models — Summary for Writer

### 8.1 The 10/20/70 Rule (BCG)
- 10% algorithms, 20% technology/data, 70% people and processes.
- Core message: the Operating Model layer IS the 70%.

### 8.2 Three Org Design Models
- Centralised → Hub-and-Spoke → Embedded (progression model).
- ING = centralised; BBVA = hub-and-spoke with embedded champions; mature orgs move toward hybrid.

### 8.3 Four Multi-Agent Orchestration Patterns
- Hierarchical/Manager-Worker, Supervisor, Pipeline, Swarm.
- Connects to McKinsey's stand-alone agents → agent groups → agentic swarms.

### 8.4 Three New Roles for the Agentic Operating Model
1. **Agent Orchestrator** — manages synthetic AI talent (new middle management).
2. **AI Product Manager** — owns agent roadmap, governance, lifecycle.
3. **Domain Leader (N-2/N-3)** — translates strategy into operational AI change (most critical).

### 8.5 Skills Shift Framework
- From task-doer to orchestrator (Ch 3 mindset table).
- From building agents to building reusable skills (compounding value).
- From technical upskilling to AI fluency across all roles.

### 8.6 Deloitte Human-Agentic Workforce Model
- Five key shifts for redesigning work for speed, scale, and impact.
- Three foundations: cultural readiness, technological infrastructure, operational readiness.

### 8.7 Agent OS Framework and A.G.E.N.T. Playbook (HDSR)
- Academic framework for radical workflow redesign around autonomous agents.
- Phased implementation roadmap.
